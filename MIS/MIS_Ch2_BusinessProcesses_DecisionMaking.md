## 1. 📖 INTRODUCTION

### Why Business Processes + IT Matter Together?

> Every organization — from a small kirana store to Tata Group — runs on **business processes**. These processes generate problems, and problems require **decisions**. Good decisions need **information**. Better IT → Better information → Better decisions → Better business outcomes.

```
Business Processes → Generate Problems
        ↓
Problems require → Decision Making
        ↓
Good decisions need → Quality Information
        ↓
Quality Information comes from → Effective IT Systems
```

### Key Linkage:
> **MIS (Management Information System)** exists at this intersection — it uses IT to support business processes and improve decision making.

## 2. 🔄 WHAT ARE BUSINESS PROCESSES?

### Definition:

> **Business Process** = A **collection of related, structured activities or tasks** performed by people or systems that produce a specific **output (product or service)** for a particular **customer or market**.

### Simpler Definition:
> A business process is **"a set of steps that a company follows to complete a task"** — from start to finish, consistently and repeatedly.

### Key Characteristics of Business Processes:

> **Mnemonic: "RIMS-C"**
> - **R**epeatable — Done consistently every time
> - **I**nterrelated — Steps are linked to each other
> - **M**easurable — Performance can be tracked (time, cost, quality)
> - **S**pecific Output — Produces a defined result
> - **C**ustomer-oriented — Designed to deliver value to a customer (internal or external)

---

### Components of a Business Process:

> **Mnemonic: "TIOP"**
> - **T**rigger/Input — What starts the process? (customer order, complaint, demand)
> - **I**nstructions/Steps — The specific activities performed
> - **O**utput — The result produced (product, service, decision)
> - **P**articipants/Resources — People, systems, equipment involved

| Component | Example (Order Fulfillment at Amazon) |
|-----------|--------------------------------------|
| **Trigger/Input** | Customer places an order online |
| **Activities** | Verify payment → Pick item from warehouse → Pack → Dispatch |
| **Output** | Item delivered to customer |
| **Participants** | Website system, warehouse staff, logistics partner |

---

### Types of Business Processes:

> **Mnemonic: "MOS"**
> - **M**anagement Processes
> - **O**perational Processes
> - **S**upporting Processes

#### 1. 🔵 Operational Processes (Core Processes)
- Directly create and deliver value to customers
- **Primary business activities**
- Examples:
  - Manufacturing (producing goods)
  - Order processing (taking and fulfilling orders)
  - Delivering services
  - Sales and marketing

#### 2. 🟡 Management Processes
- Govern and control operational processes
- Related to **planning, monitoring, controlling**
- Examples:
  - Corporate governance
  - Strategic planning
  - Budgeting
  - Performance management

#### 3. 🟢 Supporting Processes
- Support the primary processes — don't directly create customer value
- Examples:
  - HR management (recruiting, training)
  - Accounting and finance
  - IT support
  - Legal compliance

---

### Cross-Functional Business Processes:

> Most real-world business processes **cut across multiple departments** — they are cross-functional.

**Example — Order-to-Cash Process:**
```
Sales Dept        →    Finance Dept    →    Warehouse    →    Logistics
(Receive order)      (Process payment)    (Pick & Pack)    (Deliver)
```
> **IT systems (like ERP — Enterprise Resource Planning)** integrate these cross-functional processes so data flows seamlessly.

---

### Business Process vs Business Function:

| Feature | Business Function | Business Process |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Scope** | Within ONE department | Cuts ACROSS departments |
| **Focus** | Role/task | End-to-end output |
| **Example** | HR hiring | Order-to-delivery |
| **Orientation** | Activity | Customer value |

---

### Business Process Re-engineering (BPR):

> **BPR** = Fundamental rethinking and radical redesign of business processes to achieve dramatic improvements in cost, quality, speed, and service.

- Coined by **Michael Hammer and James Champy (1993)**
- NOT incremental improvement — it's **starting from scratch**
- IT is the key enabler of BPR

**Example:**
> Ford Motor Company reduced headcount in accounts payable from 500 to 125 (75% reduction) by redesigning the payment process using IT — instead of matching 3 documents (PO, receipt, invoice), the new system only needed 2 (PO and receipt). Invoice was eliminated!

## 3. 💻 EVOLUTION OF IT AND PROBLEM SOLVING

### The Evolution Journey:

> **Mnemonic: "DMSEK"** — *"Data Makes Systems Enable Knowledge"*
> - **D**ata Processing Era (1950s-60s)
> - **M**anagement Information Systems Era (1970s)
> - **S**trategic/Decision Support Era (1980s)
> - **E**nterprise/Internet Era (1990s-2000s)
> - **K**nowledge/AI Era (2010s-present)

---

### Stage 1: 🔵 Data Processing / Electronic Data Processing (EDP) Era — 1950s-60s

- **Focus:** Automate routine clerical tasks
- Computers used to process **large volumes of structured data** (payroll, billing, inventory)
- Replaced manual ledgers and calculators
- **Key Systems:** Transaction Processing Systems (TPS)
- **Problem Solving Role:** Only structured, repetitive problems solved

**Real example:** Banks using early computers to process thousands of cheques overnight instead of manually.

---

### Stage 2: 🟡 Management Information Systems (MIS) Era — 1970s

- **Focus:** Provide managers with regular reports for decision making
- Data processed in TPS was **summarized into reports** for middle managers
- Introduced concept of **information as a management tool**
- **Key Systems:** MIS, Report generators
- **Problem Solving Role:** Semi-structured problems at middle management level

**Real example:** A production manager receives weekly report on output vs. targets — can identify production shortfalls and act.

---

### Stage 3: 🟢 Decision Support Systems (DSS) Era — 1980s

- **Focus:** Support complex, non-routine managerial decisions
- Interactive systems that allowed managers to **ask "what-if" questions**
- Combined data + analytical models
- **Key Systems:** DSS, Executive Information Systems (EIS), Group Decision Support Systems (GDSS)
- **Problem Solving Role:** Semi-structured to unstructured problems

**Real example:** An airline using DSS to decide optimal pricing for different routes based on demand patterns, competitor prices, and seasonality.

---

### Stage 4: 🟠 Enterprise / Internet Era — 1990s-2000s

- **Focus:** Integrate ALL business processes organization-wide AND connect with external world
- **ERP systems** (SAP, Oracle) integrated finance, HR, supply chain, sales into ONE system
- **Internet** enabled e-commerce, B2B integration, global supply chains
- **Key Systems:** ERP, SCM (Supply Chain Management), CRM (Customer Relationship Management)
- **Problem Solving Role:** Integrated problem solving across entire organization

**Real example:** Reliance Industries deploying SAP ERP to connect its refining, retail, and telecom operations under one data system.

---

### Stage 5: 🔴 Knowledge / AI / Big Data Era — 2010s-Present

- **Focus:** Extract intelligence from massive data; enable autonomous decisions
- **Big Data, AI, Machine Learning, Cloud Computing, IoT** transform problem solving
- Machines now solve problems that required human expertise
- **Key Systems:** Business Intelligence (BI), AI platforms, Cloud ERP, IoT systems
- **Problem Solving Role:** Even unstructured, complex problems increasingly handled by AI

**Real example:**
- Amazon's recommendation engine (AI) → decides what to show each user
- Swiggy's surge pricing algorithm → adjusts delivery prices automatically based on demand
- Banks using AI for fraud detection → flags suspicious transactions in milliseconds

---

### IT in Problem Solving — The Gorry & Scott Morton Framework:

> Combined **decision structure** (structured/semi-structured/unstructured) with **management level** (operational/tactical/strategic) to match the right IT system.

| | Operational | Tactical (Middle Mgmt) | Strategic (Top Mgmt) |
|--|-------------|----------------------|---------------------|
| **Structured** | TPS (ERP) | MIS reports | — |
| **Semi-structured** | — | DSS | EIS |
| **Unstructured** | — | — | Expert Systems, AI |

---

### Types of Information Systems (Evolution Summary):

| System | Full Form | Users | Purpose | Example |
|--------|-----------|-------|---------|---------|
| **TPS** | Transaction Processing System | Operational level | Record daily transactions | POS machine at Reliance Retail |
| **MIS** | Management Information System | Middle management | Periodic reports | Weekly sales report for regional manager |
| **DSS** | Decision Support System | Middle/Senior mgmt | Analyze & model decisions | "What-if" pricing model for Flipkart |
| **EIS/ESS** | Executive Information System | Top management | Strategic overview | Dashboard showing company KPIs for CEO |
| **KMS** | Knowledge Management System | All levels | Capture & share expertise | Internal wiki at Infosys |
| **AI/BI** | Artificial Intelligence / Business Intelligence | All levels | Predictive analytics | Netflix recommendation engine |

## 4. 💡 DECISION MAKING IN BUSINESS

### What is Decision Making?

> **Decision Making** = The process of **identifying and selecting a course of action** from available alternatives to solve a problem or exploit an opportunity.

### Herbert Simon's Model of Decision Making:

> **Mnemonic: "ICD"** — *"I Choose Decisively"*
> - **I**ntelligence Phase — Find the problem
> - **C**hoice Phase — Choose the best alternative
> - **D**esign Phase — Develop alternatives

| Phase | What Happens | IT Support |
|-------|-------------|-----------|
| **Intelligence** | Scanning environment, identifying problems | MIS reports, dashboards, sensors |
| **Design** | Developing and analyzing possible solutions | DSS, modeling tools |
| **Choice** | Selecting the best solution | Optimization tools, AI |
| *(Implementation)* | Executing the chosen solution | ERP, workflow systems |
| *(Monitoring)* | Checking if decision achieved desired result | BI dashboards, KPI tracking |

---

### Types of Decisions (by Structure):

| Type | Characteristics | IT System | Example |
|------|----------------|-----------|---------|
| **Structured** | Repetitive, routine, clear rules → can be automated | TPS, ERP | Reorder stock when inventory falls below 100 units |
| **Semi-structured** | Partly routine + partly judgment required | DSS, MIS | Bank deciding whether to approve a large loan |
| **Unstructured** | Novel, complex, no clear procedure — pure judgment | EIS, AI, Expert Systems | CEO deciding to enter a new international market |

---

### Levels of Decision Making (Anthony's Triangle):

```
         ┌─────────────────┐
         │  STRATEGIC      │  ← Top Management (CEO, Board)
         │  Long-term,     │     Unstructured decisions
         │  Unstructured   │
         ├─────────────────┤
         │  TACTICAL       │  ← Middle Management (Managers)
         │  Medium-term,   │     Semi-structured decisions
         │  Semi-structured│
         ├─────────────────┤
         │  OPERATIONAL    │  ← Lower Management (Supervisors)
         │  Short-term,    │     Structured, routine decisions
         │  Structured     │
         └─────────────────┘
```

| Level | Who | Time Horizon | Decision Type | Example |
|-------|-----|-------------|--------------|---------|
| **Strategic** | CEO, Board | Long-term (3-5 yrs) | Unstructured | Enter new market? Acquire a company? |
| **Tactical** | Middle Managers | Medium-term (months) | Semi-structured | Allocate marketing budget? Hire 20 salespeople? |
| **Operational** | Supervisors | Short-term (daily) | Structured | Approve overtime? Reorder supplies? |

## 5. 📊 FACTORS AFFECTING THE COST AND VALUE OF INFORMATION

> Information is not free — it costs money to collect, process, and store. Its value depends on how useful it is for decision making. Organizations must weigh **cost vs value** of information.

---

### A. ✅ FACTORS AFFECTING THE VALUE OF INFORMATION

> **Mnemonic: "RACT-CAF"**
> - **R**elevance
> - **A**ccuracy
> - **C**ompleteness
> - **T**imeliness
> - **C**onsistency
> - **A**ccessibility
> - **F**orm/Format

| Factor | Definition | Example |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| **Relevance** | Information must relate to the decision at hand | Sales data by region is relevant for regional manager, not for HR manager |
| **Accuracy** | Information must be correct and error-free | Wrong stock data causes over-ordering or stockouts |
| **Completeness** | All necessary information must be present | A credit report missing income details is incomplete → bad loan decision |
| **Timeliness** | Information must be available WHEN needed | Competitor price data from 3 months ago is useless for today's pricing decision |
| **Consistency** | Same information should mean the same thing across the organization | Sales figures should match whether from Finance or Sales department |
| **Accessibility** | Information must be easy to access by those who need it | A manager who can't open a report due to system access issues wastes time |
| **Form/Presentation** | Information presented in the right format for the user | CEO needs graphs, not raw numbers; analyst needs detailed data |

---

### B. 💰 FACTORS AFFECTING THE COST OF INFORMATION

> **Mnemonic: "CAPS"**
> - **C**ollection cost
> - **A**nalysis/Processing cost
> - **P**eople/Labor cost
> - **S**torage and Infrastructure cost

| Cost Factor | What It Includes | Example |
|------------|-----------------|---------|
| **Collection Cost** | Cost of gathering raw data | Survey printing, field interviewers, sensors |
| **Processing Cost** | Cost of converting data into information | Software licenses, IT staff, cloud computing |
| **Storage Cost** | Cost of storing and maintaining data | Servers, databases, cloud storage fees |
| **Transmission Cost** | Cost of moving information to where it's needed | Network infrastructure, internet bandwidth |
| **Labor Cost** | Salaries of people working with information | Data analysts, MIS managers, IT staff |
| **Opportunity Cost** | Cost of NOT having information in time | A missed sales opportunity due to delayed market data |

---

### C. ⚖️ COST vs VALUE FRAMEWORK

> **The Golden Rule:** Collect information only when its **VALUE > COST** of collection.

```
Value of Information
        │
        │  ★ Ideal Zone
        │  (Value >> Cost → Worth collecting!)
        │
        │          ╱
        │         ╱  Break-even
        │        ╱
        │       ╱
        └──────────────────
                Cost of Information
```

**Practical Implications:**
1. Don't collect data you'll never use → Waste of money
2. Don't cut corners on critical data → Bad decisions are expensive
3. More detail is not always better — more detail = more cost but value may plateau
4. **Timeliness** often determines if information has value at all — stale information = zero value

---

### D. 📉 DIMINISHING VALUE OF INFORMATION

> Information value **decreases over time** — the same data becomes less valuable as time passes.

```
Value
 │ ↑ (Fresh, high value — just as decision is made)
 │ ╲
 │  ╲
 │   ╲
 │    ╲___________  (Old data — value approaches zero)
 └─────────────────→ Time
```

**Example:**
- Weather forecast: Extremely valuable today → useless after 3 days
- Stock prices: Real-time → valuable; last week's → irrelevant for trading
- Customer complaint: Resolve in 24 hours → satisfied customer; resolve after 1 week → churn

---

### E. 🎯 INFORMATION OVERLOAD

> **Information Overload** = Having SO MUCH information that decision makers become overwhelmed, leading to **worse** decisions.

- More data ≠ Better decisions beyond a point
- Modern managers suffer from information overload (100s of emails, reports, dashboards)
- **Solution:** Filters, dashboards, AI summarization, exception reporting (only highlight deviations)

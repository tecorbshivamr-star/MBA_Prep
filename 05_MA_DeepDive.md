## 📌 CHAPTER 1: Cost Concepts in Accounting

### 1.1 What is Management Accounting?

**Definition:**
Management Accounting is the process of identifying, measuring, analyzing, interpreting, and communicating financial information to managers for planning, decision-making, and control within an organization.

**Management Accounting vs Financial Accounting:**

| Aspect | Management Accounting | Financial Accounting |
|--------|----------------------|---------------------|
| Users | Internal (managers) | External (investors, banks) |
| Focus | Future-oriented | Past-oriented |
| Format | No fixed format | Follows AS/Ind AS standards |
| Frequency | As needed | Periodically (quarterly/annual) |
| Mandatory | No | Yes (Companies Act) |
| Audited | No | Yes |

---

### 1.2 Classification of Costs

**By Behaviour (Most Important!):**

| Cost Type | Behaviour | Formula | Example |
|-----------|-----------|---------|---------|
| **Fixed Cost** | Constant regardless of output | FC = Constant | Factory rent, Depreciation |
| **Variable Cost** | Changes proportionally with output | VC = v × Q | Raw materials, Direct labour |
| **Semi-Variable** | Fixed component + Variable component | SC = FC + v×Q | Electricity bill |
| **Step Cost** | Fixed within range, jumps at threshold | — | Supervisors (1 per 20 workers) |

**By Function:**
- **Manufacturing:** Direct Materials + Direct Labour + Factory Overhead
- **Non-Manufacturing:** Selling, Distribution, Administration

**By Traceability:**
- **Direct Costs:** Directly traceable to a product (Direct Material, Direct Labour)
- **Indirect Costs (Overhead):** Cannot be directly traced (rent, insurance)

**By Relevance for Decisions:**
| Cost Type | Definition | Example |
|-----------|------------|---------|
| **Relevant Cost** | Future cost that differs between alternatives | Raw material for a special order |
| **Sunk Cost** | Already incurred; irrelevant for decisions | Old machinery purchase price |
| **Opportunity Cost** | Benefit foregone by choosing one alternative | Interest lost by investing in a project |
| **Incremental Cost** | Additional cost of producing one more unit | Cost of overtime for extra batch |
| **Avoidable Cost** | Can be eliminated if activity stopped | Direct labour of a product line |

---

### 1.3 Cost Sheet — Structure

```
COST SHEET FORMAT:
Direct Materials (Opening Stock + Purchases - Closing Stock)
+ Direct Labour (Wages)
+ Direct Expenses
= PRIME COST
+ Factory Overhead (Manufacturing overhead)
= FACTORY COST / WORKS COST
+ Office & Administration Overhead
= COST OF PRODUCTION
+ Opening FG Stock - Closing FG Stock
= COST OF GOODS SOLD
+ Selling & Distribution Overhead
= COST OF SALES / TOTAL COST
+ Profit
= SELLING PRICE / SALES
```

---

### 1.4 Marginal Costing vs Absorption Costing

**Marginal Costing:**
- Only **variable costs** charged to products
- Fixed costs treated as **period costs** (written off in the period)
- Profit = Contribution - Fixed Costs

**Absorption Costing:**
- **All costs** (variable + fixed) absorbed into product cost
- Fixed overheads allocated to products using an absorption rate
- Profit = Sales - (Variable + Fixed costs absorbed)

**Contribution:**
```
Contribution = Sales Revenue - Variable Costs
            = Fixed Costs + Profit
```

**P/V Ratio (Profit-Volume Ratio):**
```
P/V Ratio = Contribution / Sales × 100
          = (Selling Price - Variable Cost) / Selling Price × 100
```

**Break-Even Point (BEP):**
```
BEP (units) = Fixed Costs / Contribution per unit
BEP (₹)    = Fixed Costs / P/V Ratio
```

**Margin of Safety:**
```
Margin of Safety = Actual Sales - Break-Even Sales
MOS Ratio = (Actual Sales - BEP Sales) / Actual Sales × 100
```

---

### 1.5 Case Study — Break-Even Analysis at a Manufacturing Company

**Background:** A bicycle manufacturer has:
- Fixed Costs = ₹12,00,000/year
- Variable Cost = ₹800/bicycle
- Selling Price = ₹1,200/bicycle

**Contribution per unit** = 1,200 - 800 = **₹400**
**BEP (units)** = 12,00,000 / 400 = **3,000 bicycles**
**BEP (₹)** = 12,00,000 / (400/1,200) = 12,00,000 / 0.333 = **₹36,00,000**

If actual sales = 4,500 bicycles:
**Margin of Safety** = 4,500 - 3,000 = **1,500 units** (33.3% of sales)

**Decision:** The 33.3% MOS means even if sales drop by 33%, the company still breaks even. Comfortable position.

## 📌 CHAPTER 2: Material, Labour and Overhead Cost and Control

### 2.1 Material Cost Control

**Objectives:** Ensure materials are purchased at right price, right quality, right time, and stored efficiently.

**Key Material Control Tools:**

**EOQ (Economic Order Quantity):**
```
EOQ = √(2 × A × O / C)
where A = Annual consumption, O = Ordering cost, C = Carrying cost per unit
```

**Reorder Level:** `= Maximum Usage × Maximum Lead Time`

**Minimum Stock Level:** `= Reorder Level - (Average Usage × Average Lead Time)`

**Maximum Stock Level:** `= Reorder Level + EOQ - (Minimum Usage × Minimum Lead Time)`

---

### 2.2 Labour Cost Control

**Labour Cost Components:**
- **Time Rate:** Payment based on hours worked
- **Piece Rate:** Payment based on units produced
- **Differential Piece Rate (Taylor's System):** Different rates for above/below standard output
- **Halsey Plan:** Share savings between worker and employer
- **Rowan Plan:** Bonus = (Time Saved / Standard Time) × Time Wages

**Labour Turnover Rate:**
```
Labour Turnover = (No. of workers left during period / Average workers) × 100
```

**Labour Efficiency Ratio:**
```
Labour Efficiency = Standard Hours / Actual Hours × 100
```

---

### 2.3 Overhead Cost Control

**Types of Overheads:**
- **Factory/Works Overhead:** Indirect manufacturing costs
- **Office Overhead:** Administration costs
- **Selling & Distribution Overhead:** Marketing costs

**Overhead Absorption Rate (OAR):**
```
OAR = Budgeted Overhead / Budgeted Base (labour hrs, machine hrs, output)
Absorbed Overhead = OAR × Actual Base
```

**Over/Under Absorption:**
```
Over Absorption = Absorbed Overhead > Actual Overhead (Credit to P&L)
Under Absorption = Absorbed Overhead < Actual Overhead (Debit to P&L)
```

## 📌 CHAPTER 3: Job, Batch and Contract Costing

### 3.1 Job Costing

**Definition:**
Job Costing is used when production is done against specific customer orders, and each job is unique and identifiable.

**Examples:** Printing press, law firms, construction, shipbuilding, IT project billing.

**Job Cost Sheet:**
```
Direct Material (from material requisition)
+ Direct Labour (from time sheets)
+ Direct Expenses
= Prime Cost
+ Factory Overhead (OAR × Actual base)
= Factory Cost
+ Admin Overhead
= Total Job Cost
+ Profit Margin
= Job Price (Quoted to Customer)
```

---

### 3.2 Batch Costing

**Definition:**
Batch Costing is a variant of job costing where a group (batch) of identical products is manufactured together. Cost is calculated per batch, then divided by units.

```
Cost per unit = Total Batch Cost / Number of units in batch
```

**Examples:** Pharmaceutical tablets, printed books, garments, biscuits.

**Optimal Batch Quantity (OBQ):**
```
OBQ = √(2 × Annual Demand × Setup Cost / Holding Cost per unit)
```
(Similar to EOQ but for production setup)

---

### 3.3 Contract Costing

**Definition:**
Contract Costing (also called Terminal Costing) is used for large, long-term contracts (usually > 1 year) like construction of buildings, bridges, highways.

**Key Features:**
- Each contract is a separate cost centre
- Work done at customer's site
- Payments made in installments (Progress Billing)

**Profit Recognition in Incomplete Contracts:**
```
Profit to be taken = Notional Profit × (Cash Received / Work Certified)
                  OR
Profit to be taken = Notional Profit × (2/3) × (Cash Received / Work Certified)
```

**Where:**
- Notional Profit = Work Certified - Cost of Work Certified
- Work Certified = Work completed and certified by architect/engineer

**Example:** A ₹5 crore highway contract — cost incurred ₹2.5 crore, work certified ₹3 crore, cash received ₹2.7 crore:
```
Notional Profit = 3 crore - 2.5 crore = ₹50 lakh
Profit to be taken = 50 lakh × (2/3) × (2.7/3) = 50 lakh × 0.667 × 0.9 = ₹30 lakh
```

**Indian Application:** L&T, DLF, NHAI contractors use contract costing for highway, metro, building projects.

## 📌 CHAPTER 4: Process Costing

### 4.1 What is Process Costing?

**Definition:**
Process Costing is used when production is continuous and homogeneous — identical units flow through a series of processes. Cost is averaged over all units.

**Examples:** Chemical plants, sugar mills, oil refineries, paper mills, paint manufacturing.

**Key Difference from Job Costing:**
| Job Costing | Process Costing |
|-------------|----------------|
| Unique jobs | Identical products |
| Cost per job | Cost averaged per unit |
| Custom manufacturing | Mass production |
| Furniture, Printing | Sugar, Oil refining |

---

### 4.2 Normal Loss and Abnormal Loss

In process industries, some loss during production is expected (evaporation, spillage).

**Normal Loss:** Expected, unavoidable loss. Often has scrap value.
```
Normal Output = Input - Normal Loss
Cost of Normal Output = Input cost - Scrap value of normal loss
Cost per unit = Cost of Normal Output / Normal Output units
```

**Abnormal Loss:** Loss ABOVE normal expectation. Charged to Abnormal Loss Account (treated as cost).
```
Abnormal Loss = Actual Loss - Normal Loss
Cost of Abnormal Loss = Abnormal Loss units × Cost per unit (of normal output)
```

**Abnormal Gain:** When actual output > Normal output. Credited to Abnormal Gain Account.

---

### 4.3 Equivalent Production Units

When closing WIP (partially completed) exists:

**Equivalent Units:** Convert incomplete units into equivalent complete units.

```
Equivalent Units = Completed Units + (Closing WIP × % completion)
Cost per equivalent unit = Total Cost / Total Equivalent Units
```

**Worked Example:**
- Input: 1,000 units; Normal loss: 10%; Closing WIP: 200 units (50% complete)
- Total cost: ₹9,000
- Normal output = 1,000 - 100 = 900 units
- Actual output completed = 600 units; WIP = 200 (50%) = 100 equivalent units
- Total equivalent units = 600 + 100 = 700
- Cost per equivalent unit = 9,000 / 700 = ₹12.86

## 📌 CHAPTER 5: Cost Concepts in Decision Making

### 5.1 Marginal Costing for Decisions

**Key Principle:** Use **Contribution** (not profit) for short-run decisions when fixed costs are irrelevant.

**Decision Situations:**

**1. Accept or Reject Special Order:**
Accept if: Selling price > Variable cost (positive contribution)
- Fixed costs already covered by regular sales → Any positive contribution is profit

**2. Make or Buy Decision:**
Make if: Variable cost of making < Purchase price (considering any avoidable fixed costs)
```
Make if: VC per unit < Market Price per unit (+ Fixed cost savings if any)
```

**3. Shut Down or Continue:**
Continue if: Contribution > Avoidable Fixed Costs
Shut down if: Contribution < Avoidable Fixed Costs

**4. Key Factor / Limiting Factor Analysis:**
When a resource is scarce, rank products by **Contribution per unit of limiting factor**.
```
Ranking = Contribution per unit / Units of limiting factor required
```

**5. Sell or Process Further:**
Process further if: Additional Revenue > Additional Cost of further processing

---

### 5.2 Case Study — HUL: Make or Buy Decision

**Scenario:** HUL evaluates whether to manufacture packaging internally or outsource.
- Internal cost: ₹5/pack (VC=₹3.50, FC=₹1.50)
- Outsource price: ₹4.20/pack
- FC are unavoidable (factory lease)

**Decision:**
- Relevant cost of making = Variable cost only = **₹3.50** (FC irrelevant as unavoidable)
- Outsource cost = **₹4.20**
- Since ₹3.50 < ₹4.20 → **Continue making internally**

If fixed costs were avoidable:
- Total cost of making = ₹3.50 + ₹1.50 = ₹5.00 > ₹4.20 → **Outsource**

**Lesson:** Sunk/unavoidable fixed costs are irrelevant to decisions. Only relevant (incremental) costs matter.

## 📌 CHAPTER 6: Standard Costing and Variance Analysis

### 6.1 Standard Costing

**Definition:**
Standard Costing is a system where predetermined (standard) costs are set for materials, labour, and overheads. Actual costs are compared to standard costs to identify variances.

**Purpose:** Control, planning, performance evaluation, pricing decisions.

---

### 6.2 Variance Analysis — All Key Formulas

**MATERIAL VARIANCES:**
```
Material Cost Variance (MCV) = Standard Cost - Actual Cost
                             = (SQ × SP) - (AQ × AP)

Material Price Variance (MPV) = AQ × (SP - AP)

Material Usage Variance (MUV) = SP × (SQ - AQ)
Material Mix Variance (MMV)   = SP × (Revised SQ - AQ)
Material Yield Variance (MYV) = SP × (Actual Yield - Standard Yield)

CHECK: MCV = MPV + MUV
       MUV = MMV + MYV
```

**LABOUR VARIANCES:**
```
Labour Cost Variance (LCV) = (SH × SR) - (AH × AR)

Labour Rate Variance (LRV)      = AH × (SR - AR)
Labour Efficiency Variance (LEV)= SR × (SH - AH)
Labour Idle Time Variance (LITV)= SR × Idle Hours (Adverse)
Labour Mix Variance              = SR × (Revised SH - AH)
```

**OVERHEAD VARIANCES (Fixed):**
```
Fixed Overhead Total Variance = Absorbed OH - Actual OH
Fixed OH Expenditure Variance = Budgeted OH - Actual OH
Fixed OH Volume Variance      = Absorbed OH - Budgeted OH
  Volume Efficiency Variance  = SR × (Standard hours for actual output - Budgeted hours)
  Volume Capacity Variance    = SR × (Budgeted hours - Actual hours worked)
```

**SALES VARIANCES:**
```
Sales Value Variance   = Actual Sales - Budgeted Sales
Sales Price Variance   = Actual Qty × (Actual Price - Standard Price)
Sales Volume Variance  = Standard Price × (Actual Volume - Budgeted Volume)
```

---

### 6.3 Variance Interpretation

| Variance | Favourable | Adverse |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| Material Price | Bought cheaper than standard | Paid more than standard |
| Material Usage | Used less than standard | Used more than standard |
| Labour Rate | Paid less per hour than standard | Paid more per hour |
| Labour Efficiency | Took fewer hours than standard | Took more hours |

---

### 6.4 Worked Example — Material Variance

**Data:**
- Standard: 5 kg @ ₹10/kg for Product A
- Actual: Produced 100 units. Actual material used: 480 kg @ ₹11/kg

```
SQ for actual output = 100 × 5 = 500 kg
SP = ₹10/kg; AP = ₹11/kg; AQ = 480 kg

MCV = (500 × 10) - (480 × 11) = 5,000 - 5,280 = ₹280 (Adverse)
MPV = 480 × (10 - 11) = -480 = ₹480 (Adverse)
MUV = 10 × (500 - 480) = 10 × 20 = ₹200 (Favourable)
CHECK: MCV = MPV + MUV = -480 + 200 = -280 ✓ (Adverse ₹280)
```

**Interpretation:**
- More was spent (₹5,280 vs ₹5,000) → Adverse variance of ₹280
- Material was bought at a higher price (₹11 vs ₹10) → Adverse price variance ₹480
- But less material was used (480 kg vs 500 kg) → Favourable usage ₹200
- Net: Adverse ₹280 (price increase more than offset usage saving)

## 📌 CHAPTER 7: Budgets and Budgetary Control

### 7.1 What is a Budget?

**Definition:**
A budget is a formal written statement of management's plans for a specified future period, expressed in financial terms (₹) and/or physical terms (units).

**Budgetary Control:** The process of comparing actual performance with the budget and taking corrective action.

---

### 7.2 Types of Budgets

| Budget Type | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| **Sales Budget** | Foundation budget; quantity and value of expected sales |
| **Production Budget** | Units to be produced = Sales + Closing stock - Opening stock |
| **Material Budget** | Material required for production |
| **Labour Budget** | Labour hours needed |
| **Cash Budget** | Forecast of cash receipts and payments |
| **Master Budget** | Consolidates all budgets; includes P&L and Balance Sheet |
| **Fixed Budget** | Prepared for one level of activity |
| **Flexible Budget** | Adjusted for different levels of activity |

---

### 7.3 Flexible Budget

**Definition:**
A Flexible Budget adjusts the budget figures for the actual level of activity achieved, enabling meaningful comparison with actual costs.

**Key Principle:** Fixed costs remain fixed; variable costs flex with output.

**Example:**
| Item | Fixed Budget (10,000 units) | Flexible Budget (8,000 units) | Actual (8,000 units) |
|------|----------------------------|------------------------------|---------------------|
| Sales @₹20 | 2,00,000 | 1,60,000 | 1,56,000 |
| Variable Costs @₹12 | 1,20,000 | 96,000 | 90,000 |
| Fixed Costs | 40,000 | 40,000 | 42,000 |
| **Profit** | **40,000** | **24,000** | **24,000** |

Comparing actual with flexible budget: Sales variance = 1,56,000 - 1,60,000 = ₹4,000 (Adverse in selling price); Variable cost variance = ₹6,000 (Favourable — used less)

---

### 7.4 Zero-Based Budgeting (ZBB)

**Definition:**
In ZBB, every budget starts from zero (scratch) each period. Every expense must be justified from the ground up, regardless of last year's budget.

**Contrast with Traditional:** Traditional budgeting = last year + increment. ZBB = justify everything.

**Advantages:** Eliminates waste; forces re-evaluation of all activities.
**Disadvantages:** Time-consuming; complex.

**India Application:** Several Indian states and PSUs have adopted elements of ZBB. NITI Aayog has recommended ZBB principles for government departments.

## 📌 CHAPTER 8: Cost Management

### 8.1 Activity-Based Costing (ABC)

**Definition:**
ABC is a costing method that identifies activities in an organization and assigns costs to products based on their use of those activities — more accurately than traditional overhead allocation.

**Traditional vs ABC:**
- **Traditional:** Single overhead rate (e.g., ₹X per machine hour) for ALL products
- **ABC:** Multiple cost drivers; each activity has its own rate

**Steps in ABC:**
1. Identify activities (e.g., machine setup, quality inspection, purchasing)
2. Assign costs to each activity (Activity Cost Pool)
3. Identify cost drivers for each activity
4. Calculate Activity Cost Rate = Pool Cost / Driver Volume
5. Assign costs to products: Cost = Activity Rate × Product's driver usage

**Example — Activity Rate:**
- Setup Department cost pool: ₹5,00,000
- No. of setups: 100
- Activity rate = ₹5,000/setup
- Product X needs 20 setups → Setup cost = ₹1,00,000

---

### 8.2 Target Costing

**Definition:**
Target Costing is a cost management approach where the target cost is determined by subtracting the desired profit from the competitive market price.

```
Target Cost = Market Price - Desired Profit
```

**Process:**
1. Market determines price (customer-driven)
2. Subtract desired profit margin
3. Design product to achieve target cost (engineering the cost out)

**Example:** Toyota sets market price of a car at ₹10 lakh. Desired profit = ₹1.5 lakh → Target cost = ₹8.5 lakh. Engineers then design the car to be manufactured at ≤ ₹8.5 lakh.

---

### 8.3 Life Cycle Costing

**Definition:**
Life Cycle Costing tracks costs across the entire product life cycle — from design through disposal — not just manufacturing.

**Stages:** R&D → Design → Manufacturing → Marketing → After-sales → Disposal

**Insight:** 80% of a product's costs are committed during the design stage (design-in costs). Manufacturing is where most costs are incurred (locked-in costs).

## 📌 CHAPTER 9: Performance Measurement — Balanced Scorecard

### 9.1 Balanced Scorecard (BSC)

**Definition (Kaplan & Norton, 1992):**
The Balanced Scorecard is a strategic management tool that measures organizational performance across FOUR perspectives, providing a balanced view beyond just financial metrics.

**Four Perspectives:**

| Perspective | Question | Example KPIs |
|-------------|---------|--------------|
| **Financial** | How do we look to shareholders? | ROI, EVA, Revenue growth, Profit margin |
| **Customer** | How do customers see us? | NPS, Customer retention, Market share |
| **Internal Process** | What must we excel at? | Cycle time, Defect rate, OEE |
| **Learning & Growth** | Can we improve and create value? | Employee training hours, Innovation rate |

**Key Insight:** Financial metrics alone are lagging indicators (they tell you what happened). BSC adds leading indicators (customer satisfaction, internal processes) that predict future financial performance.

---

### 9.2 Economic Value Added (EVA)

**Definition:**
EVA measures the true economic profit of a company after accounting for the cost of ALL capital (debt + equity).

```
EVA = NOPAT - (WACC × Capital Employed)
    = Net Operating Profit After Tax - Capital Charge
```

**Positive EVA:** Company creates value for shareholders
**Negative EVA:** Company destroys value (even if accounting profit is positive!)

**Example:**
- NOPAT = ₹100 crore
- Capital Employed = ₹800 crore; WACC = 14%
- Capital Charge = 800 × 14% = ₹112 crore
- **EVA = 100 - 112 = -₹12 crore (Value destroyed!)**

Despite accounting profit of ₹100 crore, this company doesn't cover its cost of capital.

**India Use:** Tata Group uses EVA extensively as an internal performance metric across its businesses.

## 📌 CHAPTER 10 & 11: Inventory Management & Performance Evaluation

### 10.1 Inventory Management

**ABC Analysis:**
- **A items:** High value, low quantity (20% items = 80% value) → Tight control
- **B items:** Medium value, medium quantity → Moderate control
- **C items:** Low value, high quantity (80% items = 20% value) → Simple control

**VED Analysis (for spare parts):**
- **V (Vital):** Must not run out → High safety stock
- **E (Essential):** Moderate criticality
- **D (Desirable):** Can wait

**Just-In-Time (JIT):**
- Inventory arrives exactly when needed for production
- Zero buffer stock goal
- Requires reliable suppliers and accurate demand forecasting
- **Example:** Maruti Suzuki — suppliers deliver parts on just-in-time basis to Manesar plant

---

### 10.2 Performance Measurement Ratios

**Profitability Ratios:**
```
ROI = Net Profit / Investment × 100
ROE = Net Profit / Shareholders' Equity × 100
ROCE = EBIT / Capital Employed × 100
Gross Profit Ratio = Gross Profit / Net Sales × 100
Net Profit Ratio = Net Profit / Net Sales × 100
```

**Efficiency Ratios:**
```
Asset Turnover = Net Sales / Total Assets
Inventory Turnover = COGS / Average Inventory
Debtor Turnover = Net Credit Sales / Average Debtors
Creditor Turnover = Net Credit Purchases / Average Creditors
```

**Liquidity Ratios:**
```
Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities (ideal: 2:1)
Quick Ratio = (Current Assets - Inventory) / Current Liabilities (ideal: 1:1)
```

**DuPont Analysis:**
```
ROE = Net Profit Margin × Asset Turnover × Equity Multiplier
    = (PAT/Sales) × (Sales/Assets) × (Assets/Equity)
```

### 1. ORGANISATION EFFECTIVENESS & CHANGE (OEC)

| Key Term | Plain English Definition | Real-World Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Organizational Change** | Altering the people, structure, or technology of a company to adapt to the market. | Jio launching free 4G forced Airtel to change its pricing structure entirely to survive. |
| **Lewin’s 3-Step Model** | A change strategy: Unfreeze (break old habits) → Change (implement new) → Refreeze (make it permanent). | Switching from manual testing to automation: Realizing manual is slow (Unfreeze), learning Python (Change), making Python the new daily standard (Refreeze). |
| **Distributive Negotiation** | A win-lose battle over a fixed pie. If I get more, you get less. | Haggling over the price of a used car. Every rupee the buyer saves, the seller loses. |
| **Integrative Negotiation** | A win-win approach that expands the pie by finding creative solutions that help both sides. | Negotiating a job offer: The company can't increase the salary, so they offer you remote work on Fridays instead. Both win. |
| **BATNA** | Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement. The power you hold if you walk away. | You are negotiating a 6 LPA salary, but you already have a 5.5 LPA offer from another company. The 5.5 LPA is your BATNA. |

### 2. MANAGEMENT OF INFORMATION SYSTEMS (MIS) & MR

| Key Term | Plain English Definition | Real-World Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP)** | A massive software system that connects all departments (HR, Finance, Sales) into one database. | SAP or Oracle. When a sale is made, the warehouse and finance teams see it instantly. |
| **Database Management System (DBMS)** | Software used to safely store, organize, and retrieve massive amounts of data. | SQL databases used by banks to instantly retrieve your account balance without errors. |
| **Agile Methodology** | A flexible project management style where work is done in short 2-week "sprints" instead of planning everything upfront. | Software development teams building an app feature by feature and taking customer feedback weekly. |
| **Primary Data (MR)** | Fresh data collected specifically for your current research problem. | Conducting a brand new survey asking 100 people if they like a new shoe design. |
| **Secondary Data (MR)** | Existing data that was collected by someone else in the past. | Looking up historical sales reports or reading government census data. |

### 3. ECONOMIC ENVIRONMENT OF BUSINESS (EEB)

| Key Term | Plain English Definition | Real-World Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Monetary Policy** | The Central Bank (RBI) changing interest rates and money supply to control inflation. | The RBI increases the Repo Rate, making home loans expensive, which stops people from buying houses and cools down inflation. |
| **Fiscal Policy** | The Government changing tax rates or public spending to boost the economy. | The government spending ₹50,000 Crores to build new highways, which creates jobs and injects money into the economy. |
| **Phillips Curve** | An economic theory stating that inflation and unemployment have an inverse relationship. | When the economy is booming and everyone has a job (low unemployment), people spend more, causing prices to rise (high inflation). |
| **Balance of Payments (BoP)** | A master ledger recording all money flowing into and out of a country globally. | India's BoP tracks how much money we spent buying oil from Russia vs how much money we earned selling IT services to the USA. |

### 4. PRODUCTION & OPERATIONS (POM) & DMO

| Key Term | Plain English Definition | Real-World Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Just-In-Time (JIT)** | Receiving inventory exactly when you need it for production to eliminate storage costs. | Toyota orders car parts to arrive at the factory exactly 2 hours before the car is built, saving millions on warehouse space. |
| **Six Sigma** | A strict quality control process aiming for near-perfection (only 3.4 defects per million). | A hospital using Six Sigma to ensure that out of 1 million surgeries, absolutely zero instruments are left inside patients. |
| **Linear Programming (LP)** | A math technique to find the maximum profit or minimum cost when you have limited resources. | A factory has limited steel and limited labor hours. LP calculates exactly how many cars vs bikes they should build to maximize profit. |
| **Monte Carlo Simulation** | Using a computer to run thousands of random scenarios to predict the risk of a decision. | Running 10,000 simulations of the stock market to see the percentage chance your retirement fund will run out of money. |

### 5. FINANCE & ACCOUNTING (CF & MA)

| Key Term | Plain English Definition | Real-World Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Capital Structure** | The exact mix of debt (loans) and equity (shares) a company uses to fund itself. | A company that is funded by 40% bank loans and 60% shareholder money. |
| **Working Capital** | The cash needed to run the day-to-day operations of the business (Current Assets - Current Liabilities). | The money needed to pay this month's electricity bill, employee salaries, and buy raw materials. |
| **Zero-Based Budgeting (ZBB)** | A budgeting method where every department must justify every single expense from scratch every year. | Instead of just giving the Marketing team 10% more money than last year, the CEO makes them prove why they need every single rupee. |
| **Activity-Based Costing (ABC)** | Assigning hidden overhead costs (like electricity or HR) to specific products based on how much of those activities the product actually uses. | A factory makes basic pens and luxury pens. ABC reveals that luxury pens use 80% of the quality-control inspector's time, so luxury pens should absorb 80% of his salary cost. |

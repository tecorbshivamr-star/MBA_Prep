## 🟢 BEGINNER LEVEL — "What is this about?"

Imagine two colleagues — Priya (from Sales) and Rahul (from Production) — constantly fighting:
- Priya: "Give me more products! Customers are waiting!"
- Rahul: "We can't produce faster without more budget!"

This disagreement is **Conflict** — a normal part of every organization.

Now imagine their manager sits them down and says:
- "Priya, why exactly do you need more products?"
- "Rahul, what resources would help you produce faster?"
- "Let's find a solution that works for both."

This process of reaching a solution is **Negotiation**.

And when the entire Sales team vs. Production team keeps fighting — that's **Intergroup Conflict**.

This chapter teaches you: WHY conflict happens, whether it's good or bad, and HOW to handle it professionally.

## 🔵 WHAT IS CONFLICT?

**Definition:**
Conflict is a process that begins when one party perceives that another party has negatively affected, or is about to negatively affect, something the first party cares about.

**Key word: PERCEIVED** — Conflict begins in the mind. Even if no actual harm is done, if you THINK someone is blocking your goals, conflict starts.

## 🔵 IS CONFLICT GOOD OR BAD?

This is a classic exam question! The answer has evolved over time:

### Three Views on Conflict:

| View | Period | Belief |
|------|--------|--------|
| **Traditional View** | Pre-1940s | Conflict is bad; must be eliminated |
| **Human Relations View** | 1940s-70s | Conflict is natural; can be accepted |
| **Interactionist View** | 1970s-present | Some conflict is NECESSARY for good decisions |

**The Interactionist View (Modern):**
- Too little conflict → Complacency, groupthink, stagnation
- Too much conflict → Chaos, stress, dysfunction
- **Optimal level of conflict → Best performance**

### Types of Conflict by Nature:

| Type | Description | Good or Bad? |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **Task Conflict** | Disagreement about WHAT to do (content, goals) | Can be GOOD — leads to better decisions |
| **Relationship Conflict** | Personal dislike, interpersonal friction | Almost always BAD — hurts performance |
| **Process Conflict** | Disagreement about HOW to do things | Low levels can be okay |

**India Example:**
- Task conflict: Flipkart's product team arguing about which features to add to the app → Healthy debate → Better product
- Relationship conflict: Two senior executives at a company personally disliking each other → Backstabbing, politics → Terrible outcome

## 🔵 THE CONFLICT PROCESS (Pondy's Model)

Conflict goes through stages:

```
Stage 1: LATENT CONFLICT
(Conditions for conflict exist — scarce resources, different goals)
         ↓
Stage 2: PERCEIVED CONFLICT
(One or both parties become aware of the conflict)
         ↓
Stage 3: FELT CONFLICT
(Emotional involvement — anxiety, tension, hostility)
         ↓
Stage 4: MANIFEST CONFLICT
(Visible, open conflict behavior — arguments, strikes, passive resistance)
         ↓
Stage 5: CONFLICT AFTERMATH
(Resolution or suppression; sets stage for future conflict)
```

**Example:**
- Stage 1 (Latent): HR and Finance both competing for same budget pool
- Stage 2 (Perceived): HR realizes Finance got more funds last year
- Stage 3 (Felt): HR manager feels frustrated and angry
- Stage 4 (Manifest): HR manager openly challenges Finance head in a meeting
- Stage 5 (Aftermath): They compromise on budget sharing; relationship is strained for months

## 🔵 THOMAS-KILMANN CONFLICT STYLES ⭐ Most Tested

Kenneth Thomas and Ralph Kilmann identified 5 ways people handle conflict based on two dimensions:
- **Assertiveness** (how much you try to satisfy YOUR own concerns)
- **Cooperativeness** (how much you try to satisfy THE OTHER PARTY's concerns)

```
HIGH
ASSERTIVENESS
     |
COMPETING  |  COLLABORATING
(Win-Lose) |  (Win-Win)
           |
───────────┼────────────────→
           |           HIGH
   AVOIDING|COMPROMISING   COOPERATIVENESS
           |
AVOIDING   |  ACCOMMODATING
(No win)   |  (Lose-Win)
LOW
ASSERTIVENESS
```

### The 5 Styles:

**1. COMPETING (High Assertive, Low Cooperative) — "My Way"**
- Win-lose approach; one party imposes its will
- Use when: Emergency, unpopular but correct decision, self-defense
- Risk: Damages relationships; breeds resentment
- Example: A manager saying "We're doing it my way, discussion closed"

**2. COLLABORATING (High Assertive, High Cooperative) — "Our Way"**
- Win-win; both parties work together to find solution that satisfies both
- Best style for important issues where both parties' needs matter
- Requires trust, time, and communication skill
- Example: Sales and Production sitting down to create a production schedule that meets both targets
- Best for complex organizational conflicts

**3. COMPROMISING (Moderate Assertive, Moderate Cooperative) — "Half-Way"**
- Each party gives up something; neither fully satisfied
- Use when: Time pressure, temporary solution, equal power parties
- Risk: "Lose-lose" if both sacrificed critical interests
- Example: Budget split 50-50 between two departments when both wanted 70%

**4. AVOIDING (Low Assertive, Low Cooperative) — "Not Now"**
- Sidestepping or withdrawing from conflict
- Use when: Issue is trivial, you need time to cool down, conflict will resolve itself
- Risk: Problem festers and gets worse
- Example: Ignoring a colleague's rude comment in a meeting

**5. ACCOMMODATING (Low Assertive, High Cooperative) — "Your Way"**
- Yielding to the other party's wishes
- Use when: Issue more important to other party, preserving relationship matters
- Risk: Being seen as weak; your own needs unmet
- Example: Junior employee agreeing to senior's approach even when they know a better way

### Which Style is "Best"?
None is universally best. **Context determines the best style.** A great manager switches between styles depending on the situation.

## 🔵 NEGOTIATION

### Definition:
Negotiation is a process in which two or more parties exchange goods or services and attempt to agree on the exchange rate (terms) for them.

**BATNA — Most Important Concept in Negotiation:**

**BATNA = Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement**

- Your BATNA is what you will do IF the negotiation fails
- The stronger your BATNA, the more negotiating power you have
- Example: If you're negotiating a salary and have another job offer (BATNA), you can negotiate harder
- NEVER reveal your BATNA to the other party (unless it strengthens your position)

**Reservation Price:** The least favorable point at which you'll still accept a deal (your walkaway point)

**ZOPA (Zone of Possible Agreement):** The range between the two parties' reservation prices where a deal is possible

## 🔵 TWO APPROACHES TO NEGOTIATION

### Approach 1: DISTRIBUTIVE NEGOTIATION (Win-Lose) ⭐

- **Also called:** Positional bargaining, fixed-pie, zero-sum
- The size of the "pie" is fixed; more for one = less for the other
- Parties take positions and make concessions
- Focus: POSITIONS (what each party demands)
- Relationship: Not important (often one-time transaction)

**Classic Example:**
- Buyer wants to pay ₹500 for a shirt; Seller wants ₹800
- They haggle until they agree at ₹650
- Neither got what they really wanted; it's a compromise

**Tactics in Distributive Negotiation:**
- **Anchoring:** Make the first offer (sets the reference point)
- **Extreme offers:** Start far from your target to give room for concessions
- **Deadline pressure:** "This offer expires today"
- **Good cop/Bad cop:** One person is tough; another is reasonable

### Approach 2: INTEGRATIVE NEGOTIATION (Win-Win) ⭐

- **Also called:** Interest-based, principled, expand-the-pie
- Focus on INTERESTS (why each party wants what they want), not positions
- Seek creative solutions that satisfy both parties
- Relationship: Preserved or strengthened
- Best for: Long-term relationships, complex issues, when multiple variables exist

**Classic Story:**
Two sisters fighting over one orange.
- **Distributive:** Cut orange in half (50-50 compromise — both lose)
- **Integrative:** Ask WHY each wants it
  - Sister 1: "I want the juice" (for drinking)
  - Sister 2: "I want the peel" (for baking a cake)
  - Solution: Sister 1 gets ALL the juice; Sister 2 gets ALL the peel — BOTH fully satisfied!

**India Business Example:**
Reliance and a construction contractor negotiating payment terms:
- Position: Reliance wants to pay after project completion; Contractor wants advance payment
- Interest: Reliance wants to conserve cash; Contractor needs cash flow to pay workers
- Integrative solution: Progress-based milestone payments (contractor gets cash at each phase; Reliance pays only for completed work)
- Both needs met!

## 🔵 HARVARD MODEL OF PRINCIPLED NEGOTIATION ⭐

Roger Fisher and William Ury's "Getting to Yes" (1981) — the most influential negotiation book ever written. They proposed 4 principles:

**Principle 1: SEPARATE PEOPLE FROM THE PROBLEM**
- Conflict is with the ISSUE, not the person
- "Be hard on the problem, soft on the people"
- Attack the problem, not the opponent
- Example: "Your delivery schedule is creating a problem for us" (not "You are irresponsible")

**Principle 2: FOCUS ON INTERESTS, NOT POSITIONS**
- Positions: "I want ₹1,000" (what you say you want)
- Interests: "I need enough money to cover my rent" (WHY you want it)
- Understanding interests opens up creative solutions
- Ask "Why?" repeatedly to uncover real interests

**Principle 3: INVENT OPTIONS FOR MUTUAL GAIN**
- Brainstorm multiple solutions before deciding
- Don't evaluate options while generating (kills creativity)
- Look for trades (something of low value to me, high value to you = good trade)
- Example: Can't raise salary? Offer flexible working hours (costs nothing to company, high value to employee)

**Principle 4: INSIST ON OBJECTIVE CRITERIA**
- Base decisions on fair standards, not willpower
- Use market rates, expert opinion, legal standards, precedent
- Example: "Let's use SEBI market pricing guidelines to settle the share valuation dispute"

## 🔵 CHAPTERS 3 & 4 COMBINED: INTERGROUP BEHAVIOR AND CONFLICT

### What is Intergroup Conflict?
When conflict exists not between individuals but between **entire groups** — departments, teams, divisions, or organizations.

### Why Intergroup Conflict Occurs:

| Cause | Example |
|-------|---------|
| **Shared resources** | IT budget split between Marketing and Operations |
| **Goal incompatibility** | Sales: maximize revenue; Finance: minimize costs |
| **Task interdependence** | Design team delayed → Manufacturing team can't start |
| **Jurisdictional ambiguity** | Who owns the customer relationship — Sales or Customer Success? |
| **Status and power** | One department feels it's more important/valuable |
| **Perceptual differences** | Marketing sees a customer problem; Engineering doesn't |
| **Communication gaps** | Miscommunication between India and USA team in offshore model |

### Intergroup Conflict Resolution Strategies:

| Strategy | Description | When to Use |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Superordinate Goals** | Establish a larger goal both groups must achieve together | Always — most powerful |
| **Expand Resources** | Increase the pool so both groups get enough | When resource scarcity is the cause |
| **Problem-Solving** | Joint meetings to understand both perspectives | When both parties willing |
| **Smoothing** | Emphasize commonalities; downplay differences | Short-term; low-stakes |
| **Compromise** | Both give up something | Temporary resolution |
| **Authority** | Superior commands both groups | Fast, but breeds resentment |
| **Structural Intervention** | Restructure reporting lines, create integrating roles | Long-term solution |
| **Boundary Spanners** | Appoint liaison roles between groups | Permanent coordination mechanism |

**India Case — Infosys Sales vs. Delivery teams:**
Classic IT company conflict: Sales promises "anything" to win projects; Delivery team can't deliver what was promised.
- Superordinate goal: "Customer satisfaction = Everyone wins bonuses"
- Structural: Created "Engagement Managers" who sit between Sales and Delivery
- Process: Sales proposal must be reviewed by Delivery before submission
- Result: Over-promising reduced; customer satisfaction improved

## 🔴 EXAM-READY CONTENT

### 5-Mark Answer: "Explain Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Styles with examples"

**Ans:** Thomas and Kilmann identified five conflict-handling styles based on two dimensions: assertiveness (concern for self) and cooperativeness (concern for others).

**1. Competing** (High assertive, low cooperative): Win-lose; impose your will. Useful in emergencies. Risk: Damages relationships. *Example: Manager insisting on deadline even when team objects.*

**2. Collaborating** (High assertive, high cooperative): Win-win; jointly find solutions. Best for complex issues. *Example: Sales and Production jointly designing a production-delivery schedule.*

**3. Compromising** (Moderate both): Split the difference; temporary solution. *Example: Two departments each getting 50% of requested budget.*

**4. Avoiding** (Low both): Withdraw; defer. Useful when issue is trivial. *Example: Ignoring a minor personality clash.*

**5. Accommodating** (Low assertive, high cooperative): Yield to other party. Useful when preserving relationship matters more. *Example: Junior accepting senior's decision to maintain harmony.*

**Conclusion:** No single style is universally best. Effective managers are flexible — choosing the right style for the right situation.

### 10-Mark Answer: "Compare Distributive and Integrative Negotiation. Which is better for organizational settings?"

**Introduction:**
Negotiation is a core managerial skill. Two fundamentally different approaches exist, each suited to different circumstances.

**Distributive Negotiation:**
- Fixed-pie assumption; one party's gain is another's loss
- Focus on positions; information is hidden; tactics used
- Best for: One-time transactions, price negotiation, commodity purchases
- Example: Vendor negotiating price for raw materials on a one-time purchase

**Integrative Negotiation:**
- Expand-the-pie; creative solutions that satisfy both
- Focus on interests; information is shared; problem-solving
- Best for: Ongoing relationships, complex multi-issue negotiations
- Example: Employee-employer negotiating a flexible work arrangement that meets both needs

**Harvard Model (Fisher & Ury):**
[Explain all 4 principles]

**Which is Better for Organizations?**
- Integrative negotiation is generally superior for organizational settings because:
  1. Most organizational relationships are ongoing (not one-time)
  2. Organizations have multiple interdependent goals → creative solutions possible
  3. Distributive tactics damage trust and long-term relationships
  4. Integrative outcomes create more total value (bigger pie)

**However:** Pure integrative negotiation requires trust and willingness to share information. When trust is absent or relationship is truly one-time → Distributive elements necessary.

**Conclusion:** Skilled organizational negotiators blend both approaches — establishing rapport and using integrative tools for key issues, while being appropriately firm on critical deal points.

# CHAPTER 5: POWER AND POLITICS IN ORGANIZATIONS

## 🟢 BEGINNER LEVEL — "What is this about?"

Think about your college. The principal has official authority. But there's always that one professor EVERYONE listens to — the principal consults them, students respect them — even though they're not in a formal leadership role. Why?

Because they have **Power** that goes beyond their official position.

Now imagine some people always finding ways to make themselves look good to the principal — always in the right place at the right time, befriending the right people. That's **Organizational Politics**.

Power and politics are REAL in every organization. Pretending they don't exist is naive. Managing them skillfully is a core managerial competency.

---

## 🔵 WHAT IS POWER?

**Definition:**
Power is the capacity to influence the behavior of others to get what you want. It is the ability to make things happen — to get others to act in ways they might not otherwise act.

**Power vs. Authority:**
| Aspect | Power | Authority |
|--------|-------|-----------|
| Source | Multiple sources | Formal position/hierarchy |
| Direction | Any direction | Top-down |
| Basis | Perception | Structure |
| Example | Expert consultant | HR Manager |

**Dependency is Key to Power:**
The more others depend on you, the more power you have.
- Dependency increases when what you control is: **Scarce + Important + Non-substitutable**

---

## 🔵 FRENCH & RAVEN'S FIVE BASES OF POWER ⭐ Most Tested

John French and Bertram Raven (1959) identified five sources of power:

### 1. REWARD POWER
- The ability to give or withhold rewards
- Rewards: Pay raises, promotions, recognition, good assignments
- **Example:** A manager who controls team bonuses has reward power
- **Risk:** If rewards are perceived as insufficient or unfair, power weakens

### 2. COERCIVE POWER
- The ability to punish or threaten punishment
- Punishments: Demotion, termination, poor assignments, public criticism
- **Example:** "Do this or you're fired"
- **Risk:** Creates fear and resentment; high attrition; people do minimum to avoid punishment
- Least effective long-term

### 3. LEGITIMATE POWER (Positional Power)
- Power that comes from formal position/role in the organization
- "I am the CEO, therefore you report to me"
- **Example:** A District Collector has legitimate power over all district offices
- **Basis:** Organizational structure; social norms
- **Limit:** Works only within the scope of the role; outside work, it has no power

### 4. REFERENT POWER
- Power based on admiration, respect, and identification
- People follow you because they LIKE and ADMIRE you, not because they have to
- **Also called:** Charisma
- **Example:** Narayana Murthy — employees would work extra hard simply because they admired and respected him
- **Most powerful and sustainable** form of power
- Cannot be bought or assigned — it is earned through character and behavior

### 5. EXPERT POWER
- Power derived from having superior knowledge, skill, or expertise
- **Example:** A cybersecurity expert at a firm — even the CEO listens to them on security matters
- **India:** In knowledge organizations (TCS, Infosys, HUL), expert power is crucial
- **Risk:** Quickly obsolete if expertise becomes irrelevant

### Summary Table:

| Power Base | Source | Positional or Personal? | Sustainability |
|-----------|--------|------------------------|---------------|
| Reward | Control over rewards | Positional | Medium |
| Coercive | Control over punishment | Positional | Low (breeds resentment) |
| Legitimate | Formal role/hierarchy | Positional | Medium |
| Referent | Admiration, charisma | Personal | High |
| Expert | Knowledge, skill | Personal | High (while relevant) |

**IIM Insight:** The most powerful managers combine multiple power bases. Relying on only coercive or legitimate power is fragile — as soon as you leave the role, the power disappears.

## 🔵 POWER TACTICS — HOW POWER IS EXERCISED

How do people actually USE power? Yukl & Falbe identified common influence tactics:

| Tactic | Description | Example |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| **Rational persuasion** | Use facts, data, logical arguments | "The data shows our current approach costs 30% more" |
| **Inspirational appeal** | Appeal to values and ideals | "This project will make a difference in millions of lives" |
| **Consultation** | Involve others in planning | "Help me design this new process" |
| **Ingratiation** | Flattery, making others feel good first | Compliment before asking for a favor |
| **Personal appeal** | Appeal to loyalty or friendship | "As my friend, can you help me with this?" |
| **Exchange** | Offer reciprocal benefits | "I'll cover your shift if you cover mine" |
| **Coalition building** | Get others to support your position | Forming a group of senior managers to support your proposal |
| **Legitimating** | Claim authority or precedent | "Per company policy, I have authority to do this" |
| **Pressure** | Threats, repeated demands | "If this isn't done by Friday, I'm escalating" |

**Soft Tactics** (Rational persuasion, Consultation, Inspirational appeal) → More effective long-term
**Hard Tactics** (Pressure, Coalition, Legitimating) → May work short-term but damage relationships

## 🔵 ORGANIZATIONAL POLITICS

### Definition:
Organizational Politics refers to behavior that is not formally sanctioned by the organization and is aimed at gaining or protecting power, influence, and resources to achieve one's own goals — often at the expense of organizational goals.

**Why Politics Exist:**
- Scarce resources create competition
- Ambiguity about who has authority
- Self-interest of individuals
- Performance metrics not perfectly aligned with organizational goals
- Tolerance of political behavior by leadership

### Is Politics Always Bad?

**Negative Politics:** Gossip, sabotage, backstabbing, taking credit for others' work → Destructive

**Positive Politics (Legitimate influence):** Building alliances to advance a good idea, using networks to get things done, strategically communicating achievements → Can be constructive

### Political Behaviors (Common in Organizations):

| Behavior | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Impression Management** | Making yourself look good — arriving early, associating with winners |
| **Information Control** | Sharing only what benefits you; withholding information from rivals |
| **Coalition Building** | Forming alliances of like-minded people for mutual support |
| **Blame Avoidance** | Distancing yourself from failures; finding scapegoats |
| **Networking** | Building relationships with powerful people across the organization |
| **Sponsorship Seeking** | Cultivating a powerful mentor/sponsor who will advocate for you |

### How to Navigate Organizational Politics (Without Becoming Toxic):

1. **Build genuine relationships** — Not transactional networking
2. **Understand the informal power map** — Who REALLY makes decisions?
3. **Deliver results consistently** — Performance is your best defense
4. **Develop visibility** — Let the right people know about your contributions
5. **Build a coalition for good ideas** — Use positive influence
6. **Stay ethical** — Never cross into deception or harm to others

**India Example — Narayana Murthy on Politics at Infosys:**
Infosys was deliberately designed with transparent processes, meritocracy, and performance-based reward to minimize destructive politics. Employee satisfaction consistently above industry average. Contrast: When "connections" replace merit in promotions (common in family-run companies), politics become destructive and talent leaves.

# CHAPTER 6: MANAGEMENT OF CREATIVITY AND INNOVATION

## 🟢 BEGINNER LEVEL — "What is this about?"

Why did Nokia fail even though they knew smartphones were coming? Why did Blockbuster decline when Netflix was available for them to buy? Why did Kodak invent the digital camera but never commercialize it?

**ANSWER: They couldn't innovate.** Their cultures, processes, and mindsets were trapped in the past.

Meanwhile, why does Google encourage employees to spend 20% of their time on personal projects? Why does 3M let employees experiment freely? Because they know **innovation requires creativity**, and creativity requires the right conditions.

This chapter is about: What is creativity? How does it happen? How do organizations foster or kill it? And what is the difference between creativity and innovation?

---

## 🔵 CREATIVITY vs. INNOVATION

| Aspect | Creativity | Innovation |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Generating new, useful ideas | Implementing new ideas to create value |
| **Nature** | Cognitive/mental process | Organizational/business process |
| **Output** | Novel idea or concept | New product, process, or service |
| **Analogy** | Inventing a new recipe | Opening the restaurant |
| **Risk** | Low (just thinking) | High (investment, execution) |

**Key Insight:** Creativity is a NECESSARY but NOT SUFFICIENT condition for innovation. You need both: ideas AND the ability to execute them.

---

## 🔵 THE CREATIVE PROCESS

**Wallas' 4-Stage Model (1926):**

```
Stage 1: PREPARATION
(Gather information; define problem; conscious work)
         ↓
Stage 2: INCUBATION
(Step away; unconscious mind works; "sleep on it")
         ↓
Stage 3: ILLUMINATION
("Eureka!" moment; sudden insight)
         ↓
Stage 4: VERIFICATION
(Test and refine the idea; make it work practically)
```

**Example:**
- Preparation: Dhirubhai Ambani studied textile imports/exports for years
- Incubation: Noticed patterns while traveling; pondering the problem
- Illumination: "India needs its own synthetic fiber industry — import substitution!"
- Verification: Built Reliance Textiles; tested the business model; refined it

## 🔵 TYPES OF INNOVATION

### By Degree of Change:

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Incremental Innovation** | Small improvements to existing things | iPhone adding a better camera |
| **Radical Innovation** | Completely new products/processes | Jio launching free 4G in India |
| **Disruptive Innovation** (Clayton Christensen) | Starts at low end; eventually displaces leaders | OTT platforms disrupting cable TV |
| **Architectural Innovation** | Uses existing technologies in new configurations | Tata Nano (reused components in ultra-low-cost architecture) |

### By Focus Area:

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Product Innovation** | New or improved products | Amul's new ice cream flavors |
| **Process Innovation** | Better ways of doing things | Lean manufacturing at Maruti |
| **Service Innovation** | New service delivery | Swiggy's Instamart (10-min delivery) |
| **Business Model Innovation** | New way of creating/capturing value | Jio — sell phones at cost; make money on data |

---

## 🔵 INDIVIDUAL CREATIVITY — WHAT MAKES PEOPLE CREATIVE?

**Amabile's Componential Theory (Teresa Amabile, Harvard):**

```
CREATIVITY = Domain Expertise × Creative Thinking Skills × Intrinsic Motivation
```

**1. Domain Expertise:**
- Deep knowledge of the relevant field
- Can't innovate in vacuum — need to understand the problem deeply
- Example: A. P. J. Abdul Kalam's missile expertise enabled creativity in India's space program

**2. Creative Thinking Skills:**
- Ability to think flexibly, make unexpected connections, tolerate ambiguity
- "Lateral Thinking" (Edward de Bono) — approach problems from unexpected angles
- Techniques: Brainstorming, SCAMPER, Mind Mapping, Reverse thinking

**3. Intrinsic Motivation (Most Important):**
- Doing the work because you find it interesting, enjoyable, and challenging
- Extrinsic motivation (just for money/reward) → Can actually KILL creativity
- Amabile's finding: Surveillance, deadlines, competitive evaluation → Reduce creativity
- Freedom, recognition, support → Enhance creativity

---

## 🔵 BARRIERS TO CREATIVITY IN ORGANIZATIONS

| Barrier | Example |
|---------|---------|
| **Fear of failure** | "If my idea fails, I'll be blamed" |
| **Conformity pressure** | "Don't rock the boat; that's not how we do things" |
| **Lack of time** | Too busy with daily work to think creatively |
| **Resource constraints** | "We don't have budget for experiments" |
| **Rigid structures** | Bureaucracy requiring 10 approvals for any new idea |
| **Negative evaluation** | "Ideas get mocked in meetings" |
| **Status quo bias** | "Why change what's working?" |

## 🔵 FOSTERING CREATIVITY AND INNOVATION IN ORGANIZATIONS

| Strategy | Description | Example |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| **Psychological Safety** | Create environment where risk is safe | Google's Project Aristotle finding: Teams that feel safe perform best |
| **Time for exploration** | Give employees unstructured time | Google: 20% time (Gmail, Google Maps born here) |
| **Diverse teams** | Different backgrounds = different perspectives | Zomato's multicultural product team |
| **Idea systems** | Formal channels to submit and evaluate ideas | Tata Group's InnoVent program |
| **Reward experimentation** | Celebrate learning from failure | Amazon's "Fail Fast, Learn Fast" culture |
| **Cross-functional collaboration** | Break silos | Apple's designers and engineers co-located |
| **Leadership modeling** | Leaders try new things; admit failure | Satya Nadella at Microsoft shifting from "know-it-all" to "learn-it-all" culture |

### Design Thinking (IDEO/Stanford):
A human-centered approach to innovation:
```
EMPATHIZE → DEFINE → IDEATE → PROTOTYPE → TEST → Iterate
```
- Start with deep understanding of user needs (not assumptions)
- India Application: Aravind Eye Care used design thinking to innovate cataract surgery processes → World's largest eye care operation serving millions of poor patients at near-zero cost

## 🔵 INNOVATIVE ORGANIZATIONS — CHARACTERISTICS

| Characteristic | Description |
|---------------|-------------|
| **Organic structure** | Flat hierarchy; loose, flexible; encourages experimentation |
| **High R&D investment** | Commitment to research and development |

- India Examples: Rahul Yadav's innovation at Quikr; Amul's digital team building end-to-end ordering systems

## 🔴 EXAM-READY CONTENT

### 5-Mark Answer: "Explain French & Raven's 5 Bases of Power"

**Ans:** French and Raven (1959) classified power into five types based on its source:

1. **Reward Power:** Ability to give rewards (salary increases, promotions). Effective but creates dependency. Example: Manager controlling team incentives.

2. **Coercive Power:** Ability to punish. Creates compliance but breeds resentment. Least effective long-term. Example: Threatening demotion for non-compliance.

3. **Legitimate Power:** Flows from formal organizational position. Only works within role boundaries. Example: CEO issuing directives to all departments.

4. **Referent Power:** Based on admiration and charisma. Most sustainable — people follow willingly. Example: Narayana Murthy inspiring generations of Infosys employees.

5. **Expert Power:** Based on superior knowledge or skills. Very effective in knowledge organizations. Example: Chief Data Scientist advising the board on AI strategy.

**Conclusion:** Effective managers combine multiple power bases. Over-reliance on coercive/legitimate power is fragile — it ends when the position ends.

### 5-Mark Answer: "What is creativity? How can organizations foster it?"

**Ans:** Creativity is the ability to generate novel and useful ideas. Teresa Amabile's Componential Theory identifies three components:
1. Domain expertise (deep knowledge)
2. Creative thinking skills (lateral thinking, flexibility)
3. Intrinsic motivation (most critical — doing work for love of it)

**Organizations can foster creativity by:**
- Creating **psychological safety** (safe to take risks and fail)
- Providing **time for exploration** (Google's 20% rule)
- Building **diverse teams** (different perspectives = more connections)
- Installing **idea systems** (formal channels to submit ideas)
- **Rewarding experimentation** (not punishing failures that generate learning)
- Leadership that **models creative behavior** (Microsoft's Satya Nadella)

**India Example:** Tata Group's InnoVent program invites employees across all companies to submit innovations. Winners get funding and mentorship. This signals: "Innovation is valued and rewarded here" — a critical cultural signal.

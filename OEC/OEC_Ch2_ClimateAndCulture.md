## 🟢 BEGINNER LEVEL — "What is this about?"

Have you ever walked into a restaurant and immediately felt the vibe? Some restaurants feel warm, relaxed, and friendly. Others feel cold, stiff, and formal — even before you see the menu.

Organizations are the same. Walk into a Google office and it feels open, casual, creative. Walk into a government office and it feels bureaucratic, hierarchical, slow. That **"feel"** of an organization is its **Climate**.

But where does that "feel" come from? It comes from deeper **values, beliefs, and assumptions** that people in that organization share over years — that's **Culture**.

**Simple Analogy:**
- **Culture** = The roots of a tree (deep, invisible, takes years to grow)
- **Climate** = The leaves of a tree (visible, changes with seasons/events)

---

## 🔵 ORGANIZATIONAL CLIMATE

### Definition:
Organizational Climate is the **shared perceptions** among employees about the work environment — how it feels to work here **right now**. It is the psychological atmosphere of the organization.

**Climate Dimensions (Litwin & Stringer):**
1. **Structure** — How rigid are the rules and procedures?
2. **Responsibility** — Do employees have freedom to make decisions?
3. **Reward** — Is performance recognized fairly?
4. **Risk** — Is innovation and risk-taking encouraged?
5. **Warmth** — Is there a sense of friendliness and belonging?
6. **Support** — Do colleagues and managers support each other?
7. **Standards** — How high are performance expectations?
8. **Conflict** — How is disagreement handled?
9. **Identity** — Do employees feel pride and loyalty?

**How Climate is Measured:** Employee surveys, interviews, observation

**India Example:**
- Infosys 2015-2017: Climate survey scores dropped (after founder Narayana Murthy's criticism of management went public). Employees felt uncertainty and low morale. Climate had turned negative even though the company was profitable.

## 🔵 ORGANIZATIONAL CULTURE

### Definition (Edgar Schein — Most Important):
Organizational Culture is the **pattern of basic assumptions** that a group has invented, discovered, or developed in learning to cope with its problems of external adaptation and internal integration, which has worked well enough to be considered valid, and therefore is taught to new members as the correct way to perceive, think, and feel.

**Simpler version:** Culture = "How we do things around here" — the shared values, beliefs, and behaviors that characterize an organization.

---

## 🔵 SCHEIN'S THREE-LEVEL MODEL OF CULTURE ⭐ Most Tested

Edgar Schein said culture exists at three levels, like an iceberg:

```
           ___________
          |           |    ← ARTIFACTS (Visible, on the surface)
          |___________|
               |
    ___________|___________
   |                       |  ← ESPOUSED VALUES (What we say we believe)
   |_______________________|
               |
  _____________|_____________
 |                           |  ← BASIC ASSUMPTIONS (Invisible, deep)
 |___________________________|
```

### Level 1 — ARTIFACTS (Visible but Hard to Interpret)
- What you SEE, HEAR, and FEEL when you enter an organization
- **Examples:**
  - Physical layout (open office vs. cabins)
  - Dress code (formals at HDFC Bank vs jeans at Swiggy)
  - How people address each other (Sir/Ma'am vs. first names)
  - Logos, slogans, ceremonies, rituals
  - Stories and myths about founders
- **Problem:** Easy to see, but hard to understand without knowing the deeper layers

### Level 2 — ESPOUSED VALUES (What We Say We Believe)
- Publicly stated values, ideals, strategies, and philosophies
- What appears in vision statements, annual reports, training manuals
- **Examples:**
  - Tata Group: "We do business with ethics" (espoused value)
  - Infosys: "Customer delight" as a core value
  - Wipro: "Spirit of Wipro" values booklet
- **Important:** Espoused values may or may NOT match actual behavior
  - A company says "we value work-life balance" but demands 12-hour days → GAP between espoused and actual

### Level 3 — BASIC UNDERLYING ASSUMPTIONS (Invisible, Deep)
- The deepest level — unconscious beliefs and perceptions
- "Taken for granted" — people don't even realize these beliefs exist
- These are the REAL culture — they drive actual behavior
- **Examples:**
  - "The boss is always right" (even if not stated anywhere)
  - "We never share bad news upward" (nobody wrote this rule — it just happens)
  - "Relationships matter more than merit in promotions" (often in family-run companies)
- **Hard to change** — requires major intervention to surface and shift

**Schein's Key Insight:** Most culture change efforts fail because they only address Artifacts (Level 1) without touching Basic Assumptions (Level 3).

## 🔵 HOFSTEDE'S CULTURAL DIMENSIONS ⭐ High Priority

Geert Hofstede studied IBM employees across 50+ countries and identified 5 (later 6) dimensions of national culture that affect organizational behavior.

### Dimension 1: Power Distance (PDI)
- **Definition:** The degree to which less powerful members accept and expect unequal power distribution
- **High PDI:** Hierarchy is accepted; bosses are rarely questioned; India, Malaysia, China
- **Low PDI:** Flat structures preferred; employees challenge bosses; Denmark, Netherlands, Sweden

**India:** HIGH Power Distance
- Employees say "Yes sir" even when they disagree
- Decisions flow top-down
- Seniority valued over performance in many firms

### Dimension 2: Individualism vs. Collectivism (IDV)
- **Individualism:** "I" culture — personal goals over group; USA, UK, Australia
- **Collectivism:** "We" culture — group loyalty; family/clan; India, China, Japan

**India:** Moderate-HIGH Collectivism
- Team loyalty, family obligations, nepotism in hiring
- Employees less likely to disagree publicly (don't want to shame the group)
- Startups changing this — younger generation more individualistic

### Dimension 3: Masculinity vs. Femininity (MAS)
- **Masculinity:** Values achievement, assertiveness, competition; Japan, Germany
- **Femininity:** Values cooperation, care, quality of life; Sweden, Norway

**India:** Moderate Masculinity
- Competitive work culture in IT, finance
- But also high value on relationships and harmony

### Dimension 4: Uncertainty Avoidance (UAI)
- **High UAI:** Discomfort with ambiguity; need for rules and structure; Greece, Japan
- **Low UAI:** Comfortable with ambiguity; innovative; Singapore, Sweden

**India:** Moderate-High Uncertainty Avoidance
- Indians prefer stable government jobs (certainty)
- But entrepreneurship is rising → shifting

### Dimension 5: Long-Term vs. Short-Term Orientation (LTO)
- **Long-term:** Perseverance, thrift, virtue; China, Japan
- **Short-term:** Respect for tradition, quick results; USA, UK

**India:** HIGH Long-Term Orientation
- Saving for future; delayed gratification valued in family culture
- "Jugaad" (frugal innovation) = making do with less, long-term thinking

### Hofstede's India Profile (Quick Summary):
| Dimension | India Score | Implication |
|-----------|------------|-------------|
| Power Distance | HIGH (77) | Accept hierarchy; boss-driven |
| Individualism | LOW-MODERATE (48) | Group loyalty matters |
| Masculinity | MODERATE (56) | Competitive but relationship-conscious |
| Uncertainty Avoidance | MODERATE (40) | Some tolerance for ambiguity |
| Long-Term Orientation | HIGH (51) | Future-focused, thrifty |

## 🔵 TYPES OF ORGANIZATIONAL CULTURE

### Deal & Kennedy's 4 Culture Types (Based on risk and feedback speed)

| Culture | Risk | Feedback | Example |
|---------|------|---------|---------|
| **Tough Guy/Macho** | High risk | Fast feedback | Stock traders, advertising, sports |
| **Work Hard/Play Hard** | Low risk | Fast feedback | Sales teams, FMCG companies (HUL) |
| **Bet Your Company** | High risk | Slow feedback | Oil exploration, aerospace, pharma R&D |
| **Process Culture** | Low risk | Slow feedback | Banks, government, insurance |

### Quinn's Competing Values Framework (CVF) ⭐

Based on two axes:
- Internal vs. External focus
- Flexibility vs. Control

```
         FLEXIBILITY
              |
CLAN      |   ADHOCRACY
(family)  |   (creative)
──────────┼──────────────
HIERARCHY |   MARKET
(control) |   (competitive)
              |
           CONTROL
```

| Culture Type | Focus | Characteristics | Indian Example |
|-------------|-------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Clan** | Internal + Flexible | Family-like, teamwork, employee development | Infosys early years (Murthy as patriarch) |
| **Adhocracy** | External + Flexible | Innovation, entrepreneurship, risk-taking | Startups: Zomato, BYJU'S early phase |
| **Market** | External + Control | Results-driven, competitive, customer focus | HDFC Bank (performance culture) |
| **Hierarchy** | Internal + Control | Rules, efficiency, processes | Government offices, PSU banks |

## 🔵 STRONG vs. WEAK CULTURE

| Aspect | Strong Culture | Weak Culture |
|--------|---------------|-------------|
| Shared values | Widely held; deeply believed | Not shared; vary by person |
| Consistency | Employees act consistently | Behavior varies widely |
| Employee commitment | High | Low |
| Direction | Clear | Unclear |
| Change difficulty | Harder to change | Easier to change |
| Example | Tata Group (ethics deeply embedded) | Post-merger company with two conflicting cultures |

**Strong culture advantages:**
- Less need for formal control systems (people "know" what to do)
- Faster decision-making (shared assumptions reduce debate)
- Higher employee engagement

**Strong culture risks:**
- Resistance to change (culture becomes inertia)
- Groupthink (no dissent allowed)
- Ethical blindspot (if culture normalizes wrong behavior — Enron, Satyam)

## 🔵 CULTURAL CHANGE — HOW TO CHANGE CULTURE

Changing culture is the hardest management challenge. Schein says it takes 5-10 years.

**Levers for Cultural Change:**

| Lever | How It Works |
|-------|-------------|
| **Leadership modeling** | If leaders live the new values, others follow |
| **Reward/recognition** | Reward behaviors aligned with new culture |
| **Hiring and selection** | Hire people who fit the new culture |
| **Rituals and ceremonies** | New symbols, stories, celebrations that reinforce new values |
| **Training and development** | Build skills and mindsets aligned with new culture |
| **Structure change** | New structure signals new priorities |
| **Stories and symbols** | Tell stories of heroes who embodied new values |

**India Case — Satyam Computers Culture Failure:**
- Satyam (2009 fraud scandal): Culture of fear and silence — no one questioned Ramalinga Raju's financial irregularities
- After acquisition by Tech Mahindra: Major culture change needed
  - New leadership modeling integrity
  - Whistleblower policy introduced
  - Open door culture for reporting concerns
  - New name/brand to signal fresh start
  - Result: Company recovered; now a stable Tech Mahindra business

## 🔵 ORGANIZATIONAL CLIMATE vs. CULTURE — KEY DIFFERENCES

| Aspect | Climate | Culture |
|--------|---------|---------|
| **Nature** | Perceptions (how it feels) | Values and assumptions |
| **Visibility** | More visible | Less visible (especially deeper layers) |
| **Changeability** | Can change relatively quickly | Very slow to change (years) |
| **Measurement** | Employee surveys | Ethnographic observation, interviews |
| **Focus** | Current atmosphere | Historical, deep-rooted patterns |
| **Analogy** | Weather (changes often) | Climate/Geography (stable over time) |

## 🔴 EXAM-READY CONTENT

### 5-Mark Answer: "Explain Schein's Three-Level Model of Culture"

**Ans:** Edgar Schein's Three-Level Model (1984) is the most comprehensive framework for understanding organizational culture. Schein compared culture to an iceberg — most of it is hidden beneath the surface.

**Level 1 — Artifacts:** The visible manifestations of culture — physical environment, dress code, language, rituals, ceremonies. Easy to observe but difficult to interpret without deeper knowledge.

**Level 2 — Espoused Values:** The organization's stated values, norms, and beliefs — mission statements, codes of conduct, official policies. These may or may not align with actual behavior.

**Level 3 — Basic Underlying Assumptions:** The deepest, unconscious beliefs that are "taken for granted" and drive actual behavior. These are invisible but most powerful.

**Key Insight:** Cultural change initiatives fail when they only address Level 1 (artifacts) without changing Level 3 (assumptions).

**Indian Example:** Many Indian IT companies state "we value work-life balance" (espoused value) but the basic assumption is "long hours show commitment" — employees work 12-hour days despite the stated value. Until the assumption changes, the culture won't change.

### 10-Mark Answer: "Explain Hofstede's Cultural Dimensions and their implications for Indian management"

**Introduction:**
Geert Hofstede's research on IBM employees across 50+ countries (1970s) identified cultural dimensions that explain variations in workplace behavior across nations. These dimensions are crucial for managing multicultural teams and cross-border operations.

**[Explain all 5 dimensions with India scores — as detailed above]**

**Implications for Indian Management:**

*1. High Power Distance → Management Style:*
- Top-down decision making is expected
- Participative management (involving subordinates in decisions) can feel uncomfortable initially
- MNC managers must adapt — pushing empowerment too fast creates confusion
- Indian example: Many MNCs (like P&G India) had to slow down "flat hierarchy" initiatives because Indian employees were uncomfortable with ambiguity about authority

*2. Collectivism → Team Dynamics:*
- Group credit is as important as individual recognition
- Public criticism of an individual is deeply uncomfortable (loss of face)
- Team rewards often more motivating than individual bonuses
- Indian example: HUL uses "best factory" competitions (team level) rather than individual awards

*3. Long-Term Orientation → Strategy:*
- Tata Group's willingness to invest in unprofitable divisions for 10-15 years before expecting returns
- Patience as a business virtue in Indian family businesses
- Contrasts with Western quarterly earnings pressure

**Conclusion:**
Hofstede's dimensions do not stereotype individuals but provide a statistical pattern of cultural tendencies. Managers must use these as starting points for understanding culture, not rigid rules, especially as India's culture is rapidly evolving with younger, globally-influenced professionals.

## 📌 CHAPTER 1: Organizational Change and Development (OCD)

### 1.1 What is Organizational Change?

**Definition:**
Organizational Change refers to the process by which an organization alters its structure, strategies, operational methods, technologies, culture, or the behavior of people within the organization to affect positive change and achieve organizational goals.

> *"Change is not merely necessary to life — it is life."* — Alvin Toffler

**Types of Organizational Change:**

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Planned Change** | Deliberate, intentional transformation | Infosys shifting to AI-first model |
| **Unplanned Change** | Reactive, forced by external factors | Companies shifting to WFH due to COVID |
| **Incremental Change** | Small, continuous improvements | Toyota's Kaizen philosophy |
| **Transformational Change** | Radical, organization-wide shift | Nokia's transition from rubber to telecom |
| **Structural Change** | Reorganization of hierarchy | Tata Group restructuring subsidiaries |
| **Technological Change** | Adoption of new technology | Banks implementing UPI systems |

---

### 1.2 Forces Driving Organizational Change

**Internal Forces:**
- Change in organizational strategy
- Change in workforce composition
- Employee performance gaps
- Change in leadership or management style

**External Forces:**
- **PEST Framework:** Political, Economic, Social, Technological
- Competition and market disruption
- Customer expectations
- Regulatory requirements

**Lewin's Force Field Analysis:**
Kurt Lewin proposed that change happens when **Driving Forces > Restraining Forces**.
- Driving Forces: push towards change (new technology, competitive pressure)
- Restraining Forces: resist change (employee fear, cost, inertia)

---

### 1.3 Models of Organizational Change

#### A. Lewin's 3-Step Change Model
```
UNFREEZE → CHANGE → REFREEZE
```
- **Unfreeze:** Break current mindset, create urgency (e.g., IBM realizing PC era was ending)
- **Change:** Implement new processes, train people
- **Refreeze:** Stabilize and institutionalize new behavior

#### B. Kotter's 8-Step Change Model
1. Create urgency
2. Build a guiding coalition
3. Form strategic vision
4. Enlist a volunteer army
5. Enable action by removing barriers
6. Generate short-term wins
7. Sustain acceleration
8. Institute change

#### C. McKinsey 7-S Framework
Seven interconnected elements: **Strategy, Structure, Systems, Shared Values, Style, Staff, Skills**

---

### 1.4 Organizational Development (OD)

**Definition:**
OD is a planned, systematic process using behavioral science knowledge and practices to help organizations improve their effectiveness and capacity for self-renewal.

**Key OD Interventions:**

| Intervention | Description |
|--------------|-------------|
| **Survey Feedback** | Collecting data, sharing with teams, planning change |
| **Team Building** | Improving group cohesion and effectiveness |
| **Process Consultation** | Consultant helps group improve processes |
| **Sensitivity Training** | T-Groups to improve self-awareness |
| **MBO** | Management by Objectives — goal alignment |

### CASE STUDY 1: Tata Motors and Organisational Change

**Background:** In 2008, Tata Motors acquired Jaguar Land Rover (JLR) — a British luxury car brand — from Ford for $2.3 billion. This was a massive cultural and operational shift for an Indian company.

**Challenge:** Integrating a British luxury brand with Indian manufacturing culture while retaining JLR's heritage and workforce confidence.

**Change Process Applied:**
- **Unfreeze:** Communicated new global vision; addressed employee fears about layoffs
- **Change:** Retained UK management; infused Indian engineering efficiency; invested in R&D
- **Refreeze:** JLR became profitable; revenues grew from £6B to £24B by 2019

**Lesson:** Successful change requires cultural sensitivity, leadership commitment, and a clear vision.

## 📌 CHAPTER 2: Organizational Climate and Culture

### 2.1 Organizational Culture — Definition

**Definition:**
Organizational Culture is the shared set of beliefs, values, norms, and assumptions that govern how people behave in an organization. It is "the way we do things around here."

> Edgar Schein defines culture as: *"A pattern of shared basic assumptions that the group learned as it solved its problems of external adaptation and internal integration."*

---

### 2.2 Levels of Organizational Culture (Schein's Model)

```
ARTIFACTS (Visible Layer)        ← Office design, dress code, rituals
ESPOUSED VALUES                  ← Mission statements, strategies
BASIC UNDERLYING ASSUMPTIONS     ← Unconscious beliefs (deepest layer)
```

---

### 2.3 Types of Organizational Culture

| Culture Type | Characteristics | Example |
|--------------|-----------------|---------|
| **Clan Culture** | Family-like, collaborative | Infosys early days |
| **Adhocracy Culture** | Innovative, entrepreneurial | Google, startups |
| **Market Culture** | Results-driven, competitive | Amazon, Goldman Sachs |
| **Hierarchy Culture** | Structured, process-oriented | Government departments, Indian Railways |

---

### 2.4 Organizational Climate

**Definition:**
Organizational Climate refers to the **perceptions** employees have about the work environment — how it "feels" to work in an organization. It is the **psychological atmosphere** of the workplace.

**Difference: Culture vs. Climate**

| Aspect | Culture | Climate |
|--------|---------|---------|
| Nature | Deep-rooted values & beliefs | Surface-level perceptions |
| Duration | Long-term, stable | Short-term, can shift |
| Measurement | Qualitative (ethnography) | Quantitative (surveys) |
| Change | Difficult to change | Relatively easier to change |

**Dimensions of Climate (Litwin & Stringer Model):**
Structure, Responsibility, Reward, Risk, Warmth, Support, Standards, Conflict, Identity

---

### CASE STUDY 2: Google's Culture of Innovation

**Background:** Google is consistently ranked as one of the best places to work globally. Its culture drives innovation.

**Key Cultural Elements:**
- **20% Time Policy:** Employees can spend 20% of work time on passion projects → Led to Gmail, Google Maps
- **Psychological Safety:** Employees encouraged to speak up without fear
- **Data-Driven Decisions:** People decisions backed by analytics (Project Oxygen)
- **Open Communication:** TGIF (Thank God It's Friday) — all-hands meetings with CEO

**Result:** Google generates over 70% of its revenue from products that didn't exist 5 years ago.

**Lesson:** Culture is a strategic asset. Innovation culture creates competitive advantage.

## 📌 CHAPTER 3: Management of Conflict and Negotiations

### 3.1 Conflict — Definition

**Definition:**
Conflict is a process that begins when one party perceives that another party has negatively affected, or is about to negatively affect, something the first party cares about.

**Types of Conflict:**

| Type | Description | Impact |
|------|-------------|--------|
| **Task Conflict** | Disagreement about work content | Can be positive (promotes debate) |
| **Relationship Conflict** | Interpersonal incompatibilities | Usually negative |
| **Process Conflict** | Disagreement about how work is done | Moderate — depends on context |

---

### 3.2 Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Model

**Five Conflict-Handling Styles:**

| Style | Description | Best Used When |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| **Competing** | Win-lose; aggressive | Emergency decisions |
| **Collaborating** | Win-win; problem-solving | Complex issues needing both sides |
| **Compromising** | Middle ground | Time-sensitive, equal power parties |
| **Avoiding** | Withdraw; delay | Trivial issues |
| **Accommodating** | Yield to other party | Preserving relationship matters more |

---

### 3.3 Negotiation

**Definition:**
Negotiation is a process of dialogue between two or more parties aimed at reaching a mutually acceptable agreement.

**Types:**
- **Distributive:** Win-lose; fixed pie; haggling (buying a car)
- **Integrative:** Win-win; expanding the pie; finding shared interests (labor-management talks)

**BATNA — Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement:**
The minimum acceptable outcome below which a party will walk away. Your BATNA is your power in negotiation.

**Harvard Negotiation Principles (Fisher & Ury):**
1. Separate people from the problem
2. Focus on interests, not positions
3. Invent options for mutual gain
4. Insist on objective criteria

---

### CASE STUDY 3: Maruti Suzuki Labor Dispute (Manesar, 2011-12)

**Background:** Workers at Maruti's Manesar plant demanded recognition of a new union. Conflict escalated over months.

**Events:**
- Workers went on strike; plant operations halted
- Violence erupted in July 2012; a manager was killed
- Factory shut for months, causing losses of Rs.700 crore+

**Resolution:**
- Company negotiated with state government involvement
- New wage settlements reached
- Stricter work codes implemented

**Lesson:** Unresolved conflict escalates. Early negotiation and recognizing worker grievances could have prevented losses.

## 📌 CHAPTER 4: Intergroup Behavior and Negotiation

### 4.1 Intergroup Behavior — Definition

**Definition:**
Intergroup behavior refers to the ways in which groups in an organization interact, compete, or cooperate with each other. It involves the dynamics between two or more groups and how they affect organizational functioning.

**Causes of Intergroup Conflict:**
- Resource scarcity — Competition for budget, talent
- Task interdependence — One group depends on another
- Goal incompatibility — Sales wants flexibility; finance wants control
- Status differences — Senior vs. junior departments

---

### 4.2 Key Theories

**A. Realistic Group Conflict Theory (Sherif):**
Groups compete when they have incompatible goals. Hostility reduces through superordinate goals (shared objectives).

**B. Social Identity Theory (Tajfel & Turner):**
People derive identity from group membership → In-group favoritism + Out-group discrimination.

**Implications for Management:**
- Avoid over-emphasizing departmental identity
- Create cross-functional teams
- Reward collaborative behavior

---

### 4.3 Managing Intergroup Conflict

| Strategy | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Superordinate Goals** | Create shared goals that transcend group boundaries |
| **Organizational Restructuring** | Merge conflicting departments |
| **Liaison Roles** | Appoint a coordinator between groups |
| **Inter-group Training** | Workshops, joint problem-solving |
| **Resource Expansion** | Reduce scarcity that causes conflict |

## 📌 CHAPTER 5: Power and Politics in Organizations

### 5.1 Power — Definition

**Definition:**
Power is the capacity that A has to influence the behavior of B so that B acts in accordance with A's wishes.

**French & Raven's Five Bases of Power:**

| Power Base | Source | Example |
|------------|--------|---------|
| **Legitimate** | Formal position/authority | CEO giving orders |
| **Reward** | Ability to give valued outcomes | Manager approving bonuses |
| **Coercive** | Ability to punish | Threatening termination |
| **Expert** | Special knowledge/skills | IT expert in boardroom |
| **Referent** | Personal charisma, admiration | Leaders inspiring followers |

---

### 5.2 Organizational Politics

**Definition:**
Organizational Politics refers to activities not required as part of one's formal role but that influence the distribution of advantages and disadvantages within the organization.

**Organizational Dependency Theory (Pfeffer):**
Power comes from controlling resources others depend on:
1. Resource must be **important** to the organization
2. The resource must be **scarce**
3. The resource must be **non-substitutable**

---

### CASE STUDY 4: Power Politics at Infosys (2017)

**Background:** Co-founder N.R. Narayana Murthy publicly criticized Infosys CEO Vishal Sikka over governance and salary issues.

**Political Dynamics:**
- Murthy used **Referent Power** (founder status) and **Legitimate Power** (as promoter)
- The board had **Expert Power** and **Legitimate Power**
- Battle was about control over strategic direction

**Outcome:** Sikka resigned; Nandan Nilekani came back as non-executive chairman to stabilize.

**Lesson:** Power struggles at top levels can destabilize organizations. Governance mechanisms must balance power.

## 📌 CHAPTER 6: Management of Creativity and Innovation

### 6.1 Creativity — Definition

**Definition:**
Creativity is the ability to generate novel, original, and useful ideas. In an organizational context, it is the capacity to develop new solutions to old problems or identify new opportunities.

**Components of Creativity (Amabile's Componential Model):**
1. Domain-relevant skills — Knowledge and technical proficiency
2. Creativity-relevant processes — Divergent thinking, risk tolerance
3. Intrinsic motivation — Internal passion for the task

---

### 6.2 Innovation — Types

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Product Innovation** | New or improved products | iPhone launch (2007) |
| **Process Innovation** | New or better ways of making things | Toyota Production System |
| **Business Model Innovation** | New revenue model | Netflix from DVDs to streaming |
| **Disruptive Innovation** | Creates new market, disrupts incumbents | Ola disrupted taxi industry |
| **Incremental Innovation** | Small improvements | Annual car model updates |

---

### 6.3 Design Thinking (IDEO Model)

```
EMPATHIZE → DEFINE → IDEATE → PROTOTYPE → TEST
```
A human-centered approach to innovation adopted by Apple, IDEO, and Airbnb.

---

### CASE STUDY 5: 3M and the Culture of Innovation

**Background:** 3M is one of the most innovative companies globally with over 60,000 products.

**Innovation Practices:**
- **15% Rule:** Employees spend 15% of work time on personal innovation projects
- **Genesis Grants:** Internal funding for promising ideas
- **Post-it Notes Story:** Spencer Silver's "failed" glue became Post-it Notes — a $1B+ product

**Result:** 3M generates 30% of revenue from products less than 5 years old.

**Lesson:** Structured freedom + failure tolerance = Sustained innovation engine.

## 📌 CHAPTER 7: Understanding Gender Issues in Management

### 7.1 Key Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Sex** | Biological classification (male/female) |
| **Gender** | Socially constructed roles, behaviors, and attributes |
| **Glass Ceiling** | Invisible barrier preventing women from reaching top positions |
| **Gender Pay Gap** | Difference in average earnings between men and women |
| **Gender Stereotyping** | Assuming traits based on gender |

---

### 7.2 Gender Gap — Indian Context

- Women constitute only ~17% of board directors in BSE 500 companies
- India ranks 127th out of 146 countries in Global Gender Gap Report 2023
- Gender pay gap in India: Women earn ~19% less than men for similar work

**Key Legislation in India:**
- **Maternity Benefit Act 2017:** 26 weeks maternity leave
- **POSH Act 2013:** Prevention of Sexual Harassment at workplace

---

### 7.3 Organizational Strategies for Gender Equity

1. Diversity & Inclusion (D&I) Programs
2. Mentoring and Sponsorship Programs
3. Flexible Work Arrangements
4. Equal Pay Audits
5. Anti-Harassment Policies

## 📌 CHAPTER 8: Cross-Cultural Management and Learning Organization

### 8.1 Cross-Cultural Management — Definition

**Definition:**
Cross-Cultural Management involves understanding how cultural differences across countries and ethnic groups affect behavior in organizations, and using that knowledge to manage effectively.

**Hofstede's Cultural Dimensions:**

| Dimension | High | Low | India |
|-----------|------|-----|-------|
| **Power Distance** | Hierarchy accepted | Equality preferred | High (77) |
| **Individualism** | Personal goals first | Group goals first | Collectivist (48) |
| **Masculinity** | Achievement, assertiveness | Cooperation | Moderate (56) |
| **Uncertainty Avoidance** | Low tolerance for ambiguity | High tolerance | Low (40) |
| **Long-Term Orientation** | Future-oriented | Present-focused | High (51) |

---

### 8.2 Learning Organization (Peter Senge)

**Definition:**
A Learning Organization continually expands its capacity to create its future by fostering continuous learning, adaptation, and transformation at all levels.

**Senge's Five Disciplines:**

| Discipline | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| **Personal Mastery** | Individual commitment to learning and growth |
| **Mental Models** | Challenging assumptions and beliefs |
| **Shared Vision** | Common picture of the future |
| **Team Learning** | Collective intelligence > individual intelligence |
| **Systems Thinking** | Understanding the whole, not just the parts |

---

### 8.3 Knowledge Management (Nonaka & Takeuchi)

**Types of Knowledge:**
- **Explicit Knowledge:** Documented, codifiable (manuals, databases)
- **Tacit Knowledge:** Implicit, experience-based, hard to transfer (expertise, intuition)

**SECI Model:**
```
Socialization → Externalization → Combination → Internalization
(Tacit→Tacit)   (Tacit→Explicit) (Explicit→Explicit) (Explicit→Tacit)
```

---

### CASE STUDY 7: Wipro — Cross-Cultural Management in Global Delivery

**Background:** Wipro operates in 60+ countries with diverse employee base.

**Cross-Cultural Challenges:**
- Communication differences between Indian and European clients
- Different work ethics and deadline perceptions
- Holiday/leave policy conflicts

**Wipro's Approach:**
- Cultural Sensitivity Training for all offshore-onshore teams
- Country-specific guidelines in project management handbooks
- Buddy systems pairing Indian and client-side employees
- Global Leadership Program to develop culturally agile leaders

**Result:** Wipro maintains client retention of 90%+ despite cultural complexity.

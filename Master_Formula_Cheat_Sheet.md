### 1. CORPORATE FINANCE (CF)

| Formula Name | Plain English Meaning | The Simple Formula | Real-World Example / Rule |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Future Value (FV)** | How much will my money grow to in the future? | **PV * (1 + r)^n** <br>*(Present Value multiplied by interest rate over n years)* | You invest ₹100 today at 10% for 2 years. <br>FV = 100 * (1.10) * (1.10) = **₹121** |
| **Present Value (PV)** | If someone promises me money in the future, what is it worth *today*? | **FV / (1 + r)^n** <br>*(Future Value divided by interest rate over n years)* | A promise to pay you ₹121 two years from now (at 10% interest) is only worth **₹100** today. |
| **Net Present Value (NPV)** | Are we making a real profit on this long-term project? | **(Total Present Value of Cash Inflows) MINUS (Initial Investment)** | **THE GOLDEN RULE:** If NPV is positive (+), ACCEPT the project. If negative (-), REJECT it. |
| **Profitability Index (PI)** | How much value is created for every ₹1 invested? | **(Total Present Value of Cash Inflows) / (Initial Investment)** | If PI > 1, the project is profitable. If PI < 1, reject it. |
| **Payback Period** | How many years does it take to get my initial investment back? | **Initial Investment / Annual Cash Flow** | You spend ₹50,000 on a machine. It earns ₹10,000 every year. Payback = **5 Years**. |
| **Cost of Equity (CAPM)** | What return do shareholders demand for the risk they take? | **Risk-Free Rate + Beta * (Market Return - Risk-Free Rate)** | If Govt bonds pay 5% (risk-free), but your stock is highly volatile (Beta = 1.5), shareholders will demand a much higher return. |
| **WACC (Weighted Avg. Cost of Capital)** | The average interest rate a company pays to all its investors (both debt and equity). | **(Weight of Equity * Cost of Equity) + (Weight of Debt * Cost of Debt)** | A company is funded 60% by equity and 40% by debt. You calculate the combined average cost to see if a new project can cover it. |
| **Operating Leverage (DOL)** | How much do my profits jump if my sales jump? | **Contribution / EBIT** | Companies with massive Fixed Costs (like airlines) have High Operating Leverage. |
| **Financial Leverage (DFL)** | How much does taking a loan (debt) amplify my financial risk? | **EBIT / EBT** | High debt = High interest payments = High Financial Leverage. |

### 2. MANAGEMENT ACCOUNTING (MA)

| Formula Name | Plain English Meaning | The Simple Formula | Real-World Example / Rule |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Contribution Margin** | Money left over after making the product to cover your rent/fixed costs. | **Sales Price - Variable Costs** | You sell a shirt for ₹500. It costs ₹300 to make. Your Contribution is **₹200**. |
| **Break-Even Point (Units)** | Exactly how many items do I need to sell so I make zero profit and zero loss? | **Fixed Costs / Contribution per Unit** | Your shop rent is ₹10,000. You make ₹100 contribution per shirt. Break-Even = **100 shirts**. |
| **Margin of Safety** | How much can my sales drop before I actually start losing money? | **Actual Sales - Break-Even Sales** | This is your "buffer zone" to survive a bad business month. |
| **Material Price Variance** | Did we pay more for raw materials than we originally planned? | **Actual Qty * (Standard Price - Actual Price)** | We planned to pay ₹10/kg, but paid ₹12/kg. This is an **Adverse (Bad) Variance**. |
| **Labor Efficiency Variance** | Did our workers take more time to finish the job than we planned? | **Standard Rate * (Standard Hours - Actual Hours)** | Planned 5 hours, but workers took 7 hours. This costs the company money (Adverse Variance). |

### 3. PRODUCTION & OPERATIONS MGT. (POM) & DMO

| Formula Name | Plain English Meaning | The Simple Formula | Real-World Example / Rule |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Economic Order Qty (EOQ)** | The "perfect" batch size to order so you don't spend too much on storage. | **Square Root of: (2 * Demand * Ordering Cost) / Holding Cost** | This formula perfectly balances the cost of ordering goods vs the cost of storing them in a warehouse. |
| **Reorder Point (ROP)** | When exactly should I call the supplier to bring more stock? | **Average Daily Demand * Lead Time (in days)** | You sell 5 phones a day. Supplier takes 3 days to deliver. Reorder when you hit **15 phones** left in stock. |
| **Utilization Factor** | Queuing Theory: How busy is the server/cashier? | **Arrival Rate / Service Rate** | 5 customers arrive per hour. Cashier can serve 10 per hour. Utilization = 5/10 = **50% Busy**. |
| **Expected Monetary Value (EMV)** | A weighted average of all possible financial outcomes in a decision. | **Payoff * Probability (%)** | 50% chance to win ₹1,000. 50% chance to win ₹0. <br>EMV = (0.50*1000) + (0.50*0) = **₹500**. |
| **PERT Expected Time** | How long a project will take considering best, worst, and normal scenarios. | **(Optimistic + 4*Most Likely + Pessimistic) / 6** | A task usually takes 5 days, but could take 3 (best) or 9 (worst). Expected Time = (3 + 20 + 9) / 6 = 5.33 days. |

### 4. ECONOMIC ENVIRONMENT OF BUSINESS (EEB)

| Formula Name | Plain English Meaning | The Simple Formula | Real-World Example / Rule |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Investment Multiplier** | If the government injects cash into the economy, how much total wealth is generated? | **1 / (1 - MPC)** <br>*(MPC = Marginal Propensity to Consume)* | If govt spends ₹100Cr, and people spend 80% of what they earn, that money changes hands repeatedly, creating massive total wealth. |
| **GDP (Expenditure)** | The total value of everything produced in the country. | **Consumption + Investment + Govt Spending + (Exports - Imports)** | This is the standard way a country's economic size is measured globally. |

## 📌 CHAPTER 1: Introduction to Macroeconomics

### 1.1 What is Macroeconomics?

**Definition:**
Macroeconomics is the branch of economics that studies the behavior and performance of an economy as a whole — including aggregate output, employment, price levels, growth, and the interrelationships among these variables.

> *"Macroeconomics is the study of the economy in the large — its total output, employment, price level, and growth."* — Paul Samuelson

**Difference: Micro vs Macro Economics**

| Aspect | Microeconomics | Macroeconomics |
|--------|---------------|----------------|
| Focus | Individual firms, consumers | Entire economy |
| Variables | Price of a product, firm output | GDP, inflation, unemployment |
| Policy | Competition law, price regulation | Fiscal policy, monetary policy |
| Example | Why onion prices rise | Why India's inflation is 5% |

---

### 1.2 Key Macroeconomic Variables

| Variable | Definition | India (2024 approx.) |
|----------|------------|---------------------|
| **GDP** | Total value of goods & services produced | $3.7 trillion (5th largest) |
| **Inflation** | Rate of rise in general price level | ~5% (CPI) |
| **Unemployment** | % of labour force without work | ~7-8% |
| **Interest Rate** | Cost of borrowing money | Repo Rate: 6.5% (RBI) |
| **Exchange Rate** | Price of domestic currency in foreign currency | ~₹83/USD |
| **Current Account** | Net trade in goods, services, income | Deficit ~$35 billion |

---

### 1.3 Circular Flow of Income

The economy is a circular system:
```
HOUSEHOLDS ─── Labour, Capital ───► FIRMS
HOUSEHOLDS ◄── Wages, Profit ────── FIRMS
```

**Two-Sector:** Households + Firms
**Three-Sector:** + Government (taxes, spending)
**Four-Sector:** + Foreign sector (exports, imports)

**Injections vs Leakages:**
- **Injections (add to flow):** Investment (I), Government Spending (G), Exports (X)
- **Leakages (withdraw from flow):** Savings (S), Taxes (T), Imports (M)
- **Equilibrium:** Total Injections = Total Leakages → I + G + X = S + T + M

---

### 1.4 Macroeconomic Goals

1. **Economic Growth** — Rising real GDP
2. **Full Employment** — Low unemployment (natural rate ~4-5%)
3. **Price Stability** — Low, stable inflation (RBI target: 4% ±2%)
4. **Balance of Payments Equilibrium** — Sustainable external accounts
5. **Equitable Distribution** — Reducing income inequality

## 📌 CHAPTER 2: National Accounts and Problems of GDP Measurement

### 2.1 GDP — Definition

**Definition:**
Gross Domestic Product (GDP) is the total monetary value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders in a given period (usually one year).

**Three Approaches to Measure GDP:**

**1. Expenditure Approach (Most Common):**
```
GDP = C + I + G + (X - M)
```
- **C** = Private Consumption (household spending)
- **I** = Gross Investment (business capital spending)
- **G** = Government Expenditure
- **X - M** = Net Exports (Exports minus Imports)

**2. Income Approach:**
```
GDP = Wages + Rent + Interest + Profit + Net Indirect Taxes
```

**3. Production/Value Added Approach:**
```
GDP = Σ Value Added at each stage of production
Value Added = Output value - Input cost
```

---

### 2.2 GDP vs GNP vs NNP

| Concept | Formula | What it Measures |
|---------|---------|-----------------|
| **GDP** | All production within borders | Domestic economic activity |
| **GNP** | GDP + Net Factor Income from Abroad | Output of nationals (home + abroad) |
| **NNP** | GNP - Depreciation | Net output after asset wear |
| **NNP at Factor Cost** | NNP - Indirect Taxes + Subsidies | National Income |
| **Per Capita Income** | National Income / Population | Average income per person |

---

### 2.3 Real GDP vs Nominal GDP

- **Nominal GDP:** Measured at current prices (includes inflation effect)
- **Real GDP:** Measured at base year prices (removes inflation effect — true growth)
- **GDP Deflator** = (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) × 100

**Example:**
- 2023: Nominal GDP = ₹300 trillion, Base year prices show ₹280 trillion
- Real GDP = ₹280 trillion; GDP Deflator = (300/280) × 100 = 107.1 → Inflation ~7.1%

---

### 2.4 Problems in GDP Measurement

| Problem | Description | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Black Economy** | Unreported/illegal transactions | India's cash economy (estimated 20-25% of GDP) |
| **Household Work** | Unpaid domestic work not counted | Indian women's unpaid labour |
| **Environmental Degradation** | GDP ignores environmental damage | Ganga pollution caused by industrial growth |
| **Income Distribution** | GDP doesn't show inequality | India's GDP grows but Gini coefficient worsens |
| **Quality of Life** | High GDP ≠ high well-being | USA high GDP but high inequality |
| **Underground Economy** | Barter, informal sector not captured | India's agricultural barter |

**Alternatives to GDP:**
- **HDI (Human Development Index):** GDP + Health + Education (UNDP)
- **GNH (Gross National Happiness):** Bhutan's holistic measure
- **Green GDP:** GDP adjusted for environmental costs

---

### 2.5 Case Study — India's GDP Measurement Reform

**Background:** India revised its GDP methodology in 2015, shifting base year from 2004-05 to 2011-12 and adopting MCA21 database for corporate sector data.

**Key Change:** Shifted from factor cost to market prices for GDP measurement (aligned with international standards).

**Controversy:** New methodology showed higher GDP growth rates, raising questions about accuracy. The National Statistical Office (NSO) and independent economists continue to debate the reliability of India's back-series data.

**Lesson:** GDP measurement is not just economics — it is politics. The method chosen affects policy decisions, credit ratings, and international perception.

## 📌 CHAPTER 3: Economic Growth

### 3.1 What is Economic Growth?

**Definition:**
Economic Growth refers to the increase in a country's productive capacity over time, measured as the percentage increase in real GDP.

**Types:**
- **Actual Growth:** Increase in current GDP
- **Potential Growth:** Increase in an economy's productive capacity (maximum sustainable output)
- **Inclusive Growth:** Growth that is equitable and benefits all sections

---

### 3.2 Sources of Economic Growth

| Source | Description | India Example |
|--------|-------------|---------------|
| **Capital Accumulation** | More machines, infrastructure | National Infrastructure Pipeline (NIP) — ₹111 lakh crore |
| **Labour Force Growth** | More workers | India's demographic dividend (65% population below 35) |
| **Technological Progress** | Better production methods | UPI, Digital India, IT sector |
| **Human Capital** | Education, health of workforce | PM Kaushal Vikas Yojana |
| **Natural Resources** | Land, minerals | India's coal, iron ore |
| **Institutional Quality** | Property rights, rule of law | GST reform, IBC (Insolvency Code) |

---

### 3.3 Growth Theories

**1. Classical Growth Theory (Adam Smith, Ricardo):**
- Growth driven by capital accumulation and division of labour
- Diminishing returns eventually leads to a stationary state

**2. Harrod-Domar Model:**
```
Growth Rate (g) = Savings Rate (s) / Capital-Output Ratio (k)
g = s/k
```
- Higher savings → Higher investment → Higher growth
- India's challenge: Low savings in informal sector limits growth

**3. Solow Growth Model (Neo-Classical):**
- Growth driven by: Capital, Labour, and Technology (TFP)
- **Key insight:** In long run, only technological progress drives sustained growth
- Diminishing returns to capital → Countries converge in income (Convergence Hypothesis)

```
Y = f(K, L, A)
where A = Total Factor Productivity (TFP)
```

**4. Endogenous Growth Theory (Romer, Lucas):**
- Knowledge and human capital are endogenous (produced within the economy)
- No diminishing returns to knowledge → Sustained growth possible
- Policy matters: R&D investment, education spending sustain growth

---

### 3.4 India's Growth Story

| Period | GDP Growth | Key Driver |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| 1947-1980 | ~3.5% ("Hindu Rate of Growth") | Public sector, license raj |
| 1980-1991 | ~5.5% | Partial reforms, Green Revolution |
| 1991-2003 | ~6% | LPG Reforms (Liberalisation, Privatisation, Globalisation) |
| 2003-2011 | ~8% | IT boom, global integration, infrastructure |
| 2016-2019 | ~7% | GST, demonetisation disruption |
| 2021-2024 | ~7-8% | Post-COVID recovery, digital economy |

**India's Growth Challenges:**
- Jobless growth — GDP grows but employment doesn't keep pace
- Agricultural sector stagnation
- Infrastructure bottlenecks
- Skill mismatch

## 📌 CHAPTER 4: The IS-LM Model

### 4.1 Introduction

The IS-LM model (Hicks-Hansen Model, 1937) is the cornerstone of short-run macroeconomic analysis. It shows the simultaneous equilibrium of:
- **Goods market** (IS curve)
- **Money market** (LM curve)

---

### 4.2 IS Curve (Investment-Savings)

**Definition:**
The IS curve shows all combinations of interest rate (r) and output (Y) at which the **goods market** is in equilibrium (Planned Investment = Planned Savings).

**Derivation Logic:**
- Higher interest rate (r↑) → Investment falls (I↓) → Aggregate demand falls → Output falls (Y↓)
- Therefore: IS curve **slopes downward** (negative relationship between r and Y)

**IS Curve Equation:**
```
Y = C + I(r) + G
or equivalently: S(Y) + T = I(r) + G
```

**Shifts in IS Curve:**
- **Rightward shift:** Increase in G, decrease in T, increase in autonomous investment
- **Leftward shift:** Decrease in G, increase in T, decrease in confidence (animal spirits)

---

### 4.3 LM Curve (Liquidity-Money)

**Definition:**
The LM curve shows all combinations of interest rate (r) and output (Y) at which the **money market** is in equilibrium (Money Supply = Money Demand).

**Derivation Logic:**
- Higher output (Y↑) → Transactions demand for money rises → At fixed money supply, interest rate must rise to choke off excess money demand
- Therefore: LM curve **slopes upward** (positive relationship between r and Y)

**Money Market Equilibrium:**
```
M/P = L(r, Y)    (Real money supply = Real money demand)
```

**Shifts in LM Curve:**
- **Rightward shift:** Increase in money supply (M↑) or decrease in price level (P↓)
- **Leftward shift:** Decrease in money supply, increase in prices

---

### 4.4 IS-LM Equilibrium

The intersection of IS and LM curves determines the **equilibrium interest rate (r*)** and **equilibrium output (Y*)** simultaneously.

```
       r
       │   IS        LM
       │  \         /
    r* │   \   *   /
       │    \ ↑  /
       │     \  /
       └──────────────── Y
              Y*
```

---

### 4.5 Policy Effects in IS-LM

**Fiscal Policy (Government changes G or T):**
- Increase in G → IS shifts right → Y rises, r rises
- **Crowding Out:** Higher G raises r → Private investment falls → Effect on Y is less than expected
- Full Crowding Out: LM curve vertical (Classical) → Fiscal policy ineffective

**Monetary Policy (Central bank changes M):**
- Increase in M → LM shifts right → Y rises, r falls
- **Liquidity Trap:** LM curve horizontal (Keynesian extreme) → Monetary policy ineffective
- Japan's lost decade (1990s) and near-zero interest rates → Classic liquidity trap

---

### 4.6 Case Study — India's COVID-19 Fiscal Response

**Background:** COVID-19 (2020) caused a massive negative shock to Indian economy — GDP contracted -7.3% in FY21.

**IS-LM Analysis:**
- Shock: IS curve shifted massively LEFT (consumption, investment crashed)
- RBI Response (LM shift): Cut Repo Rate from 5.15% to 4% → LM shifts RIGHT
- Government Response (IS shift): Rs.29.87 lakh crore stimulus (PM Garib Kalyan, PLI schemes) → IS shifts RIGHT

**Result:** India recovered with 8.9% growth in FY22, fastest large economy recovery globally.

**Lesson:** Simultaneous fiscal + monetary stimulus (coordinated IS+LM shifts) is most effective in a severe recession.

## 📌 CHAPTER 5: AD/AS Framework

### 5.1 Aggregate Demand (AD)

**Definition:**
Aggregate Demand is the total demand for all goods and services in an economy at a given price level.

```
AD = C + I + G + (X - M)
```

**AD Curve:** Slopes **downward** (higher price level → lower real output demanded)

**Why AD slopes down? (Three Effects):**
1. **Wealth Effect:** Higher prices → Real wealth falls → Consumption falls
2. **Interest Rate Effect:** Higher prices → Higher money demand → Higher interest rates → Lower investment
3. **Exchange Rate Effect:** Higher prices → Currency appreciates → Exports fall, Imports rise → Net exports fall

**AD Curve Shifts (Rightward):**
- Higher G, lower T, higher consumer confidence, higher exports, lower interest rates

---

### 5.2 Aggregate Supply (AS)

**Short-Run AS (SRAS):** Slopes **upward** — higher prices → higher output (firms earn more profit)

**Long-Run AS (LRAS):** Perfectly **vertical** at potential output (Y*) — in long run, output is determined by productive capacity, not price level

**SRAS Shifts:**
- Input price changes (oil price shock → SRAS shifts left)
- Technology improvement → SRAS shifts right
- Supply chain disruptions (COVID) → SRAS shifts left

---

### 5.3 AD-AS Equilibrium and Business Cycle

```
Short-Run Equilibrium: SRAS intersects AD
Long-Run Equilibrium: LRAS intersects AD (at Y*)
```

**Demand-Pull Inflation:** AD shifts right → Price level and output both rise
- Example: India's COVID recovery stimulus causing inflation (2022-23)

**Cost-Push Inflation:** SRAS shifts left → Price level rises, output falls (Stagflation)
- Example: 1973 Oil Shock → Global stagflation

**Recessionary Gap:** Y < Y* → Economy below potential → Policy response: stimulate AD

**Inflationary Gap:** Y > Y* → Economy overheating → Policy response: contract AD

---

### 5.4 Case Study — India's Stagflation Risk (2022)

**Background:** Post-COVID, Russia-Ukraine war pushed global oil and commodity prices sharply higher.

**AD-AS Analysis:**
- Oil price rise → SRAS shifted LEFT (cost-push pressure)
- Meanwhile, post-COVID recovery → AD shifting RIGHT
- Combined effect: Sharp rise in inflation (CPI touched 7.8% in April 2022 — above RBI's 6% upper limit)

**RBI's Response:**
- Raised Repo Rate from 4% to 6.5% in 10 months (2022-23) — steepest hike cycle
- Effect: AD contracted (interest rate↑ → I↓, consumption↓)
- Inflation cooled to ~5% by 2023 but growth also moderated

**Lesson:** Supply shocks create a dilemma — if you fix inflation (contract AD), growth suffers; if you support growth (expand AD), inflation worsens. This is the stagflation trap.

## 📌 CHAPTER 6: Phillips Curve, Monetary Policy & Fiscal Policy

### 6.1 The Phillips Curve

**Definition:**
The Phillips Curve shows the short-run inverse relationship between the rate of unemployment and the rate of inflation in an economy.

**Original Phillips Curve (A.W. Phillips, 1958):**
- Based on UK data 1861-1957
- Finding: When unemployment is low, wages and prices rise faster → Higher inflation
- Implication: Policymakers can choose between inflation and unemployment

```
Inflation (π)
     │
  7% │  *
  5% │    *
  3% │       *
  1% │           *
     └──────────────── Unemployment (u)
        3%  5%  7%  9%
```

**Trade-off:** Move left on the curve (lower unemployment) → Move up (higher inflation)

---

### 6.2 Breakdown of Phillips Curve — Stagflation (1970s)

**Natural Rate of Unemployment (Milton Friedman):**
- The economy has a Natural Rate of Unemployment (NRU) or NAIRU (Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of Unemployment) below which inflation accelerates
- Long-run Phillips Curve is **vertical** at NRU → No long-run trade-off!

**Expectations-Augmented Phillips Curve:**
```
π = πᵉ - β(u - u*)
```
Where: π = actual inflation, πᵉ = expected inflation, u = unemployment, u* = NRU

**If actual unemployment = NRU:** π = πᵉ (no surprise inflation)
**If u < u*:** π > πᵉ (inflation exceeds expectations → accelerates)

---

### 6.3 Monetary Policy

**Definition:**
Monetary Policy refers to actions taken by the central bank (RBI in India) to control the money supply and interest rates to achieve macroeconomic objectives.

**RBI's Monetary Policy Tools:**

| Tool | Description | Current Value (2024) |
|------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Repo Rate** | Rate at which RBI lends to banks | 6.5% |
| **Reverse Repo Rate** | Rate at which RBI borrows from banks | 3.35% |
| **CRR** | % of deposits banks must keep with RBI (cash) | 4% |
| **SLR** | % of deposits banks must keep in liquid assets | 18% |
| **Open Market Operations** | RBI buys/sells government securities | Active |
| **MSF (Marginal Standing Facility)** | Emergency borrowing rate for banks | 6.75% |

**Expansionary Monetary Policy:**
- Cut Repo Rate → Banks borrow cheaply → Lend more → Investment↑ → GDP↑
- Used during recession (COVID 2020: Repo cut to 4%)

**Contractionary Monetary Policy:**
- Raise Repo Rate → Borrowing expensive → Investment↓ → Inflation cools
- Used during high inflation (2022-23: Repo raised to 6.5%)

**Monetary Policy Committee (MPC):**
- 6-member committee (3 RBI officials + 3 external experts)
- Meets every 2 months; decides Repo Rate by majority vote
- Inflation target: 4% (±2% band) — legally mandated

---

### 6.4 Fiscal Policy

**Definition:**
Fiscal Policy refers to government's use of taxation and public expenditure to influence the economy.

**Types:**
- **Expansionary:** Increase G, decrease T → Boost aggregate demand
- **Contractionary:** Decrease G, increase T → Cool overheating economy

**India's Fiscal Framework:**
- **FRBM Act (Fiscal Responsibility and Budget Management Act, 2003):** Limits fiscal deficit to 3% of GDP
- **Fiscal Deficit = Government Expenditure - Government Revenue** (total)
- **Revenue Deficit** = Revenue Expenditure - Revenue Receipts

**India's Fiscal Deficit (2024-25 Budget):** 5.1% of GDP (still above FRBM target due to COVID spending and welfare commitments)

---

### 6.5 Effectiveness Comparison

| Policy | Effective When | Ineffective When |
|--------|---------------|-----------------|
| Fiscal Policy | Unemployment, recession | High crowding out (high interest rates) |
| Monetary Policy | Inflation control | Liquidity trap (near-zero rates) |
| Combined | Severe recession (like COVID) | Conflicting objectives |

## 📌 CHAPTER 7: Theories of Trade and Balance of Payments

### 7.1 Why Nations Trade?

**Absolute Advantage (Adam Smith):**
A country has an absolute advantage if it can produce a good using fewer resources than another country.

**Comparative Advantage (David Ricardo):**
A country should specialize in producing the good in which it has the **lowest opportunity cost**, even if it has absolute advantage in everything.

**Example:**
| Country | Cloth (hrs) | Wine (hrs) |
|---------|------------|-----------|
| England | 100 | 120 |
| Portugal | 90 | 80 |

Portugal has absolute advantage in both. But Portugal's comparative advantage is in Wine (80 vs 100 hrs opportunity cost). England's comparative advantage is in Cloth. → Both gain from trade by specializing!

---

### 7.2 Modern Trade Theories

**Heckscher-Ohlin (H-O) Theory:**
Countries export goods that use their **abundant factors** intensively.
- Labour-abundant countries (India) → Export labour-intensive goods (textiles, software services)
- Capital-abundant countries (USA) → Export capital-intensive goods (aircraft, machinery)

**Leontief Paradox:** USA (capital-abundant) actually exported labour-intensive goods — contradicted H-O theory.

**New Trade Theory (Paul Krugman, Nobel 2008):**
- Economies of scale and first-mover advantages → Countries specialize in products regardless of resource endowments
- Explains why similar countries (USA, Germany) trade similar goods (cars for cars)

---

### 7.3 Balance of Payments (BoP)

**Definition:**
Balance of Payments is a systematic record of all economic transactions between residents of a country and the rest of the world during a given period.

**Structure of BoP:**

**1. Current Account:**
- Trade balance (goods): Exports - Imports
- Invisibles: Services, remittances, income
- **India 2024:** Current Account Deficit (CAD) ~ $35-40 billion (mainly oil imports)

**2. Capital Account:**
- FDI (Foreign Direct Investment)
- FPI (Foreign Portfolio Investment)
- External Commercial Borrowings (ECBs)
- **India 2024:** Net capital inflow ~ $50 billion (FDI + FPI)

**3. Reserve Account:**
- Change in foreign exchange reserves
- **India's Forex Reserves (2024):** ~$640 billion (4th largest globally)

**Overall BoP = Current Account + Capital Account + Reserve Account = 0** (always balances)

---

### 7.4 India's Balance of Payments — Key Features

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Largest Import** | Crude Oil (~$200 billion/year) |
| **Largest Export** | Software Services (~$250 billion) |
| **Remittances** | ~$120 billion/year (world's largest receiver) |
| **FDI Inflow** | ~$60 billion/year |
| **Trade Deficit** | ~$240 billion (goods) |
| **Services Surplus** | ~$140 billion |

## 📌 CHAPTER 8: Exchange Rates and the Mundell-Fleming Model

### 8.1 Exchange Rate Systems

| System | Description | Example |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| **Fixed** | Rate pegged to another currency/gold | China (managed peg to USD) |
| **Floating** | Rate determined by market demand/supply | India (managed float) |
| **Managed Float** | Central bank intervenes to limit volatility | India, most emerging markets |

**Factors Determining Exchange Rate:**
1. Inflation differentials (PPP theory)
2. Interest rate differentials (Interest Rate Parity)
3. Trade balance (current account)
4. Capital flows
5. Speculation
6. Central bank intervention

---

### 8.2 Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)

**Definition:**
PPP theory states that exchange rates adjust so that identical goods cost the same in different countries when expressed in the same currency.

**PPP Formula:**
```
e = P_domestic / P_foreign
```

**The Big Mac Index (The Economist):**
Uses price of McDonald's Big Mac across countries to assess currency over/undervaluation. If Big Mac costs $5 in US and ₹250 in India, implied PPP rate = ₹250/$5 = ₹50/$. If actual rate is ₹83/$, the Rupee is **undervalued** by ~40%.

---

### 8.3 The Mundell-Fleming Model

**Definition:**
The Mundell-Fleming Model is the open-economy extension of the IS-LM model. It adds the **BP curve (Balance of Payments equilibrium)** to determine the joint equilibrium of goods, money, and foreign exchange markets simultaneously under different exchange rate regimes.

**Three Curves:**
- **IS curve:** Goods market equilibrium (now includes exports and imports)
- **LM curve:** Money market equilibrium
- **BP curve:** BoP equilibrium (Current Account + Capital Account = 0)

**Key Results (Mundell-Fleming Conclusions):**

**Under Fixed Exchange Rates:**
| Policy | Effectiveness |
|--------|--------------|
| Fiscal Policy (↑G) | **EFFECTIVE** → IS shifts right, BoP surplus, money supply expands, Y rises |
| Monetary Policy (↑M) | **INEFFECTIVE** → LM shifts right, exchange rate pressure, central bank sells forex, M contracts back |

**Under Floating Exchange Rates:**
| Policy | Effectiveness |
|--------|--------------|
| Fiscal Policy (↑G) | **INEFFECTIVE** → Higher r attracts capital, currency appreciates, exports fall, IS shifts back |
| Monetary Policy (↑M) | **EFFECTIVE** → Lower r, capital outflow, currency depreciates, exports rise, IS shifts right |

**The Impossible Trinity (Trilemma):**
A country CANNOT simultaneously have:
1. Fixed exchange rate
2. Free capital movement
3. Independent monetary policy
→ Can only have 2 of the 3!

```
         Free Capital Mobility
              /         \
             /           \
    Fixed Rate       Independent
    + Open         Monetary Policy
    Capital        + Open Capital
    (No Monetary   (Float Rate)
     Independence)
```

**India's Choice:** Managed float + Partially open capital account + Independent monetary policy (attempts all 3 with compromise on each)

---

### 8.4 Case Study — India's Rupee Depreciation (2022-23)

**Background:** In 2022, the US Federal Reserve raised interest rates aggressively (from 0% to 5.25% — fastest in 40 years). This caused a global flight of capital from emerging markets to the US.

**Mundell-Fleming Analysis:**
- Higher US rates → Capital flows OUT of India (FPI sold $25 billion of Indian assets in 2022)
- Capital outflow → Demand for USD rises, Rupee demand falls → Rupee depreciates
- Rupee fell from ₹74/$ to ₹83/$ (12% depreciation in 2022)
- **RBI Intervention:** Sold $100+ billion from forex reserves to slow depreciation

**Impact on India:**
- Import costs rose (oil, gold, electronics)
- Inflation imported
- RBI had to raise rates to defend Rupee + control inflation
- Software exporters (TCS, Infosys) BENEFITED — earned more rupees per dollar of revenue

**Lesson:** In a globally integrated economy, US monetary policy affects every emerging market. Understanding the Mundell-Fleming model helps predict these transmission channels.

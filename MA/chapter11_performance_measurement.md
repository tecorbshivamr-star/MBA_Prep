## 11.2 Introduction

> As organizations grow, they split into **divisions and departments**. Each division needs its own performance measurement system that:
> - Is **fair** (measures only what the manager controls)
> - **Aligns** with overall company goals
> - Uses **both financial and non-financial** measures

> 🧠 **BugZero Growing Up:**
> BugZero started as 5 people. Now it has 3 divisions:
> - **QA Services Division** (earns revenue from client contracts)
> - **Tool Development Division** (builds internal testing tools)
> - **Training Division** (trains new QA hires)
> Each division needs its own performance metrics — and they should all push toward BugZero's overall goal!

## 11.3 Responsibility Centres ⭐⭐

> **Responsibility Centre** = A unit of an organization where a **manager is accountable** for a specific aspect of performance.

| Type | Manager Responsible For | Performance Measure | BugZero Example |
|------|------------------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Cost Centre** | **Costs only** (no revenue control) | Actual Cost vs Budget | BugZero IT infrastructure team |
| **Revenue Centre** | **Revenue only** (no cost control) | Actual Revenue vs Target | BugZero Sales team |
| **Profit Centre** | **Both Revenue AND Costs** | Profit = Revenue − Cost | BugZero QA Services Division |
| **Investment Centre** | Revenue, Costs **AND** Assets/Capital | ROI, RI, EVA | BugZero as a whole company |

### 📌 Key Principle: **Controllability**
> A manager should only be judged on what they **can control**.
> - Cost centre manager → judge on costs, NOT revenue
> - Profit centre manager → judge on profit, NOT capital employed

---

## 11.4 Performance Management System

> **Performance Management System** = A structured process to **set targets, measure results, compare with targets, reward achievement, and improve performance**.

### 📌 Steps in Performance Management
```
1. Set Strategic Goals (Vision → Mission → Objectives)
         ↓
2. Develop KPIs (Key Performance Indicators)
         ↓
3. Set Targets/Standards for each KPI
         ↓
4. Measure Actual Performance
         ↓
5. Compare Actual vs Target (Variance)
         ↓
6. Take Corrective Action
         ↓
7. Reward / Recognize Achievement
         ↓
8. Review and Update System (loop back)
```

## 11.5 Divisional Performance Measures ⭐⭐

### 📌 A) Return on Investment (ROI)

```
ROI = (Net Profit / Capital Employed) × 100

Or = (Net Profit / Divisional Assets) × 100
```

**Limitation:** May cause **goal incongruence** — a manager may reject a good project if it lowers divisional ROI even though it benefits the company.

---

### 📌 B) Residual Income (RI)

```
RI = Divisional Profit − (Capital Employed × Required Rate of Return)

Positive RI → Division creating value above the cost of capital ✅
Negative RI → Division destroying value ❌
```

**Advantage over ROI:** Manager will accept any project where project RI > 0, which is **goal congruent** with company.

---

### 📌 C) Transfer Pricing ⭐

> **Transfer Price** = The price charged when one division of a company **sells goods/services to another division** of the same company.

| Method | Transfer Price | When Used |
|--------|---------------|-----------|
| **Market Price** | Same as external market price | When market exists for the product |
| **Marginal/Variable Cost** | Variable cost of producing division | When selling division has spare capacity |
| **Full Cost** | Total cost (variable + fixed) | Simple but no profit for selling division |
| **Cost Plus** | Cost + % markup | Common in practice |
| **Negotiated Price** | Agreed between divisions | When managers have autonomy |

> **Goal:** Transfer price should motivate both divisions AND be fair to both.

## 11.6 Pros and Cons of Performance Measures ⭐

### ✅ Pros
| Advantage | Explanation |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Clarity** | Clear targets → employees know what's expected |
| **Motivation** | Rewards tied to performance → incentivizes effort |
| **Control** | Identifies problems early |
| **Accountability** | Managers responsible for their area |
| **Resource allocation** | High-performing divisions get more resources |

### ❌ Cons
| Limitation | Explanation |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Gaming the system** | Managers manipulate numbers to hit targets |
| **Short-termism** | Focus on short-term KPIs, ignore long-term health |
| **Tunnel vision** | Optimize one metric at expense of others |
| **Costly** | Time and money to collect and analyze data |
| **Subjectivity** | Some non-financial measures hard to quantify fairly |
| **Demotivation** | Unachievable targets discourage employees |

## 11.7 Financial Performance Measures

### 📌 Key Ratios

| Measure | Formula | Indicates |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| **ROI** | Net Profit / Capital Employed × 100 | Overall return on assets |
| **RI** | Profit − (CE × Required Rate) | Value above minimum return |
| **Net Profit Margin** | Net Profit / Revenue × 100 | Profitability per ₹ of sales |
| **Asset Turnover** | Revenue / Capital Employed | Efficiency of asset use |
| **EPS** | Profit after tax / No. of shares | Return per equity share |

> 🔑 **Note:** ROI = Net Profit Margin × Asset Turnover
> ```
> ROI = (Profit/Sales) × (Sales/Capital) = Profit/Capital
> ```

## 11.8 Economic Value Added (EVA) ⭐⭐⭐

> **EVA** = Measures the **true economic profit** created — profit **after deducting the cost of ALL capital** (both debt and equity). Developed by **Stern Stewart & Co.**

```
EVA = NOPAT − (Capital Employed × WACC)

Where:
NOPAT = Net Operating Profit After Tax
        (Operating Profit adjusted for taxes, excludes financing costs)

WACC  = Weighted Average Cost of Capital
        (Average cost of debt + equity, weighted by proportion)

Capital Employed = Total assets − Current liabilities
```

### 📌 Interpretation
```
EVA > 0 → Company is creating value (earning more than cost of capital) ✅
EVA = 0 → Just covering cost of capital (break-even on economic terms)
EVA < 0 → Company is destroying value (not earning enough) ❌
```

### 📊 Solved Example

BugZero:
- Operating Profit (before interest & tax) = ₹15,00,000
- Tax rate = 30%
- Capital Employed = ₹80,00,000
- WACC = 12%

```
NOPAT = ₹15,00,000 × (1 − 30%) = ₹15,00,000 × 0.70 = ₹10,50,000

Cost of Capital = ₹80,00,000 × 12% = ₹9,60,000

EVA = ₹10,50,000 − ₹9,60,000 = ₹90,000 (Positive → Value Created ✅)
```

---

## 11.9 Limitations of EVA

| Limitation | Explanation |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Complex calculation** | NOPAT and WACC require many adjustments |
| **Short-term focus** | Can discourage long-term investment (R&D, training) |
| **WACC estimation** | Difficult to accurately estimate cost of equity |
| **Not comparable** | Hard to compare EVA across different-sized companies |
| **Accounting adjustments** | Requires many add-backs to reported profit |
| **Ignores non-financial** | Only financial — misses customer, employee factors |

## 11.10 Non-Financial Performance Measures

> Pure financial measures = **lagging indicators** (tell what happened).
> Non-financial measures = **leading indicators** (predict future success).

| Category | Measures | BugZero Example |
|---------|---------|----------------|
| **Quality** | Defect rate, rework %, first-time pass rate | Bug escape rate < 3% |
| **Customer** | Satisfaction score, retention rate, complaints | NPS score 8.5/10 |
| **Delivery/Time** | On-time delivery %, cycle time | 95% on-time delivery |
| **Innovation** | New products launched, patents, R&D spend | 2 new automation tools/year |
| **Employee** | Staff turnover, training hours, absenteeism | 85% retention, 10 training days |
| **Environmental** | Carbon footprint, energy use, waste | Carbon-neutral operations |

## 11.12 Performance Pyramid ⭐⭐

> **Performance Pyramid** = A model by **Lynch and Cross (1991)** that shows how organizational objectives **cascade down** from corporate vision to operational departments, with performance measures flowing back **up**.

```
        ┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
        │        CORPORATE VISION             │ ← Financial / Market measures
        └─────────────────┬───────────────────┘
               ┌──────────┴──────────┐
               ▼                     ▼
      ┌─────────────────┐   ┌─────────────────┐
      │ BUSINESS UNIT   │   │  BUSINESS UNIT  │ ← Customer Satisfaction & Flexibility
      └────────┬────────┘   └────────┬────────┘
         ┌─────┴─────┐         ┌─────┴─────┐
         ▼           ▼         ▼           ▼
    ┌─────────┐ ┌─────────┐ ┌─────────┐ ┌─────────┐
    │BUS. OPS │ │BUS. OPS │ │BUS. OPS │ │BUS. OPS │ ← Quality & Delivery
    └────┬────┘ └────┬────┘ └────┬────┘ └────┬────┘
         └────────────────────────────────────┘
                          ↓
         DEPARTMENTS/WORK CENTRES → Cycle Time & Waste
```

### 📌 Key Measures at Each Level

| Level | External Effectiveness | Internal Efficiency |
|-------|----------------------|-----------------------|
| Corporate | Market share, Financial return | — |
| Business Unit | Customer satisfaction | Flexibility |
| Business Operations | Quality | Delivery |
| Departments | — | Cycle time, Waste |

> 🔑 **Key Idea:** Objectives flow **DOWN** (top to bottom). Measures flow **UP** (bottom to top).

## 11.13 Building Block Model ⭐⭐⭐

> **Building Block Model** = A performance measurement framework by **Fitzgerald and Moon (1996)** built on **3 building blocks**: Dimensions, Standards, and Rewards.

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                  BUILDING BLOCK MODEL                  │
├──────────────────┬─────────────────┬───────────────────┤
│   DIMENSIONS     │    STANDARDS    │     REWARDS       │
│  (What to        │   (How to set   │  (How to motivate │
│   measure?)      │    targets?)    │    achievement?)  │
├──────────────────┼─────────────────┼───────────────────┤
│ 1. Financial     │ 1. Ownership    │ 1. Clarity        │
│    Performance   │    (Managers    │    (Rewards are   │
│ 2. Competitive-  │    involved in  │    understood)    │
│    ness          │    setting)     │ 2. Motivation     │
│ 3. Quality       │ 2. Achievability│    (Linked to     │
│ 4. Flexibility   │    (Stretching  │    performance)   │
│ 5. Resource      │    but          │ 3. Controllability│
│    Utilization   │    achievable)  │    (Rewarded for  │
│ 6. Innovation    │ 3. Equity/      │    what they      │
│                  │    Fairness     │    control)       │
└──────────────────┴─────────────────┴───────────────────┘
```

### 📌 The 6 Dimensions ⭐

| Dimension | Description | BugZero Measure |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Financial Performance** | Revenue, profit, cash flow | Monthly profit margin |
| **Competitiveness** | Market share, client wins vs competitors | New clients won vs rival firms |
| **Quality** | Error rates, customer satisfaction | Bug escape rate < 3% |
| **Flexibility** | Ability to adapt to change | Time to onboard new client type |
| **Resource Utilization** | Efficiency of assets and people | Engineer utilization rate |
| **Innovation** | New services, improvements | New automation tool deployments |

### 📌 The 3 Standards ⭐
1. **Ownership** — Managers participate in setting their own targets → more commitment
2. **Achievability** — Targets must be challenging but realistic (not impossible)
3. **Equity** — All departments judged fairly given their circumstances

### 📌 The 3 Rewards ⭐
1. **Clarity** — Employees understand exactly how they'll be rewarded
2. **Motivation** — Reward system actually motivates the desired behavior
3. **Controllability** — Employees rewarded only for things within their control

## 11.14 Performance Prism ⭐⭐

> **Performance Prism** = A framework by **Neely, Adams and Kennerley (2002)** that takes a **stakeholder-centric** approach — measures performance across **5 facets** shaped like a prism.

```
         ┌─────────────────────────────┐
  TOP:   │  STAKEHOLDER SATISFACTION   │ ← Who are our stakeholders? What do they want?
         └──────────────┬──────────────┘
                        │
     ┌──────────────────┼──────────────────┐
     ▼                  ▼                  ▼
┌─────────┐      ┌──────────┐       ┌──────────┐
│STRATEGIES│     │PROCESSES │       │CAPABILIT-│
│          │     │          │       │IES       │
└─────────┘      └──────────┘       └──────────┘
                        │
         ┌──────────────┴──────────────┐
 BOTTOM: │  STAKEHOLDER CONTRIBUTION   │ ← What do we need from stakeholders?
         └─────────────────────────────┘
```

### 📌 The 5 Facets of Performance Prism ⭐

| Facet | Question Asked | BugZero Example |
|-------|---------------|----------------|
| **Stakeholder Satisfaction** | What do stakeholders want and need? | Clients: quality & speed. Employees: fair pay & growth |
| **Strategies** | What strategies deliver this satisfaction? | Automate 80% of testing → faster delivery |
| **Processes** | What processes execute these strategies? | QA pipeline, client onboarding, reporting |
| **Capabilities** | What capabilities support these processes? | Skilled QA team, automation tools, client relationships |
| **Stakeholder Contribution** | What must stakeholders contribute for us to succeed? | Clients pay on time, employees innovate, investors fund growth |

### 📌 Stakeholders Covered
```
Investors → Shareholders → Customers → Employees → Suppliers → Regulators → Communities
```

> 🔑 **Key difference from BSC:** BSC focuses on company's strategy. **Performance Prism starts with STAKEHOLDERS first** — their needs define the strategy.

## 11.15 Triple Bottom Line (TBL) ⭐⭐

> **Triple Bottom Line (TBL)** = A framework by **John Elkington (1994)** that measures organizational success across **3 dimensions**: Profit, People, and Planet — not just financial profit.

```
            ┌───────────────────┐
            │      PEOPLE       │ (Social)
            │  Are we fair to   │
            │  employees,       │
            │  communities,     │
            │  society?         │
            └─────────┬─────────┘
                      │
         ┌────────────┼────────────┐
         ▼            ▼            ▼
  ┌───────────┐ ┌───────────┐ ┌───────────┐
  │  PROFIT   │ │           │ │  PLANET   │
  │(Economic) │ │   TBL     │ │(Environ-  │
  │ Financial │ │           │ │ mental)   │
  │ viability │ │           │ │ Are we    │
  │           │ │           │ │ green?    │
  └───────────┘ └───────────┘ └───────────┘
```

### 📌 The 3 Ps of TBL

| Dimension | Focus | Measures | BugZero Example |
|-----------|-------|---------|-----------------|
| **Profit** (Economic) | Financial sustainability | Revenue, profit, ROI, EVA | ₹30L annual profit |
| **People** (Social) | Social responsibility | Employee welfare, community impact, diversity | Gender diversity 40%, CSR spend |
| **Planet** (Environmental) | Environmental impact | Carbon footprint, energy use, waste | Cloud servers on renewable energy |

### 📌 Why TBL Matters
- Traditional accounting → Only measures **Profit**
- TBL → Measures **all three** → Complete picture of corporate responsibility
- Used by: MNCs, government organisations, social enterprises
- Related to: **CSR (Corporate Social Responsibility)** and **ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance)**

### ✅ Advantages of TBL
1. Encourages **long-term thinking**
2. Builds **brand reputation** and stakeholder trust
3. Helps meet **regulatory requirements** (environmental laws)
4. Attracts **socially conscious investors**

### ❌ Limitations of TBL
1. People and Planet metrics are **hard to quantify**
2. No standard measurement system
3. Risk of **greenwashing** (companies faking environmental commitment)
4. May conflict with short-term profit goals

## 📊 Comparison of All Performance Frameworks ⭐⭐

| Framework | Developer | Focus | Key Feature |
|-----------|----------|-------|-------------|
| **Balanced Scorecard** | Kaplan & Norton (1992) | 4 perspectives | Strategy-driven, cause & effect |
| **Performance Pyramid** | Lynch & Cross (1991) | Hierarchical cascade | Objectives down, measures up |
| **Building Block Model** | Fitzgerald & Moon (1996) | Service sector | 6 Dimensions + Standards + Rewards |
| **Performance Prism** | Neely et al. (2002) | Stakeholder-centric | 5 Facets, starts with stakeholders |
| **Triple Bottom Line** | Elkington (1994) | Sustainability | Profit + People + Planet |

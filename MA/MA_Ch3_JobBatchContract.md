## 🟢 BEGINNER LEVEL — "What is this about?"

Not all companies make identical products on a massive assembly line. 
- If you are a high-end tailor making a custom wedding suit, every suit is unique. You need to track the exact fabric and time spent on *that specific suit*. This is **Job Costing**.
- If you are a baker making 500 identical chocolate chip cookies at once, you calculate the cost of the whole tray and divide by 500. This is **Batch Costing**.
- If you are Larsen & Toubro (L&T) building a massive ₹500 Crore bridge that takes 4 years to complete, you use **Contract Costing**.

---

## 🟡 INTERMEDIATE LEVEL — "Key Concepts & Frameworks"

### 1. Job Costing
Used for custom, one-off orders. You maintain a "Job Cost Sheet" for each client.
- E.g., A printing press printing wedding cards vs. corporate brochures. Each job gets a unique Job Number. Direct costs are traced to the job, and overheads are absorbed using an OAR (Overhead Absorption Rate).

### 2. Batch Costing
Used when goods are produced in identical groups.
- E.g., Pharmaceuticals making a batch of 10,000 Paracetamol tablets. 
- **Optimal Batch Quantity (OBQ):** Just like EOQ tells you how much to buy, OBQ tells you the perfect batch size to produce to minimize machine setup costs and storage costs.

### 3. Contract Costing (Terminal Costing)
Used for long-term construction projects spanning multiple financial years.
- **The Problem:** If a bridge takes 4 years to build, does the company show zero profit for 3 years and massive profit in Year 4? No! Shareholders would hate that. 
- **The Solution:** The company estimates the "Notional Profit" based on how much work is certified as complete by the architect, and recognizes a portion of that profit every year.

## 🔴 ADVANCED / EXAM LEVEL — "How to write it in the exam?"

### Q. What is Contract Costing? How is profit calculated and transferred to the Profit & Loss Account on an incomplete contract? (15 Marks)

**1. Definition and Features:**
Contract costing is a specific form of order costing applied where work is undertaken to customer's special requirements, and each order is of long duration (usually > 1 year). The work is generally carried out at the site (e.g., civil engineering, ship building).
*   Each contract is treated as a separate cost centre.
*   Most costs are direct (site labour, specific materials, specialized plant).
*   Payments are received in installments based on the architect's certificate (Progress Payments), with a portion withheld as "Retention Money."

**2. Key Terminology:**
*   **Work Certified:** The value of work completed which has been approved and certified by the contractee’s architect.
*   **Work Uncertified:** Work done since the last certification date, valued at cost.
*   **Notional Profit:** The estimated profit on the contract up to the current date. 
    *(Notional Profit = Value of Work Certified - Cost of Work Certified).*

**3. Rules for Profit Transfer on Incomplete Contracts (The Crux):**
Because the contract is incomplete, recognizing 100% of the Notional Profit is risky (future costs might overrun). The accounting rules dictate how much profit can be safely transferred to the P&L Account based on the percentage of completion:

*   **Rule 1: If Completion is less than 25%**
    No profit is recognized. It is considered too early to predict the outcome safely. The entire Notional Profit is kept in reserve.

*   **Rule 2: If Completion is between 25% and 50%**
    Formula: 
    *Profit to P&L = Notional Profit × (1/3) × (Cash Received / Work Certified)*

*   **Rule 3: If Completion is between 50% and 90% (Substantial Completion)**
    Formula: 
    *Profit to P&L = Notional Profit × (2/3) × (Cash Received / Work Certified)*
    (More profit is recognized because the risk of failure is lower).

*   **Rule 4: If Completion is near 100%**
    Estimated Total Profit is calculated (Contract Price - Estimated Total Cost). The portion transferred to P&L is proportional to the work certified.

**Conclusion:**
This conservative approach ensures that construction firms do not overstate their profits and pay dividends out of unrealized, risky paper profits, adhering perfectly to the accounting principle of prudence.

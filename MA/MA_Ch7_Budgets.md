## 🟢 BEGINNER LEVEL — "What is this about?"

If you plan a road trip, you decide in advance how much money you will spend on petrol, food, and hotels. That is a **Budget**. 
A company does the exact same thing. They predict their sales for the next year, and based on that, they predict how much material they need to buy and how many workers they need to hire. 

**Budgetary Control** is what happens during the road trip. You check your wallet halfway through the trip to see if you are spending more money than you planned. If you are over budget on food, you eat cheaper food the next day to get back on track.

## 🟡 INTERMEDIATE LEVEL — "Key Concepts & Frameworks"

### 1. Types of Budgets
There is a logical sequence to creating corporate budgets:
1. **Sales Budget:** You *always* start here. You can't plan production until you know how much you can sell.
2. **Production Budget:** Based on sales + desired closing stock.
3. **Material & Labour Budgets:** Based on production requirements.
4. **Cash Budget:** Ensures the company won't run out of liquid cash during the year.
5. **Master Budget:** The final culmination—a projected Profit & Loss account and Balance Sheet.

### 2. Fixed vs. Flexible Budgets
- **Fixed Budget:** Designed for a *single* level of activity (e.g., 10,000 units). If the company ends up producing 8,000 units, comparing actual costs to the 10,000-unit budget is totally useless and unfair.
- **Flexible Budget:** The smart way. It adjusts the budgeted variable costs based on the *actual* level of output achieved, keeping fixed costs constant. It provides a true "apples-to-apples" comparison.

### 3. Zero-Based Budgeting (ZBB)
Traditional budgeting takes last year's budget and adds 10% for inflation. 
**ZBB** throws last year's budget in the trash. Every department manager starts with ₹0. They must mathematically justify every single rupee they ask for, proving how it adds value to the company. It destroys corporate waste but takes a lot of time to calculate.

## 🔴 ADVANCED / EXAM LEVEL — "How to write it in the exam?"

### Q. What is a Flexible Budget? Why is it considered superior to a Fixed Budget for performance evaluation? Prepare a hypothetical Flexible Budget to illustrate your answer. (15 Marks)

**1. Definition of a Flexible Budget:**
A flexible budget is a dynamic financial plan that adjusts or "flexes" according to the actual volume of activity achieved. Unlike a static/fixed budget, which is set for only one assumed level of production, a flexible budget provides reliable cost benchmarks for any level of output within a relevant range. 

**2. The Fundamental Principle:**
The creation of a flexible budget relies entirely on the strict classification of costs into their behavioral components:
*   **Fixed Costs:** Remain absolutely constant in total, regardless of the output level.
*   **Variable Costs:** Change in direct proportion to the volume of output (per-unit cost remains constant).
*   **Semi-Variable Costs:** Must be mathematically split into their fixed and variable components before budgeting.

**3. Why it is Superior for Performance Evaluation:**
Performance evaluation requires comparing "Actuals" against "Standards." If a factory manager was budgeted to produce 10,000 units with a material cost of ₹1,00,000, but due to low demand, they only produced 6,000 units at a cost of ₹70,000.
*   *Fixed Budget View:* Compares ₹70k actual vs ₹100k budget and incorrectly declares the manager a genius for saving ₹30,000.
*   *Flexible Budget View:* Recalculates the budget for 6,000 units. (₹10 per unit × 6,000 = ₹60,000). Compares ₹70k actual vs ₹60k flexed budget and correctly identifies a massive ₹10,000 adverse variance (inefficiency). It compares apples to apples.

**4. Hypothetical Flexible Budget Illustration:**
*Assume a factory with a maximum capacity of 10,000 units. Variable Cost = ₹50/unit. Fixed Cost = ₹2,00,000.*

| Particulars | Budget at 60% Capacity (6,000 units) | Budget at 80% Capacity (8,000 units) | Budget at 100% Capacity (10,000 units) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Sales Revenue (₹100/unit)** | ₹6,00,000 | ₹8,00,000 | ₹10,00,000 |
| **Variable Costs (₹50/unit)** | (₹3,00,000) | (₹4,00,000) | (₹5,00,000) |
| **Contribution** | **₹3,00,000** | **₹4,00,000** | **₹5,00,000** |
| **Fixed Costs** | (₹2,00,000) | (₹2,00,000) | (₹2,00,000) |
| **Net Profit** | **₹1,00,000** | **₹2,00,000** | **₹3,00,000** |

**Conclusion:**
A flexible budget is an indispensable tool for variance analysis. By neutralizing the volume variance, it allows top management to accurately isolate and measure true operational efficiency and cost control.

## 🟢 BEGINNER LEVEL — "What is this about?"

The old way of accounting was simple: Calculate how much it costs to make a product, add a 20% profit margin, and set the selling price. (Cost + Profit = Price). 
But today, competition is brutal. If your calculated price is higher than the market rate, nobody buys your product. 

**Modern Cost Management** flips the equation. The market tells you the Price. You subtract your desired Profit. What's left is your **Target Cost**. 
> Price (Fixed by Market) - Profit (Demanded by Shareholders) = Target Cost
Now, the engineers and accountants must work together to figure out how to manufacture the product cheaply enough to hit that target!

## 🟡 INTERMEDIATE LEVEL — "Key Concepts & Frameworks"

### 1. Target Costing
Used heavily by Japanese automakers (Toyota). 
- *Step 1:* Market research says customers will pay exactly ₹10 Lakhs for a new SUV.
- *Step 2:* The company wants a 15% profit (₹1.5 Lakhs).
- *Step 3:* The Target Cost is therefore ₹8.5 Lakhs.
- *Step 4:* "Value Engineering" — Engineers strip away non-essential features, use cheaper materials, and redesign the car until it costs exactly ₹8.5 Lakhs to build.

### 2. Activity-Based Costing (ABC)
Traditional accounting takes the total factory electricity bill and just divides it equally among all products. That is inaccurate.
**ABC Costing** traces overheads to the *activities* that cause them. 
If Product A requires the machine to be set up 50 times, and Product B requires it 2 times, Product A should absorb way more of the "Machine Setup Costs." It uses **Cost Drivers** (like 'number of setups' or 'number of quality inspections') to assign overheads highly accurately.

### 3. Life Cycle Costing
Traditional accounting only looks at manufacturing costs.
Life Cycle Costing tracks a product from the day it is born in an R&D lab to the day it dies and is recycled. 
- *Insight:* 80% of a product's lifetime costs are "locked in" during the R&D/Design phase. Once the blueprint is sent to the factory, it's too late to save money. Cost control must happen at the design stage.

## 🔴 ADVANCED / EXAM LEVEL — "How to write it in the exam?"

### Q. What is Activity-Based Costing (ABC)? How does it overcome the limitations of traditional overhead absorption systems? Explain the concepts of Cost Pools and Cost Drivers. (15 Marks)

**1. The Flaw in Traditional Costing:**
Traditional absorption costing systems allocate manufacturing overheads to products using a single, volume-based rate (e.g., Direct Labour Hours or Machine Hours). This worked well in the 1950s when overheads were low and direct labour was high. Today, in highly automated factories, overheads (IT, maintenance, setups) are massive. Allocating them based solely on labour hours heavily overcosts high-volume simple products and undercosts low-volume complex products. 

**2. Definition of Activity-Based Costing (ABC):**
ABC is a modern costing methodology that identifies the specific activities performed in an organization, accumulates the costs of those activities, and then assigns these costs to products based on their actual consumption of the activities. 

**3. Key Terminology:**
*   **Activity:** Any event or transaction that incurs a cost (e.g., setting up a machine, inspecting quality, processing a purchase order).
*   **Cost Pool:** A grouping of individual overhead costs associated with a specific activity (e.g., all costs related to machine setups are pooled into a 'Setup Cost Pool').
*   **Cost Driver:** The quantifiable metric that "drives" the cost of the activity. It is the allocation base. 
    *   *Examples:* For the 'Purchasing' activity pool, the cost driver is 'Number of Purchase Orders'. For the 'Quality Control' pool, the cost driver is 'Number of Inspections'.

**4. The ABC Process (Steps):**
1.  Identify major activities (e.g., Machining, Setup, Inspection).
2.  Assign total overhead costs to these Activity Cost Pools.
3.  Identify the most appropriate Cost Driver for each pool.
4.  Calculate the **Activity Rate** (Total Cost in Pool ÷ Total Volume of Cost Driver).
5.  Assign costs to products based on their actual usage of the cost drivers (Activity Rate × Product's specific driver volume).

**5. How ABC Overcomes Traditional Limitations:**
*   **Accuracy:** By using multiple cost drivers (including non-volume related drivers like 'number of setups'), ABC prevents cross-subsidization between products. Management finally sees the true profitability of complex, low-volume product lines.
*   **Cost Management:** ABC highlights the cost of specific activities. If management sees that "Machine Setups" cost ₹50 Lakhs a year, they can focus engineering efforts on reducing setup times, thereby cutting costs at the root cause. This leads to Activity-Based Management (ABM).

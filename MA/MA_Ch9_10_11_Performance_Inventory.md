## 🟢 BEGINNER LEVEL — "What is this about?"

If a CEO only looks at "Net Profit," they are driving a car by only looking in the rearview mirror. Net Profit tells you what happened *last year*. 

To know if the company will survive *next year*, you need a **Balanced Scorecard**. You have to check if your employees are learning new skills, if your factory processes are getting faster, and if your customers are actually happy. 
If customers hate you today, your Net Profit will collapse tomorrow.

Finally, we look at **Inventory Management** (making sure you have just enough raw material, but not too much) and **Financial Ratios** (quick formulas to check the financial health of the business).

## 🟡 INTERMEDIATE LEVEL — "Key Concepts & Frameworks"

### 1. The Balanced Scorecard (BSC)
Developed by Kaplan and Norton. It forces management to look at the business from 4 different perspectives:
1. **Financial Perspective:** "How do we look to shareholders?" (Metric: ROI, Cash Flow).
2. **Customer Perspective:** "How do customers see us?" (Metric: Customer retention rate, Net Promoter Score).
3. **Internal Business Process:** "What must we excel at?" (Metric: Manufacturing cycle time, Defect rate).
4. **Learning and Growth:** "Can we continue to improve?" (Metric: Employee training hours, R&D spend).

### 2. Economic Value Added (EVA)
An accounting profit of ₹100 Crores looks great. But what if the shareholders gave the company ₹2,000 Crores of capital to get that return? A 5% return is terrible! 
**EVA** deducts the "Cost of Capital" from the profit. 
> **EVA = Net Operating Profit After Tax (NOPAT) - (Total Capital Employed × WACC)**
If EVA is negative, the company is destroying shareholder wealth, even if accounting profit is positive.

### 3. Inventory Management (ABC & JIT)
- **ABC Analysis:** 
  - 'A' Items: High value, low quantity (e.g., Car Engine). Track them strictly.
  - 'B' Items: Medium value.
  - 'C' Items: Low value, high quantity (e.g., Screws). Don't waste time tracking every screw.
- **Just-In-Time (JIT):** Invented by Toyota. Buy materials exactly when you need them for the assembly line. Result: Zero warehouse storage costs.

## 🔴 ADVANCED / EXAM LEVEL — "How to write it in the exam?"

### Q. Write short notes on: (a) The Balanced Scorecard (b) Economic Value Added (EVA) (c) Just-In-Time (JIT) Inventory. (15 Marks)

**(a) The Balanced Scorecard (BSC):**
*   **Concept:** Traditional performance measurement relied solely on financial metrics, which are "lagging indicators" of past decisions. The Balanced Scorecard (Kaplan & Norton, 1992) is a strategic management system that translates a firm's strategy into a comprehensive set of performance measures.
*   **The Four Perspectives:** It balances financial objectives with the operational drivers of future financial performance across four perspectives:
    1.  *Financial:* Revenue growth, cost reduction, asset utilization.
    2.  *Customer:* Market share, customer acquisition, satisfaction, profitability.
    3.  *Internal Process:* Innovation cycle, operations (quality, speed), post-sales service.
    4.  *Learning & Growth:* Human capital (skills), information capital (IT systems), organizational capital (culture/alignment).
*   **Significance:** It prevents management from taking short-term actions that boost current profits but destroy long-term value (e.g., cutting R&D or employee training).

**(b) Economic Value Added (EVA):**
*   **Concept:** EVA is a registered trademark of Stern Stewart & Co. It is a true measure of economic profit. While traditional accounting subtracts interest on debt to calculate profit, it ignores the *cost of equity*. EVA deducts the cost of ALL capital (both debt and equity).
*   **Formula:** $EVA = NOPAT - (Capital Employed \times WACC)$
    *(NOPAT = Net Operating Profit After Tax; WACC = Weighted Average Cost of Capital)*.
*   **Significance:** A positive EVA proves the company generated returns higher than the hurdle rate demanded by its investors, thereby creating genuine wealth. A negative EVA indicates wealth destruction, regardless of what the EPS (Earnings Per Share) says.

**(c) Just-In-Time (JIT) Inventory System:**
*   **Concept:** Pioneered by Toyota in Japan, JIT is a "pull" manufacturing philosophy designed to eliminate all forms of waste (Muda), particularly excess inventory. Materials are purchased, and goods are produced, *exactly* when they are needed for the next stage of production or for final sale.
*   **Requirements for Success:** JIT requires a total overhaul of the supply chain. It necessitates ultra-reliable suppliers (delivering defect-free materials directly to the factory floor), a highly flexible workforce, rapid machine setup times, and zero tolerance for quality defects.
*   **Benefits:** It massively reduces working capital tied up in raw materials, eliminates warehouse holding costs, and exposes hidden operational inefficiencies.

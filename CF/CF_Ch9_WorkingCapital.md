## 🟢 BEGINNER LEVEL — "What is this about?"

Capital Budgeting (Chapter 4) was about buying long-term assets, like a ₹50 Crore factory.
But once the factory is built, you need cash *every single day* to buy raw materials, pay worker salaries, and pay the electricity bill before your customers finally pay you for the finished goods. 

**Working Capital** is the money needed to run the day-to-day operations of the business. 
> **Net Working Capital = Current Assets - Current Liabilities**

If a company runs out of working capital, it cannot pay its bills and goes bankrupt, even if it has a billion-dollar factory! Cash is oxygen.

---

## 🟡 INTERMEDIATE LEVEL — "Key Concepts & Frameworks"

### 1. The Operating Cycle
This is the heart of working capital. It's the time it takes to convert cash back into cash:
1. Use cash to buy Raw Materials.
2. Convert Raw Materials into Work-In-Progress (WIP).
3. Convert WIP into Finished Goods.
4. Sell Finished Goods to customers on credit (Debtors).
5. Collect cash from Debtors.
*The longer this cycle takes, the more Working Capital money you need tied up in the business.*

### 2. The Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC)
> **CCC = Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO) + Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) - Days Payable Outstanding (DPO)**
- **DIO:** How long inventory sits in the warehouse (e.g., 30 days).
- **DSO:** How long customers take to pay you (e.g., 45 days).
- **DPO:** How long you take to pay your suppliers (e.g., 60 days).
- *Example CCC = 30 + 45 - 60 = 15 Days.* You only need to finance 15 days of operations out of your own pocket. (Amazon actually has a negative CCC, meaning suppliers fund their operations!)

### 3. Inventory Management (EOQ)
You don't want too much inventory (costs money to store, might expire). You don't want too little (stockouts, angry customers). 
**Economic Order Quantity (EOQ)** is a mathematical formula that tells you exactly how much to order to perfectly balance holding costs and ordering costs.

## 🔴 ADVANCED / EXAM LEVEL — "How to write it in the exam?"

### Q. Define Working Capital Management. Explain the three alternative working capital financing strategies based on the matching principle. (15 Marks)

**1. Definition:**
Working Capital Management involves the administration of a firm's current assets (cash, marketable securities, receivables, and inventory) and current liabilities (creditors, short-term bank loans) to achieve a balance between profitability and liquidity. The goal is to ensure the firm has enough cash to survive while minimizing the cost of idle funds.

**2. Permanent vs. Temporary Working Capital:**
Before financing, management must classify the assets:
*   **Fixed Assets:** Buildings, machinery (Long-term).
*   **Permanent Working Capital:** The absolute minimum base level of inventory and cash required to operate at any given time, regardless of seasonality. (Behaves like a long-term asset).
*   **Temporary (Fluctuating) Working Capital:** Extra working capital needed during peak seasons (e.g., a retailer needing extra inventory before Diwali). 

**3. The Three Financing Strategies:**
How should a firm finance these three buckets? 

*   **A. Hedging (Moderate/Matching) Strategy:**
    *   *The Rule:* Match the maturity of the asset with the maturity of the financing.
    *   *Application:* Finance Fixed Assets and Permanent Working Capital with Long-Term funds (equity, long-term debt). Finance Temporary Working Capital with Short-Term funds (bank overdrafts).
    *   *Result:* Moderate risk and moderate profitability. 

*   **B. Conservative Strategy:**
    *   *The Rule:* Rely heavily on safe, long-term funds.
    *   *Application:* Finance Fixed Assets, Permanent Working Capital, AND a portion of Temporary Working Capital with Long-Term funds. 
    *   *Result:* Very Low Risk (no fear of a bank suddenly recalling a short-term loan). However, Low Profitability (long-term debt is expensive, and you pay interest on idle cash during the off-season).

*   **C. Aggressive Strategy:**
    *   *The Rule:* Rely heavily on cheap, short-term funds.
    *   *Application:* Finance all Temporary Working Capital, AND a portion of Permanent Working Capital with Short-Term funds.
    *   *Result:* High Profitability (short-term interest rates are usually lower). However, High Risk (if short-term interest rates spike, or the bank refuses to renew the loan, the firm faces immediate liquidity crisis and potential bankruptcy).

**Conclusion:**
The choice of strategy depends on the management's risk appetite. A highly profitable but risky tech startup might use a conservative strategy to protect against cash crunches, while a stable FMCG company with predictable cash flows might use an aggressive strategy to maximize shareholder returns.

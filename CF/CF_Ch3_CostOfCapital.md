## 🟢 BEGINNER LEVEL — "What is this about?"

Money is not free. 
If a company needs ₹100 Crores to build a factory, it has to get it from somewhere:
1. **From the Bank/Bondholders (Debt):** They demand Interest. (Let's say 8%).
2. **From Shareholders (Equity):** They demand returns via Dividends and Stock Growth. (Since this is riskier for them, they demand more, say 14%).

The **Cost of Capital** is simply the average of these costs. If the company is funded 50% by Debt and 50% by Equity, its average cost of capital is roughly 11%. 

**The Golden Rule:** The company MUST generate a return higher than 11% on its new factory. If the factory only generates 9%, the company is destroying shareholder wealth. This average is called **WACC (Weighted Average Cost of Capital)**.

---

## 🟡 INTERMEDIATE LEVEL — "Key Concepts & Frameworks"

### 1. Cost of Debt (Kd)
Debt is cheap for a company because **Interest is Tax-Deductible**. 
If a company pays ₹10 in interest, it reduces its taxable profit by ₹10, saving ₹3 in taxes (assuming a 30% tax rate). 
> **Kd (after-tax) = Interest Rate × (1 - Tax Rate)**

### 2. Cost of Equity (Ke)
Equity is expensive because it's risky (shareholders get paid last if the company goes bankrupt), and dividends are NOT tax-deductible. There are two main ways to calculate it:
1. **Dividend Growth Model (Gordon Model):**
   > **Ke = (D₁ / P₀) + g**
   *(Where D₁ is next year's dividend, P₀ is current stock price, g is growth rate)*
2. **CAPM (Capital Asset Pricing Model):**
   > **Ke = Rf + β(Rm - Rf)**
   *(Rf = Risk-free rate like Govt bonds, β = Beta/Volatility of the stock, Rm = Market return)*

### 3. WACC Calculation
You multiply the cost of each type of capital by its percentage weight in the total capital structure.
> **WACC = (Weight of Equity × Ke) + (Weight of Debt × Kd after-tax)**

## 🔴 ADVANCED / EXAM LEVEL — "How to write it in the exam?"

### Q. What is the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC)? Calculate the WACC for XYZ Ltd given the following information. (15 Marks)
**Data:** 
- Total Capital: Equity = ₹6,00,000; Debt = ₹4,00,000 (Total = ₹10,00,000)
- Cost of Equity (Ke) = 15%
- Interest Rate on Debt = 10%
- Corporate Tax Rate = 30%

**1. Definition of WACC:**
The Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) is the overall minimum required rate of return that a firm must earn on its existing asset base to satisfy its creditors, owners, and other providers of capital. It acts as the discount rate (hurdle rate) for evaluating new capital budgeting projects.

**2. Step-by-Step Calculation:**

**Step 1: Calculate the specific cost of each component.**
*   **Cost of Equity (Ke):** Given directly as 15%.
*   **Cost of Debt (Kd after-tax):** 
    Kd = Interest Rate × (1 - Tax Rate)
    Kd = 10% × (1 - 0.30)
    Kd = 10% × 0.70 = **7%**

**Step 2: Calculate the weights of each component (using book value or market value proportions).**
*   Total Capital = 6,00,000 + 4,00,000 = 10,00,000
*   Weight of Equity (We) = 6,00,000 / 10,00,000 = **0.60**
*   Weight of Debt (Wd) = 4,00,000 / 10,00,000 = **0.40**

**Step 3: Calculate WACC.**
*   WACC = (We × Ke) + (Wd × Kd)
*   WACC = (0.60 × 15%) + (0.40 × 7%)
*   WACC = 9.0% + 2.8%
*   **WACC = 11.8%**

**3. Interpretation for Management:**
XYZ Ltd has an overall cost of capital of 11.8%. This means any new project or expansion the company undertakes must generate an Internal Rate of Return (IRR) strictly greater than 11.8%. If a project yields 10%, accepting it will destroy shareholder value because it doesn't even cover the blended cost of financing the project.

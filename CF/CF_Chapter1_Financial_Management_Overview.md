## 1.2 Introduction

### What is Corporate Finance?

**Corporate Finance** deals with the financial decisions made by corporations and the tools and analysis used to make these decisions. It is fundamentally about **how businesses raise money, invest it, and return value to shareholders**.

Every business — whether a startup, SME, or MNC — constantly faces three fundamental financial questions:

1. **What long-term investments should the firm make?** *(Capital Budgeting / Investment Decision)*
2. **How should the firm raise money to fund those investments?** *(Capital Structure / Financing Decision)*
3. **How much of the firm's profit should be paid back to shareholders?** *(Dividend Decision)*

> 📌 *"Finance is the lifeblood of business. Without proper financial management, even the best business ideas fail."*

---

## 1.3 Meaning of Financial Management

### Definition

**Financial Management** is the managerial activity concerned with the **planning, organizing, directing, and controlling** of financial activities — such as procurement and utilization of funds — of an enterprise.

It applies general management principles to the financial resources of the enterprise.

| Scholar | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Howard & Upton** | "Financial Management is the application of the planning and control functions to the finance function." |
| **Phillippatus** | "Financial Management is concerned with the managerial decision that results in the acquisition and financing of long-term and short-term credits for the firm." |
| **Weston & Brigham** | "Financial Management is an area of financial decision making harmonizing individual motives and enterprise goals." |
| **Solomon** | "Financial Management is concerned with the efficient use of an important economic resource — capital funds." |

### In Simple Terms:

Financial Management = **How to raise money + How to invest it + How to distribute returns**

```
FINANCIAL MANAGEMENT
        │
        ├── Procurement of Funds (Financing Decision)
        │       └── From shareholders, banks, bonds, retained earnings
        │
        ├── Utilization of Funds (Investment Decision)
        │       └── In fixed assets, working capital, projects
        │
        └── Distribution of Profits (Dividend Decision)
                └── Pay dividends OR reinvest (retained earnings)
```

## 1.4 Scope of Financial Management

The scope of financial management has evolved from a **narrow, traditional** view to a **broad, modern** view.

### Traditional Scope (Early 20th Century)

The traditional scope was **narrow and descriptive**, focused on:
- **Raising of funds** (capital markets, instruments)
- **Legal and procedural aspects** (mergers, consolidation, reorganization)
- Seen primarily as a **staff/clerical function**

> **Criticism of Traditional Approach:**
> - Only concerned with raising funds, not their efficient use
> - Ignored day-to-day financial problems
> - Did not focus on decision making or maximizing value

---

### Modern Scope (Post-1950s)

The modern approach is **analytical and decision-oriented**, covering:

| Area | What It Covers |
|---|---|
| **Financial Planning** | Estimating fund requirements; forecasting |
| **Capital Budgeting** | Long-term investment decisions (which assets to buy) |
| **Capital Structure** | Optimal mix of debt and equity |
| **Working Capital Management** | Day-to-day liquidity (cash, inventory, receivables) |
| **Profit Appropriation** | Dividend policy; retained earnings vs. payout |
| **Financial Control** | Monitoring performance; ratio analysis; auditing |
| **Risk Management** | Managing financial risks (interest rate, currency, credit) |

> **💡 Key Insight**: Modern Financial Management covers **BOTH procurement AND efficient utilization** of funds — it is a **decision-making function**, not just a fund-raising function.

## 1.5 Evolution of Financial Management

Financial Management has evolved through distinct phases:

| Phase | Period | Focus | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Traditional Phase** | 1900–1940s | Raising funds | Descriptive, legal focus, episodic events (mergers, IPOs) |
| **Transitional Phase** | 1940s–1950s | Day-to-day financial problems | Budget controls, liquidity analysis, routine financial management |
| **Modern Phase** | 1950s–Present | Optimal use of funds, wealth maximisation | Analytical, quantitative, decision-oriented, shareholder value focus |

### Milestones in Evolution:

- **1920s**: Focus on capital markets and securities
- **1950s**: **Joel Dean** introduced Capital Budgeting techniques (NPV, IRR)
- **1952**: **Harry Markowitz** introduced Portfolio Theory (risk-return)
- **1958**: **Modigliani & Miller (M&M)** — Capital Structure irrelevance theorem
- **1960s**: **CAPM** (Capital Asset Pricing Model) by Sharpe, Lintner, Mossin
- **1970s**: **Options Pricing** — Black-Scholes Model
- **1980s–90s**: Corporate Governance, Agency Theory gains prominence
- **2000s+**: Behavioral Finance, ESG, CSR integration

> 📌 *The shift was from a **descriptive** (what firms do) to a **normative** approach (what firms SHOULD do to maximize value).*

## 1.6 Decisions / Functions of Financial Management

The **Finance Manager** is responsible for three key decisions:

---

### Decision 1: Investment Decision (Capital Budgeting)

**Definition**: Decision regarding allocation of capital to long-term assets that will generate returns in the future.

**Also called**: Capital Budgeting / Capital Expenditure (CAPEX) Decision

**Question it answers**: *"Where should we invest our money?"*

#### Types of Investment Decisions:
| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Long-term (Capital Budgeting)** | Investment in fixed assets | New plant, machinery, acquisition |
| **Short-term (Working Capital)** | Investment in current assets | Inventory, debtors, cash management |

#### Key Appraisal Techniques:

| Method | Type | Decision Rule |
|---|---|---|
| **Payback Period** | Non-discounting | Accept if PBP < Max acceptable period |
| **ARR** (Accounting Rate of Return) | Non-discounting | Accept if ARR > required rate |
| **NPV** (Net Present Value) | Discounting (DCF) | Accept if NPV > 0 |
| **IRR** (Internal Rate of Return) | Discounting (DCF) | Accept if IRR > Cost of Capital |
| **PI** (Profitability Index) | Discounting (DCF) | Accept if PI > 1 |

> **💡 NPV is the most theoretically sound method** because it directly measures wealth creation.

---

### Decision 2: Financing Decision (Capital Structure)

**Definition**: Decision regarding the **best mix of debt and equity** to finance the firm's assets.

**Question it answers**: *"How should we raise the money we need?"*

#### Sources of Finance:

```
SOURCES OF FUNDS
│
├── OWNED FUNDS (Equity)
│       ├── Equity Share Capital
│       ├── Preference Share Capital
│       └── Retained Earnings
│
└── BORROWED FUNDS (Debt)
        ├── Long-term: Debentures, Bank Loans, Bonds
        └── Short-term: Working Capital Loans, Trade Credit
```

#### Key Concepts:

| Concept | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Capital Structure** | Mix of debt and equity used to finance assets |
| **Financial Leverage** | Use of debt to amplify returns (and risk) |
| **Cost of Capital (WACC)** | Weighted average cost of all financing sources |
| **Optimal Capital Structure** | Mix that minimises WACC and maximises firm value |

**WACC Formula:**
```
WACC = (E/V × Ke) + (D/V × Kd × (1 - Tax Rate))

Where:
  E = Market value of equity
  D = Market value of debt
  V = E + D (Total value)
  Ke = Cost of equity
  Kd = Cost of debt
```

> **Trade-off**: Debt is cheaper (tax shield on interest) but increases financial risk. The finance manager must find the **optimal balance**.

---

### Decision 3: Dividend Decision

**Definition**: Decision regarding **how much of the profit** to distribute to shareholders as dividends vs. retain within the company (retained earnings).

**Question it answers**: *"How much should we pay back to shareholders?"*

#### Key Considerations:

| Factor | Pay More Dividend | Retain More |
|---|---|---|
| **Shareholders' preference** | Need regular income | Prefer capital gains |
| **Investment opportunities** | Few profitable projects | Many profitable projects available |
| **Cash position** | High cash surplus | Funds needed for growth |
| **Tax** | Dividends taxable in hand | Capital gains may be taxed lower |

#### Schools of Thought on Dividends:

| Theory | Proponent | View |
|---|---|---|
| **Dividend Irrelevance** | Modigliani & Miller (1961) | In a perfect market, dividend policy doesn't affect firm value |
| **Bird-in-Hand Theory** | Gordon & Lintner | Investors prefer dividends now (certain) over future capital gains (uncertain) → High dividend = High value |
| **Tax Preference Theory** | Litzenberger & Ramaswamy | Investors prefer low dividends due to tax advantages of capital gains |

> **💡 In practice**: Most firms follow a **stable dividend policy** to signal financial health and maintain investor confidence.

---

### Interrelationship of the Three Decisions

```
        INVESTMENT DECISION
        (What to invest in?)
               │
               ▼
        Determines ASSETS needed
               │
               ▼
        FINANCING DECISION ────► How funded (Debt/Equity)?
        (How to fund it?)          │
                                   ▼
                           DIVIDEND DECISION
                           (How much to pay out?)
                           (Retained earnings fund future investments)
```

> The three decisions are **interdependent** — a high-return investment influences financing, which affects cash availability for dividends.

## 1.7 Objective / Goal of Financial Management

### Two Competing Objectives:

#### Objective 1: Profit Maximisation (Traditional View)

**Definition**: The firm should take decisions that **maximize accounting profits (EPS or Net Profit)**.

**Arguments in FAVOUR:**
- Simple and easy to measure
- Profits = economic reward for risk-taking
- Necessary for survival and growth

**Arguments AGAINST (Criticisms):**

| Criticism | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Ignores Time Value of Money** | ₹1 today ≠ ₹1 tomorrow. Profit max ignores when profits are earned |
| **Ignores Risk** | Two projects with same profit but different risk are treated equally |
| **Ambiguity** | Which profit? Net profit? EPS? Before/after tax? |
| **Ignores Quality of Earnings** | Doesn't distinguish between certain vs uncertain earnings |
| **Promotes Short-termism** | Managers may cut R&D or maintenance to boost short-term profits |
| **Ignores Non-monetary Benefits** | Ignores social welfare, employee satisfaction, goodwill |

---

#### Objective 2: Wealth Maximisation / Shareholder Value Maximisation (Modern View) ✅

**Definition**: The firm should take decisions that **maximize the market price of equity shares (market value of the firm)**.

> **"The goal of Financial Management is to maximize the current market value per share."** — Stephen Ross

**Also stated as**: Maximise the **Net Present Value (NPV)** of all cash flows to shareholders.

**Why Wealth Maximisation is BETTER:**

| Feature | Profit Maximisation | Wealth Maximisation |
|---|---|---|
| **Time Value of Money** | ❌ Ignores | ✅ Considers (uses DCF) |
| **Risk** | ❌ Ignores | ✅ Incorporates risk-adjusted returns |
| **Ambiguity** | ❌ Ambiguous (which profit?) | ✅ Clear — market price of share |
| **Long-term Focus** | ❌ Short-term | ✅ Long-term value creation |
| **Stakeholders** | ❌ Only profit | ✅ Shareholders, bondholders, society |

```
Firm Value = Σ [Expected Cash Flows / (1 + Risk-adjusted Rate)^t]
```

> ⚠️ **Wealth Maximisation ≠ Greed** — it means creating sustainable, long-term value, taking risk properly into account, for the benefit of all stakeholders.

---

### Profit Maximisation vs. Wealth Maximisation — The Exam Table

| Basis | Profit Maximisation | Wealth Maximisation |
|---|---|---|
| **Focus** | Accounting profit / EPS | Market price of shares / NPV |
| **Time Horizon** | Short-term | Long-term |
| **Risk** | Ignored | Considered |
| **Time Value of Money** | Ignored | Incorporated |
| **Ambiguity** | High (many profit measures) | Low (clear: share price) |
| **Social Responsibility** | Ignored | Considered |
| **Stakeholder Impact** | Only shareholders | All stakeholders |
| **Modern Acceptance** | Outdated | ✅ Widely accepted |

---

### Other Subsidiary Objectives

While wealth maximisation is the primary goal, firms also pursue:
1. **Liquidity**: Maintaining sufficient cash/current assets
2. **Solvency**: Ability to meet long-term obligations
3. **Profitability**: Earning adequate returns
4. **Efficiency**: Optimal utilisation of resources

## 1.8 Agency Issues

### The Principal–Agent Problem

**Context**: In modern corporations, **ownership (shareholders) is separated from management (executives)**. This separation creates the **Agency Relationship**.

| Role | Who | Goal |
|---|---|---|
| **Principal** | Shareholders (Owners) | Maximise shareholder wealth |
| **Agent** | Managers (CEO, CFO, etc.) | May pursue personal goals (salary, perks, job security) |

**Agency Problem** arises when the **interests of agents (managers) diverge from the interests of principals (shareholders)**.

> 📌 *Formally articulated by Jensen & Meckling (1976) in their seminal paper on Agency Theory.*

---

### Forms of Agency Conflict

#### 1. Shareholders vs. Managers

| Manager's Self-Interest | Conflict with Shareholders |
|---|---|
| **Empire Building** | Managers acquire large firms to increase power/prestige, even if it destroys value |
| **Risk Aversion** | Managers prefer safe projects (to protect job) vs. high-return/high-risk projects |
| **Excessive Perks** | Corporate jets, luxury offices — reduce firm value |
| **Short-termism** | Boost short-term earnings (bonuses) at expense of long-term value |
| **Entrenchment** | Resist takeovers/mergers that would benefit shareholders |

#### 2. Shareholders vs. Debtholders (Bondholders)

| Conflict | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Asset Substitution** | Shareholders switch to riskier projects (upside to them, downside to debtholders) |
| **Underinvestment** | Near-bankruptcy firms reject +NPV projects (gains go to debtholders) |
| **Dividend Payout** | Paying excess dividends at expense of debtholders' claims |

---

### Agency Costs

**Definition**: Costs incurred to resolve the agency problem.

```
AGENCY COSTS
│
├── Monitoring Costs (by Principals/Shareholders)
│       └── Audit fees, board oversight, financial reporting
│
├── Bonding Costs (by Agents/Managers)
│       └── Performance bonds, non-compete agreements
│
└── Residual Loss
        └── Remaining value lost despite monitoring & bonding
            (the irreducible cost of the conflict)

Total Agency Cost = Monitoring Cost + Bonding Cost + Residual Loss
```

---

### Mechanisms to Reduce Agency Problems

| Mechanism | How It Works |
|---|---|
| **Performance-based Pay** | Bonuses, ESOPs align manager interests with shareholders |
| **Board of Directors** | Independent directors monitor management; Audit Committee |
| **Threat of Takeover** | Poorly managed firms become takeover targets — disciplining managers |
| **Managerial Labor Market** | Reputation effects — poor managers lose future job opportunities |
| **Debt Financing** | Debt creates obligation, reducing free cash flow available for waste |
| **Legal and Regulatory Framework** | SEBI regulations, Companies Act, Clause 49 (India) |
| **Institutional Investors** | Large shareholders (FIIs, MFs) actively monitor management |

---

### Free Cash Flow Problem

> **Free Cash Flow Hypothesis (Jensen, 1986)**: Managers with **excess free cash flow** tend to **waste it** on empire-building, acquisitions, or perks rather than returning it to shareholders.

**Solution**: Force managers to return cash via higher **dividends**, **share buybacks**, or taking on **debt**.

## 1.9 Financial Management and Other Areas of Management

| Area of Management | Relationship with Financial Management |
|---|---|
| **Accounting** | Provides financial data (P&L, Balance Sheet, Cash Flow) that FM uses for analysis and decisions |
| **Economics** | Macro (interest rates, inflation, GDP) and Micro (demand, pricing) inform financial decisions. FM = Applied Economics |
| **Marketing** | Investment in new products, advertising budgets, pricing require financial analysis |
| **Human Resources** | Compensation structures, ESOP schemes, training budgets involve financial decisions |
| **Operations/Production** | Capital investment in machinery, plant (Capital Budgeting); Inventory management (Working Capital) |
| **Strategic Management** | Corporate strategy is constrained and enabled by financial resources |
| **IT** | ERP systems (SAP, Oracle), Financial Modelling, Fintech enable efficient financial management |

### FM vs. Accounting — A Key Distinction

| Feature | **Accounting** | **Financial Management** |
|---|---|---|
| **Focus** | Recording past transactions | Making future decisions |
| **Nature** | Historical, Descriptive | Forward-looking, Prescriptive |
| **Basis** | Accrual Accounting | Cash Flows |
| **Decision Making** | Provides information | Uses information to decide |

> **💡 Key Insight**: Accounting operates on **accrual basis**, while FM focuses on **cash flows**. A firm can be profitable (accrual) but insolvent (cash crisis).

## 1.10 Corporate Governance and Corporate Social Responsibility

### Corporate Governance

**Definition**: The **system of rules, practices, and processes** by which a company is **directed and controlled**, balancing interests of shareholders, management, customers, suppliers, financiers, government, and community.

> **"Corporate Governance is the way power is exercised over corporate entities."** — Cadbury Committee (1992)

#### Key Principles of Good Corporate Governance (OECD):

| Principle | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Transparency** | Timely, accurate, and complete disclosure of information |
| **Accountability** | Management accountable to shareholders and board |
| **Fairness** | Equal treatment of all shareholders (minority & majority) |
| **Responsibility** | Compliance with laws; responsibility to all stakeholders |
| **Independence** | Independent directors free from conflicts of interest |

#### Corporate Governance Framework in India:

| Regulation/Body | Role |
|---|---|
| **Companies Act, 2013** | Defines director responsibilities, audit requirements, disclosure norms |
| **SEBI (LODR) Regulations, 2015** | Listing obligations and disclosure requirements for listed companies |
| **Clause 49 / Regulation 17-27** | Board composition, Audit Committee, Related Party Transactions |
| **Audit Committee** | Oversees financial reporting and internal controls |

#### Board Composition (Listed Companies — India):
- At least **1/3rd Independent Directors** (if Chairman is Non-Executive)
- At least **50% Independent Directors** (if Chairman is Executive/Promoter)
- At least **1 Woman Director**
- **Audit Committee**: ≥3 directors, 2/3rd independent

#### Famous Governance Failures:
- **Enron (2001)**: Creative accounting, off-balance-sheet fraud
- **Satyam (2009)**: India's biggest accounting fraud — fabricated accounts of ₹7,800 Cr
- **IL&FS (2018)**: Systemic governance failure in a systemically important NBFC

---

### Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)

**Definition**: CSR is a management concept whereby companies **integrate social and environmental concerns** into their business operations and interactions with stakeholders.

#### CSR in India — Companies Act 2013, Section 135:

**Applicability** — A company must constitute a CSR Committee and spend on CSR if it meets ANY of:
```
Net Worth  ≥ ₹500 Crore
OR Turnover ≥ ₹1,000 Crore
OR Net Profit ≥ ₹5 Crore  (in preceding financial year)
```
**Mandatory CSR Spend**: **2% of average net profit** of the last 3 financial years.

**CSR Activities (Schedule VII)**: Education, healthcare, hunger eradication, environmental sustainability, gender equality, rural development, sports, etc.

---

#### Carroll's Pyramid of CSR (4 Levels):

```
          ▲
         /│\
        / │ \   PHILANTHROPIC (Be a good corporate citizen)
       /  │  \
      /───┼───\
     /    │    \ ETHICAL (Do what is right, fair, just)
    /     │     \
   /──────┼──────\
  /       │       \ LEGAL (Obey the law)
 /        │        \
/─────────┼─────────\
          │          ECONOMIC (Be profitable — the foundation)
```

| Level | Responsibility | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Economic** | Make a profit (foundation) | Generate revenue, create jobs |
| **Legal** | Comply with laws | Pay taxes, follow labor laws |
| **Ethical** | Do what's right beyond the law | Fair wages, no discrimination |
| **Philanthropic** | Give back to society | Schools, hospitals, rural development |

---

#### Corporate Governance vs. CSR:

| Feature | **Corporate Governance** | **CSR** |
|---|---|---|
| **Focus** | Internal — how company is directed | External — how company impacts society |
| **Primary Beneficiary** | Shareholders and stakeholders | Society, community, environment |
| **Nature** | Regulatory / Mandatory (in part) | Voluntary + Mandatory (India CSR law) |
| **Goal** | Accountability and transparency | Sustainable and ethical business |

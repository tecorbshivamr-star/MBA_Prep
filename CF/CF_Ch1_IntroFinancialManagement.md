## 🟢 BEGINNER LEVEL — "What is this about?"

Imagine you want to start a new business—let's say, a chain of cafes. You immediately face three massive questions:
1. **What should I buy?** (Coffee machines, furniture, prime real estate). This is the **Investment Decision**.
2. **Where do I get the money from?** (My own savings, a bank loan, or selling shares to investors). This is the **Financing Decision**.
3. **What do I do with the profits?** (Reinvest them to open more cafes, or pay them out to the investors as a reward). This is the **Dividend Decision**.

**Corporate Finance** is simply the study of how companies make these three exact decisions to grow the value of the business.

## 🟡 INTERMEDIATE LEVEL — "Key Concepts & Frameworks"

### 1. The Ultimate Goal of Financial Management
- **Old View (Profit Maximization):** Just make as much profit as possible. *Flaw:* This ignores risk and the timing of cash flows. A company might take huge gambles to show short-term profits but go bankrupt next year.
- **Modern View (Shareholder Wealth Maximization - SWM):** The real goal is to increase the company's stock price over the long term. This automatically means the market believes the company is generating stable, risk-adjusted, long-term cash flows.

### 2. The Agency Problem
In large corporations, the owners (shareholders) don't run the company. They hire managers (CEO, CFO) to run it. 
- The "Agency Problem" occurs when managers act in their *own* self-interest (e.g., buying a private corporate jet for themselves) instead of maximizing shareholder wealth. 
- *Solution:* Give managers stock options so their wealth is tied to the shareholders' wealth, and enforce strict Corporate Governance rules.

### 3. Financial Markets Overview
To raise money, companies go to financial markets:
- **Money Market:** For short-term needs (< 1 year). e.g., Commercial Papers.
- **Capital Market:** For long-term needs (> 1 year). e.g., issuing Shares (Equity) or Bonds (Debt).

## 🔴 ADVANCED / EXAM LEVEL — "How to write it in the exam?"

### Q. Discuss the three fundamental decisions in Financial Management. Why is Wealth Maximization considered a superior objective compared to Profit Maximization? (15 Marks)

**1. Introduction:**
Financial management is the application of general management principles to the financial resources of an enterprise. It revolves around three core, interrelated decisions.

**2. The Three Fundamental Decisions:**
*   **A. Investment Decision (Capital Budgeting):** Deciding which long-term assets the firm should invest in. This requires evaluating future cash flows and assessing risk (e.g., using Net Present Value - NPV). The hurdle is ensuring the return on investment exceeds the cost of capital.
*   **B. Financing Decision (Capital Structure):** Determining the optimal mix of debt and equity to finance the investments. Debt is cheaper (due to tax shields) but increases bankruptcy risk. The goal is to minimize the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC).
*   **C. Dividend Decision (Dividend Policy):** Deciding what proportion of earnings to distribute to shareholders as dividends versus retaining for reinvestment. 

**3. Profit Maximization vs. Wealth Maximization (The Superiority Argument):**
*Draw this comparison in a table or structured bullet points.*
*   **Ambiguity vs. Clarity:** "Profit" is an ambiguous accounting term that can be manipulated (e.g., changing depreciation methods). "Shareholder Wealth" (Market Price per Share × Number of Shares) is an objective market reality.
*   **Timing of Returns:** Profit maximization ignores the time value of money. (Earning ₹1 lakh today is better than earning ₹1 lakh in 5 years). Wealth maximization discounts future cash flows to present value.
*   **Risk/Quality of Benefits:** Profit maximization ignores the risk attached to cash flows. A high-profit/high-risk project might destroy shareholder wealth. Wealth maximization explicitly incorporates a risk premium into the discount rate.

**Conclusion:**
While profit maximization serves as a basic operational benchmark, Shareholder Wealth Maximization (SWM) is the universally accepted normative goal of corporate finance, as it perfectly aligns management decisions with the ultimate welfare of the company's owners.

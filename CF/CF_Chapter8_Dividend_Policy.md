## 8.2 Introduction

### What is Dividend Policy?

**Dividend** is the portion of a firm's **net profit distributed to shareholders** as a return on their investment.

**Dividend Policy** is the set of guidelines a firm uses to decide:
1. **How much** of earnings to distribute as dividends?
2. **How much** to retain for reinvestment (retained earnings)?
3. **What form** should the dividend take (cash, stock, etc.)?

```
Net Profit (EAT)
      │
      ├──► DIVIDENDS (paid to shareholders)
      │
      └──► RETAINED EARNINGS (reinvested in the firm)

Payout Ratio + Retention Ratio = 1 (100%)
```

### The Central Question of Dividend Policy:

> *"Does dividend policy affect the market value of the firm (share price)?"*

Three theories give different answers — this is the crux of Chapter 8.

---

### Dividend vs. Retained Earnings — The Trade-off:

| Factor | Pay Dividend | Retain Earnings |
|---|---|---|
| **Shareholders** | Immediate income, certainty | Capital gain (uncertain, deferred) |
| **Firm** | Less retained funds for growth | More internal funds for investment |
| **Tax** | Dividends taxed as income | Capital gains (taxed at lower rate) |
| **Signal** | High dividend = profitable firm | Low dividend = growth firm |
| **Flexibility** | Reduces future financial flexibility | Preserves financial flexibility |

---

## 8.3 Factors Determining Dividend Policy

| Factor | How It Influences Dividend |
|---|---|
| **Earnings Stability** | Stable earnings → Higher, consistent dividends; Volatile earnings → Low/erratic dividends |
| **Growth Opportunities** | High-growth firms → Retain more (reinvest in +NPV projects); Mature firms → Pay more |
| **Cash Position (Liquidity)** | High cash → Can pay more dividends; Cash-scarce firms → Pay less even if profitable |
| **Debt Obligations** | Loan covenants may restrict dividend payments; High debt → Need cash for repayments |
| **Tax Considerations** | Dividend tax vs. capital gains tax preferences of shareholders |
| **Shareholders' Preference** | Income-oriented (e.g., retired) prefer dividends; Growth-oriented prefer reinvestment |
| **Control** | Issuing new equity (due to low retention) dilutes control → Prefer high retention |
| **Legal Restrictions** | Companies Act: Dividends paid only from current profits or specific reserves |
| **Inflation** | Rising prices → Need more retained earnings to replace assets → Lower dividends |
| **Investment Opportunities** | More profitable projects available → Retain more; fewer projects → Pay more |
| **Market Signals** | Dividend cuts seen negatively; increases seen positively by market |
| **Industry Norms** | Firms follow industry-average payout ratios to avoid negative signalling |

## 8.4 Measures of Dividend Policies

### Key Dividend Metrics:

| Measure | Formula | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| **Dividend Per Share (DPS)** | Total Dividends / Number of Shares | Rupee dividend per share |
| **Dividend Payout Ratio (DPR)** | DPS / EPS = Total Dividends / EAT | % of earnings paid as dividend |
| **Retention Ratio (b)** | 1 − DPR | % of earnings retained |
| **Dividend Yield** | DPS / Market Price per Share × 100 | Return from dividends as % of share price |
| **Earnings Per Share (EPS)** | EAT / Number of Equity Shares | Earnings per share |
| **Dividend Cover** | EPS / DPS = 1 / DPR | Times dividend is covered by earnings |

### Sustainable Growth Rate:
```
g = ROE × b = ROE × Retention Ratio

Where:
  ROE = Return on Equity = EAT / Equity
  b   = Retention Ratio (ploughback ratio)
```

> 💡 Higher retention → Higher sustainable growth rate → Higher future EPS (but lower current dividends).

### Example:
> EAT = ₹5,00,000; Shares = 1,00,000; DPS = ₹3; Market Price = ₹60; ROE = 15%.

```
EPS           = 5,00,000 / 1,00,000 = ₹5.00
DPR           = 3 / 5 = 60%
Retention (b) = 1 − 0.60 = 40%
Dividend Yield = 3 / 60 × 100 = 5%
Dividend Cover = 5 / 3 = 1.67 times
Growth Rate (g) = 15% × 40% = 6%
```

## 8.5 Theories of Dividend

### Three Competing Views:

| Theory | Proponent | Does Dividend Policy Matter? | Optimal Policy |
|---|---|---|---|
| **M&M Irrelevance** | Modigliani & Miller (1961) | ❌ No — Irrelevant | Any policy |
| **Walter's Model** | James E. Walter | ✅ Yes — Relevant | Depends on r vs Ke |
| **Gordon's Model** | Myron Gordon | ✅ Yes — Relevant | High dividend preferred |

---

### Theory 1: M&M Dividend Irrelevance Theory ✅ (Most Important)

**Proponents**: Modigliani & Miller (1961)

**Core Argument**: In a **perfect capital market**, dividend policy is **irrelevant** to firm value. The total return to shareholders (dividends + capital gains) is the same regardless of dividend policy.

#### Assumptions of M&M:
1. **Perfect capital markets** (no taxes, no transaction costs)
2. **Rational investors** (prefer more wealth to less)
3. **No flotation costs** on new equity issues
4. **Certain future cash flows** (risk can be ignored for this analysis)
5. **Investment policy is fixed** (dividend decision does not change investments)

#### The M&M Argument:

```
Step 1: Firm pays dividend D₁ to existing shareholders
Step 2: Firm needs to fund its investment (unchanged)
Step 3: Since less retained earnings (due to dividend), firm issues NEW equity
Step 4: New shareholders' capital replaces exactly what was paid as dividend
Step 5: Existing shareholders lose capital gain = dividend received
        → Total wealth unchanged: Dividend ↑ by ₹X, Capital Gain ↓ by ₹X

∴ Firm value is independent of dividend policy
```

#### M&M Valuation Formula:
```
P₀ = (D₁ + P₁) / (1 + Ke)

Where:
  P₀ = Current share price
  D₁ = Dividend at end of Year 1
  P₁ = Share price at end of Year 1
  Ke = Required rate of return (cost of equity)
```

**Key Conclusion:**
> Any increase in dividends is exactly offset by a fall in share price (capital loss). Total return is unchanged.

#### Criticisms of M&M (Why Dividends DO Matter in Practice):

| Criticism | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Taxes** | Dividends taxed differently from capital gains; investors have tax preferences |
| **Transaction Costs** | Selling shares to create "homemade dividend" has brokerage costs |
| **Flotation Costs** | Issuing new equity to replace dividend is costly (underwriting fees) |
| **Information Asymmetry** | Managers know more than investors → Dividends signal quality (Signalling Theory) |
| **Bird-in-Hand** | Investors prefer certain dividends over uncertain future capital gains (Gordon & Lintner) |
| **Clientele Effect** | Different investors prefer different dividend levels; changing policy upsets clientele |

---

### Theory 2: Walter's Model

**Proponent**: James E. Walter (1963)

**Core Argument**: Dividend policy IS relevant. The optimal policy depends on the **relationship between the firm's internal rate of return (r) and the cost of equity (Ke)**.

#### Walter's Formula:
```
P₀ = [D + (r/Ke) × (E − D)] / Ke

Where:
  P₀ = Market price per share
  D  = Dividend per share
  E  = Earnings per share
  r  = Firm's internal rate of return (return on investment)
  Ke = Cost of equity (required rate of return)
  (E − D) = Retained earnings per share
```

#### Walter's Decision Rules:

| Condition | Firm Type | Optimal Dividend Policy | Logic |
|---|---|---|---|
| **r > Ke** | **Growth Firm** | **Pay ZERO dividend** (retain everything) | Firm earns more than shareholders can → Retain and reinvest |
| **r = Ke** | **Normal Firm** | **Dividend policy irrelevant** | Firm earns exactly what shareholders need → Any payout ratio is fine |
| **r < Ke** | **Declining Firm** | **Pay 100% dividend** | Firm earns less than shareholders can → Pay out so they invest elsewhere |

#### Walter's Model — Numerical Example:

> **Growth Firm**: E = ₹10, Ke = 10%, r = 15%
>
> Calculate P₀ at D = ₹0, ₹5, ₹10.

```
At D = ₹0 (retain all):
P₀ = [0 + (0.15/0.10) × (10−0)] / 0.10
   = [0 + 1.5 × 10] / 0.10
   = 15 / 0.10 = ₹150

At D = ₹5:
P₀ = [5 + 1.5 × (10−5)] / 0.10
   = [5 + 7.5] / 0.10
   = 12.5 / 0.10 = ₹125

At D = ₹10 (pay all):
P₀ = [10 + 1.5 × (10−10)] / 0.10
   = [10 + 0] / 0.10
   = 10 / 0.10 = ₹100
```

**Conclusion (r > Ke = Growth Firm)**: **Lower dividend → Higher share price** → Optimal policy: Retain ALL earnings (D = 0).

> Now try **Declining Firm** (r = 8%, Ke = 10%):

```
At D = ₹0:
P₀ = [0 + (0.08/0.10) × 10] / 0.10 = 8/0.10 = ₹80

At D = ₹10:
P₀ = [10 + 0.8 × 0] / 0.10 = 10/0.10 = ₹100
```

**Conclusion (r < Ke = Declining Firm)**: **Higher dividend → Higher share price** → Optimal policy: Pay ALL earnings as dividend.

#### Assumptions and Limitations of Walter's Model:

| Assumption | Limitation |
|---|---|
| Financing only through retained earnings (no external equity/debt) | Unrealistic — firms use mixed financing |
| r is constant forever | Returns vary across projects over time |
| Ke is constant | Leverage changes affect Ke |
| All earnings either paid as dividend or reinvested | Simplified |

### Theory 3: Gordon's Model

**Proponent**: Myron Gordon (1959, 1962) — **Bird-in-Hand Theory**

**Core Argument**: Dividend policy IS relevant. Investors **prefer current dividends** over future capital gains because:
- Dividends are **certain** (bird in hand)
- Future capital gains are **uncertain** (bird in bush)
- Therefore, a **higher dividend** leads to a **higher share price**

#### Gordon's Formula (Constant Growth DDM):
```
P₀ = E(1 − b) / (Ke − br)

Where:
  P₀  = Market price per share
  E   = Earnings per share
  b   = Retention ratio (ploughback ratio)
  (1−b) = Dividend payout ratio
  r   = Rate of return on investment
  Ke  = Cost of equity
  br  = g = Sustainable growth rate (g = ROE × b)
  Ke > br (condition for finite price)
```

> Note: E(1−b) = DPS (Dividend Per Share) and br = g, so this reduces to **P₀ = D₁ / (Ke − g)** — the standard Gordon Growth Model.

#### Gordon's Decision Rules — Same as Walter's:

| Condition | Optimal Policy | Share Price |
|---|---|---|
| **r > Ke** | Pay lower dividend (higher b) → g ↑ → P₀ ↑ | Higher price at lower payout |
| **r = Ke** | Dividend policy irrelevant | Price unchanged |
| **r < Ke** | Pay higher dividend (lower b) → P₀ ↑ | Higher price at higher payout |

#### Gordon's Model — Numerical Example:

> E = ₹10, Ke = 10%, r = 15%
> Calculate P₀ at b = 0.80, 0.50, 0.20.

```
At b = 0.80 (retain 80%, pay 20%):
DPS = 10 × (1−0.80) = ₹2
g   = 0.80 × 15%    = 12%
P₀  = 2 / (0.10 − 0.12) → Denominator negative! ← Ke < g (not valid)

At b = 0.60 (retain 60%, pay 40%):
DPS = 10 × 0.40 = ₹4
g   = 0.60 × 15% = 9%
P₀  = 4 / (0.10 − 0.09) = 4 / 0.01 = ₹400

At b = 0.40 (retain 40%, pay 60%):
DPS = 10 × 0.60 = ₹6
g   = 0.40 × 15% = 6%
P₀  = 6 / (0.10 − 0.06) = 6 / 0.04 = ₹150

At b = 0.20 (retain 20%, pay 80%):
DPS = 10 × 0.80 = ₹8
g   = 0.20 × 15% = 3%
P₀  = 8 / (0.10 − 0.03) = 8 / 0.07 = ₹114.3
```

**Conclusion (r > Ke)**: Higher retention → Higher P₀. Optimal = High retention.

---

### Comparison of All Three Theories:

| Feature | **M&M** | **Walter's Model** | **Gordon's Model** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Dividend Relevance** | Irrelevant | Relevant | Relevant |
| **Key Variable** | — | r vs Ke | r vs Ke |
| **Assumes Perfect Markets** | ✅ Yes | Partial | ❌ No |
| **Growth Firm (r>Ke)** | Any policy = same | D=0 optimal | High retention optimal |
| **Declining Firm (r<Ke)** | Any policy = same | D=100% optimal | High payout optimal |
| **Investor Preference** | Indifferent | Value-based | Bird-in-Hand (dividends preferred) |
| **Taxes/Transaction Costs** | Ignored | Ignored | Ignored |
| **Formula** | P₀=(D₁+P₁)/(1+Ke) | P₀=[D+(r/Ke)(E−D)]/Ke | P₀=E(1−b)/(Ke−br) |

---

### Other Relevant Concepts:

#### Signalling Theory (Information Content of Dividends):
> Dividend **increases** signal management's confidence in future earnings → Share price rises.
> Dividend **cuts** signal trouble → Share price falls.
> Managers use dividends as a **signalling mechanism**.

#### Clientele Effect:
> Different shareholders have different dividend preferences based on tax bracket, income needs.
> Firms attract a **clientele** of investors who match their dividend policy.
> Changing dividend policy may cause investors to sell → Temporary price disruption.

#### Bird-in-Hand Fallacy (M&M rebuttal):
> M&M argue the bird-in-hand argument is a **fallacy** — risk of the firm's cash flows determines risk, not whether earnings are paid as dividends or retained. Both come from the same risky cash flow stream.

## 8.6 Forms of Dividend Policies

### Types of Dividend:

| Form | Description | Impact |
|---|---|---|
| **Cash Dividend** | Most common; paid in cash per share | Reduces cash and retained earnings |
| **Stock Dividend (Bonus Shares)** | Additional shares issued instead of cash | No cash outflow; increases shares; dilutes EPS |
| **Scrip Dividend** | Promissory note to pay dividend later | Used when cash is scarce |
| **Property Dividend** | Payment in non-cash assets (rare) | Asset disposed off; reduces book value |
| **Liquidating Dividend** | Return of capital (not earnings) | Reduces paid-up capital; firm winding down |
| **Share Buyback (Repurchase)** | Company buys back its own shares | Reduces shares outstanding; ↑ EPS; tax-efficient |

---

### Stock Dividend (Bonus Shares) vs. Cash Dividend:

| Feature | **Cash Dividend** | **Bonus Shares / Stock Dividend** |
|---|---|---|
| **Cash Flow** | Reduces cash | No cash outflow |
| **Share Count** | Unchanged | Increases |
| **EPS** | Unchanged | Decreases (diluted) |
| **Share Price** | Falls by dividend amount (ex-div) | Falls proportionately |
| **Shareholder Wealth** | Same in perfect market | Same in perfect market |
| **Signal** | Company has cash | Company wants to conserve cash |
| **Tax** | Taxable as income | Usually tax-free (return of capital) |
| **Use** | When firm has surplus cash | When firm needs to conserve cash |

---

### Share Buyback / Repurchase:

**Mechanics**: Company uses cash to buy its own shares from the open market or through a tender offer → Shares are cancelled → Fewer shares outstanding.

**Effects:**
```
Shares outstanding ↓ → EPS ↑ (same earnings, fewer shares)
                     → P/E ratio adjusts
                     → Share price may rise
```

**Reasons for Buyback:**
1. Return excess cash to shareholders (tax-efficient vs dividend)
2. Signal that shares are undervalued
3. Improve EPS and ROE metrics
4. Reduce dilution from ESOP exercises
5. Alter capital structure (replace equity with debt)

**India**: SEBI regulates buybacks; max 10% of paid-up capital without shareholder approval; 25% with approval.

## 8.7 Dividend Policies in Practice

### Policy 1: Stable Dividend Policy ✅ (Most Common)

**Definition**: Pay a **fixed dividend per share** each year, regardless of fluctuations in earnings.

```
DPS = Constant (e.g., ₹5 per share every year)
EPS fluctuates but DPS remains stable
```

**Merits:**
- Creates investor confidence and loyalty
- Signals financial stability
- Attracts income-seeking investors
- Supports share price stability

**Demerits:**
- May pay dividend even in loss years (from reserves)
- Less flexibility when earnings drop sharply

---

### Policy 2: Constant Payout Ratio Policy

**Definition**: Pay a **fixed percentage of earnings** as dividend each year.

```
DPR = Constant (e.g., 40% of EAT every year)
DPS fluctuates with EPS
```

**Merits:**
- Directly tied to earnings — sustainable
- Aligns with firm's financial performance

**Demerits:**
- Dividend fluctuates — investors dislike uncertainty
- Bad signal when earnings (and dividends) fall

---

### Policy 3: Stable Dividend + Extra (Hybrid)

**Definition**: Pay a **low stable regular dividend** plus an **extra/special dividend** in good years.

```
DPS = Low Base Dividend + Extra Dividend (if earnings are exceptional)
```

> Used by cyclical firms (auto, steel) to manage expectations while sharing windfalls.

---

### Policy 4: Residual Dividend Policy

**Definition**: Dividends are **paid only after all profitable investment opportunities** have been funded from earnings.

```
Residual Dividend = Earnings − (Equity needed for target capital structure × New Investment)

If Residual > 0 → Pay dividend
If Residual = 0 → No dividend (all retained for investment)
```

**Logic**: Investment decisions come first; dividend is whatever is "left over."

**Merits:**
- Consistent with value maximisation (invest in all +NPV projects first)
- Minimises need for external equity financing

**Demerits:**
- Highly variable dividends (investors dislike)
- Difficult to predict or signal future stability

---

### Dividend Smoothing / Lintner's Model:

**John Lintner (1956)** — Observed that managers:
1. Have a **target payout ratio** in mind
2. **Adjust dividends slowly** toward the target (partial adjustment)
3. Are **reluctant to cut dividends** (avoid negative signal at all costs)
4. Increase dividends only when confident earnings are **permanently higher**

```
Lintner's Partial Adjustment Model:
ΔD = α + c × (Target Dividend − Previous Dividend)

Where:
  α = constant (usually small)
  c = speed of adjustment (0 < c < 1; lower = slower adjustment)
  Target Dividend = Target Payout Ratio × Current EPS
```

### Practical Dividend Policy — India Context:

| Aspect | India Practice |
|---|---|
| **Legal Framework** | Companies Act 2013 — dividends from current year profits or free reserves |
| **Interim vs Final** | Board declares interim dividend; shareholders approve final dividend at AGM |
| **Payment Timeline** | Dividend must be paid within 30 days of declaration |
| **Unpaid Dividend** | Transferred to IEPF (Investor Education and Protection Fund) after 7 years |
| **Bonus Shares** | Common way to reward shareholders; requires free reserves |
| **Buyback** | SEBI Buyback Regulations; tax-efficient for shareholders |

## 🟢 BEGINNER LEVEL — "What is this about?"

**Capital Structure** is simply the mix of Debt (Loans/Bonds) and Equity (Shares) a company uses to fund itself.
- If you use 100% Equity, you have zero debt. You can never go bankrupt, but equity is expensive.
- If you use 90% Debt, you get a massive tax shield (interest saves taxes), but one bad month of sales and the bank will seize your factory (bankruptcy risk).

**The Big Question of this Chapter:** Is there a "perfect" or "optimal" mix of Debt and Equity that minimizes the company's WACC and maximizes the company's total value?

---

## 🟡 INTERMEDIATE LEVEL — "Key Concepts & Frameworks"

Different economists have argued about this for decades. Here are the 4 main theories:

### 1. The Net Income (NI) Approach (Traditional)
Says that since Debt is cheaper than Equity, adding more Debt will *always* lower your WACC and increase the value of the firm. (Conclusion: 100% Debt is optimal).

### 2. Modigliani-Miller (MM) Approach — WITHOUT TAXES
Two Nobel laureates (MM) shocked the world by proving mathematically that in a perfect world (no taxes, no bankruptcy costs), **Capital Structure is completely Irrelevant.** 
The value of a firm depends on its operating profits (EBIT), not how you slice the pie between debt and equity. (Value of Unlevered Firm = Value of Levered Firm).

### 3. Modigliani-Miller (MM) Approach — WITH TAXES
MM later admitted that in the real world, taxes exist. Because interest on debt is tax-deductible, debt provides a "Tax Shield." Therefore, the value of a firm *increases* as you add debt. (Value of Levered Firm = Value of Unlevered Firm + PV of Tax Shield).

### 4. Trade-Off Theory (The Realistic View)
You can't have 100% debt. The Trade-Off theory says you balance two things:
1. The **Tax Shield benefits** of adding debt.
2. The **Financial Distress (Bankruptcy) costs** of adding debt.
The "Optimal Capital Structure" is the exact point where the tax benefit of one more rupee of debt is perfectly offset by the increased risk of bankruptcy.

## 🔴 ADVANCED / EXAM LEVEL — "How to write it in the exam?"

### Q. Discuss the Modigliani-Miller (MM) Hypothesis regarding capital structure. How does the introduction of corporate taxes alter their conclusion? (15 Marks)

**1. Introduction:**
The debate on capital structure revolves around whether modifying the debt-equity mix can impact the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) and the overall Value of the Firm (V). The most famous contribution to this debate is the Modigliani-Miller (MM) Hypothesis.

**2. MM Hypothesis - Proposition I (Without Taxes):**
In 1958, Modigliani and Miller argued that under a set of perfect market assumptions (no taxes, no transaction costs, symmetric information, and individuals can borrow at the same rate as corporations), **capital structure is completely irrelevant**.
*   **The Logic (Arbitrage):** The value of a firm is determined entirely by its underlying earning power (EBIT) and the risk of its assets, not by how it is financed. If a levered firm (with debt) was somehow valued higher than an identical unlevered firm (no debt), investors would engage in "homemade leverage" (arbitrage) by selling the overvalued firm and buying the undervalued one, bringing the values back to equality.
*   **Equation:** $V_L = V_U$ (Value of Levered Firm = Value of Unlevered Firm). WACC remains constant regardless of debt levels.

**3. MM Hypothesis - Proposition II (The Cost of Equity):**
MM argued that as a firm takes on more cheap debt, the financial risk to the equity shareholders increases. Consequently, shareholders demand a higher return. 
*   **The Logic:** The benefit of using cheaper debt is exactly and entirely offset by the rising cost of equity (Ke). Therefore, the overall WACC never drops.

**4. The Introduction of Corporate Taxes (MM 1963 Correction):**
In reality, corporate taxes exist, and tax laws treat debt and equity differently. Interest paid on debt is a tax-deductible expense, while dividends paid on equity are not. This creates an **Interest Tax Shield**.
*   **Revised Logic:** Because the government effectively subsidizes a portion of the debt through tax savings, the levered firm has higher cash flows available to its investors than the unlevered firm.
*   **Revised Equation:** $V_L = V_U + (T \times D)$ (Value of Levered Firm = Value of Unlevered + Present Value of Tax Shield).
*   **New Conclusion:** Under MM with taxes, WACC continuously declines as debt increases. The theoretical conclusion is that a firm should employ 100% debt to maximize its value.

**5. Conclusion & The Trade-Off Reality:**
While MM with taxes suggests 100% debt, real-world firms don't do this because of Financial Distress (Bankruptcy) costs and Agency costs. The modern "Trade-Off Theory" combines MM's tax shield with bankruptcy costs to find a balanced, optimal debt-equity ratio.

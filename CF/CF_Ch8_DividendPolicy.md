## 🟢 BEGINNER LEVEL — "What is this about?"

At the end of the year, a company makes ₹100 Crores in Net Profit. The Board of Directors must make a critical choice:
- **Option A (Dividend):** Give the ₹100 Crores to the shareholders as cash. Shareholders are happy *today*, but the company has no money to grow.
- **Option B (Retained Earnings):** Keep the ₹100 Crores and use it to build a new factory. Shareholders get zero cash today, but if the factory succeeds, the stock price will go up, making them richer *tomorrow*.

**Dividend Policy** is the strategy a company uses to decide exactly how much to pay out versus how much to retain.

---

## 🟡 INTERMEDIATE LEVEL — "Key Concepts & Frameworks"

### 1. The Core Conflict: Growth vs. Income
- **Mature Companies (like ITC or Coal India):** They don't have many new fast-growth projects. They should pay high dividends.
- **Growth Companies (like Tesla or Zomato):** They need every rupee they can get to fund their rapid expansion. They should pay zero dividends and retain everything.

### 2. Major Dividend Theories
- **Dividend Irrelevance (Modigliani-Miller):** In a perfect world, it doesn't matter. If a company pays a dividend, its stock price drops by the exact amount of the dividend. The total wealth of the shareholder remains identical.
- **Bird-in-the-Hand Theory (Gordon/Lintner):** Investors hate risk. A cash dividend *today* (a bird in the hand) is certain. A promise of capital gains *tomorrow* (two in the bush) is uncertain. Therefore, investors will pay a premium for stocks with high dividends.
- **Signaling Theory:** Managers know more about the company than the public. If management suddenly raises the dividend, they are "signaling" extreme confidence that future profits will be high. The stock price shoots up.

### 3. Types of Dividends
- **Cash Dividend:** Actual money deposited in the shareholder's bank account.
- **Stock Dividend (Bonus Shares):** Giving shareholders extra shares instead of cash (e.g., a 1:1 bonus means you get 1 free share for every 1 you own). The stock price halves, but you have double the shares. Wealth remains exactly the same.

## 🔴 ADVANCED / EXAM LEVEL — "How to write it in the exam?"

### Q. Critically evaluate Walter's Model of Dividend Policy. How does the relationship between Internal Rate of Return (r) and Cost of Capital (Ke) determine the optimum dividend payout ratio? (15 Marks)

**1. Introduction:**
Professor James E. Walter proposed a highly influential model showing that dividend policy is relevant and actively affects the valuation of the firm. His model connects a firm's internal profitability (r) with the market's required rate of return (Ke).

**2. The Formula:**
$P = \frac{D + \frac{r}{Ke}(E - D)}{Ke}$
Where:
*   P = Market Price of the equity share
*   D = Dividend per share
*   E = Earnings per share (E - D = Retained Earnings)
*   r = Internal Rate of Return on the firm's investments
*   Ke = Cost of Equity Capital

**3. The Logic & Optimum Payout Ratio:**
Walter argues that the optimal dividend policy depends entirely on the relationship between **r** (what the company can earn on retained money) and **Ke** (what the shareholders can earn if the money is given to them to invest elsewhere).

*   **Case 1: Growth Firms (r > Ke)**
    *   The firm earns a *higher* return on investments than what shareholders expect.
    *   *Action:* The firm should retain 100% of its earnings to maximize shareholder wealth.
    *   *Optimum Payout Ratio = 0%.*

*   **Case 2: Declining Firms (r < Ke)**
    *   The firm earns a *lower* return on investments than what shareholders can earn elsewhere. (The firm is destroying value by reinvesting).
    *   *Action:* The firm should distribute 100% of its earnings as dividends so shareholders can invest the money in better opportunities.
    *   *Optimum Payout Ratio = 100%.*

*   **Case 3: Normal Firms (r = Ke)**
    *   The firm earns exactly what the shareholders expect.
    *   *Action:* The dividend policy is irrelevant. Whether the firm pays 0%, 50%, or 100% payout, the market price (P) of the share will remain absolutely constant.

**4. Critical Evaluation & Assumptions:**
While mathematically elegant, Walter's model relies on strict, unrealistic assumptions:
*   It assumes all investment is financed exclusively by retained earnings (no external debt or new equity).
*   It assumes both 'r' and 'Ke' remain constant, regardless of the size of the investment or the risk profile of the firm.
*   It assumes a 100% retention rate is possible without upsetting shareholders who rely on current income (ignoring the clientele effect). 

**Conclusion:**
Despite its theoretical limitations, Walter's model provides a brilliant conceptual framework proving that retention is only justified if the firm's internal projects create positive NPV (where r > Ke).

## 🟢 BEGINNER LEVEL — "What is this about?"

Imagine you manage a factory and you have ₹50 Lakhs to spend. You have two options:
- Option A: Buy a new machine that makes 1,000 extra shoes a day.
- Option B: Buy a new delivery truck to save on shipping costs.

You can't do both. How do you decide which one makes more financial sense? You use **Capital Budgeting**. It is the process of planning and deciding whether large, long-term investments are worth the money. 

---

## 🟡 INTERMEDIATE LEVEL — "Key Concepts & Frameworks"

There are 4 main techniques to evaluate a project:

### 1. Payback Period (PBP)
- **What is it?** How many years will it take to get my initial money back?
- **Formula:** Initial Investment / Annual Cash Inflow.
- **Rule:** Choose the project with the shortest payback period.
- **Flaw:** It completely ignores the Time Value of Money and ignores any cash made *after* the payback period.

### 2. Net Present Value (NPV) — THE MOST IMPORTANT
- **What is it?** It discounts ALL future cash inflows back to today's value, and subtracts the initial cost.
- **Formula:** PV of all Inflows - PV of Outflows (Initial Cost).
- **Rule:** If NPV > 0 (Positive), **ACCEPT**. If NPV < 0 (Negative), **REJECT**. 

### 3. Internal Rate of Return (IRR)
- **What is it?** It is the specific discount rate (%) at which your project breaks even (where NPV = 0). It's the project's own rate of return.
- **Rule:** If IRR > WACC (Cost of Capital), **ACCEPT**. If IRR < WACC, **REJECT**.

### 4. Profitability Index (PI)
- **What is it?** It's the ratio of payoff to investment.
- **Formula:** PV of Inflows / Initial Investment.
- **Rule:** If PI > 1, **ACCEPT**. Useful when you have limited money and need to rank projects.

## 🔴 ADVANCED / EXAM LEVEL — "How to write it in the exam?"

### Q. What is Capital Budgeting? Compare Net Present Value (NPV) and Internal Rate of Return (IRR). Why is NPV generally considered a superior method? (15 Marks)

**1. Definition of Capital Budgeting:**
Capital Budgeting is the process of identifying, evaluating, and selecting long-term investments whose returns are expected to extend beyond one year. It is a critical, irreversible decision that shapes the future risk and profitability of the firm.

**2. NPV vs. IRR (Comparison):**
*   **Metric:** NPV measures the absolute amount of wealth created in currency (e.g., ₹5 Lakhs). IRR measures the relative return as a percentage (e.g., 14%).
*   **Reinvestment Assumption (Crucial Difference):**
    *   **NPV** assumes that all intermediate cash flows are reinvested at the firm's Cost of Capital (WACC). This is a highly realistic assumption.
    *   **IRR** assumes that all intermediate cash flows are reinvested at the project's own IRR. If a project has an unusually high IRR (say, 30%), it is unrealistic to assume the firm can constantly reinvest cash flows at 30% elsewhere.
*   **Multiple IRRs:** If a project has non-conventional cash flows (e.g., it requires a massive cleanup cost in year 5, meaning a negative cash flow), the mathematical equation will produce multiple IRRs, making the method useless. NPV handles changing cash flow signs effortlessly.

**3. Why NPV is Superior (The Golden Rule):**
When evaluating mutually exclusive projects (e.g., choosing either Project A or Project B), NPV and IRR can sometimes give conflicting rankings (due to differences in project scale or timing of cash flows).
Whenever NPV and IRR conflict, a financial manager must **always trust NPV**. Why? Because the ultimate goal of Financial Management is *Shareholder Wealth Maximization*. NPV directly measures the absolute Rupee addition to shareholder wealth, whereas IRR only measures a percentage return, which could be misleading on a smaller project base. 

**Conclusion:**
While IRR is popular among executives because percentages are easy to understand and communicate, NPV is the academically sound, robust method that aligns perfectly with the core objective of corporate finance.

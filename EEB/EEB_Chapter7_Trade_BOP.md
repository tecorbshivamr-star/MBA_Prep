## 7.2 Introduction

### Why Do Countries Trade?

Countries trade because:
1. **Different resource endowments** — Saudi Arabia has oil, Brazil has coffee
2. **Different technologies** — USA produces better software, Germany better cars
3. **Economies of scale** — Producing more reduces cost per unit
4. **Consumer variety** — Trade gives access to more goods
5. **Competition** — Exposure to foreign competition improves efficiency

### Benefits of International Trade

| Benefit | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Higher output** | Countries specialize in what they do best → Total world output rises |
| **Lower prices** | Competition + efficiency → Cheaper goods for consumers |
| **Technology transfer** | FDI and imports bring new technologies |
| **Economies of scale** | Larger markets allow mass production |
| **Employment** | Export-oriented industries create jobs |
| **Foreign exchange** | Exports earn forex reserves |

### Costs / Concerns of Trade

- **Job losses** in import-competing sectors
- **Income inequality** — skilled workers gain, unskilled may lose
- **Dependence** on foreign suppliers (strategic vulnerability)
- **Trade imbalances** — persistent deficits drain reserves

## 7.3 Theories of International Trade

### Theory 1: Mercantilism (16th–18th Century)

**Core Idea**: A nation's wealth = its stock of gold and silver. Export as much as possible, import as little as possible.

**Policy**: High tariffs on imports, subsidies for exports, colonial exploitation

**Criticism** (Adam Smith, 1776):
- Wealth is NOT gold — it's the ability to produce goods and services
- Restricting imports makes a country poorer (higher prices, less variety)
- Zero-sum thinking — trade can be **win-win**, not win-lose

> **Legacy**: Mercantilist thinking still influences modern **protectionism** and "trade wars"

---

### Theory 2: Absolute Advantage (Adam Smith, 1776)

**Core Idea**: A country should produce and export goods in which it has an **absolute advantage** — i.e., it can produce more of a good using the **same resources** as another country.

**Example:**

| Country | Wheat (per worker/day) | Cloth (per worker/day) |
|---|---|---|
| **India** | 10 units | 5 units |
| **UK** | 4 units | 8 units |

- India has **absolute advantage in wheat** (10 > 4)
- UK has **absolute advantage in cloth** (8 > 5)
- → India should export wheat, UK should export cloth

**Problem with Absolute Advantage:**
- What if one country has absolute advantage in **BOTH** goods?
- Should trade still occur? → **David Ricardo** said YES → Comparative Advantage

---

### Theory 3: Comparative Advantage (David Ricardo, 1817)

**Core Idea**: A country should specialize in producing and exporting the good in which it has the **lowest opportunity cost**, even if it has no absolute advantage in anything.

> 📌 *"Even if a country is absolutely worse at everything, it should still trade by specializing in what it is relatively LEAST BAD at."*

#### Comparative Advantage — Numerical Example (MUST KNOW)

**Given:**

| Country | Wheat (units/worker/day) | Cloth (units/worker/day) |
|---|---|---|
| **India** | 6 | 4 |
| **USA** | 10 | 8 |

**USA has absolute advantage in BOTH goods.**

**Step 1: Calculate Opportunity Costs**

*Opportunity cost of 1 unit of Wheat = Units of Cloth given up*

```
India:   OC of 1 Wheat = 4/6 = 0.67 cloth
USA:     OC of 1 Wheat = 8/10 = 0.80 cloth

India:   OC of 1 Cloth = 6/4 = 1.5 wheat
USA:     OC of 1 Cloth = 10/8 = 1.25 wheat
```

**Step 2: Identify Comparative Advantage**

| Good | India's OC | USA's OC | Comparative Advantage |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Wheat** | 0.67 cloth | 0.80 cloth | **India** (lower OC) |
| **Cloth** | 1.5 wheat | 1.25 wheat | **USA** (lower OC) |

**Conclusion:**
- India should **specialize in and export Wheat** (despite having no absolute advantage)
- USA should **specialize in and export Cloth**
- Both countries gain from trade!

#### Why Does This Work? (Intuition)

India is relatively better at wheat compared to cloth (giving up only 0.67 cloth per wheat vs USA's 0.80). USA is relatively better at cloth. By specializing and trading, **total world output increases**.

**Terms of Trade**: The actual exchange rate between goods in trade
- Must be between the two opportunity costs for both countries to gain
- For wheat: Between 0.67 and 0.80 cloth per wheat

### Theory 4: Heckscher-Ohlin (H-O) Model (Factor Endowments Theory)

**Economists**: Eli Heckscher (1919) and Bertil Ohlin (1933)

**Core Idea**: Countries export goods that **intensively use their abundant factors** of production, and import goods that intensively use their **scarce factors**.

```
Labor-abundant country → Export labor-intensive goods
Capital-abundant country → Export capital-intensive goods
```

**Examples:**

| Country | Abundant Factor | Export Specialization |
|---|---|---|
| **India, Bangladesh** | Labor (cheap) | Textiles, garments, IT services |
| **USA, Germany** | Capital, Technology | Aircraft, machinery, pharmaceuticals |
| **Saudi Arabia** | Natural resources (oil) | Petroleum products |
| **Australia, Canada** | Land | Agricultural commodities, minerals |

**Leontief Paradox**: Wassily Leontief found that USA (capital-abundant) actually exported **labor-intensive** goods — contradicting H-O theory. Suggested human capital and technology matter more than physical capital alone.

---

### Theory 5: Product Life Cycle Theory (Raymond Vernon, 1966)

**Core Idea**: Products go through a life cycle — and the **country that produces and exports them shifts** over time.

```
Stage 1: INTRODUCTION
→ New product invented in rich country (USA)
→ Produced and consumed domestically
→ Exported to other rich countries

Stage 2: GROWTH / MATURITY
→ Product standardized; production becomes routine
→ Production shifts to lower-cost developed countries
→ Original inventor starts importing

Stage 3: DECLINE / STANDARDIZATION
→ Fully standardized product
→ Production moves to developing countries (cheapest labor)
→ Inventor country fully imports the good
```

**India Example:**
- **Electronics**: USA invented, Japan/S.Korea matured, China/India now manufactures
- **IT Services**: Started in USA, matured, offshore to India (India's comparative advantage = skilled cheap labor)

---

### Theory 6: New Trade Theory (Paul Krugman, 1979 — Nobel Prize 2008)

**Core Idea**: Trade can occur even between **similar countries** (similar factor endowments, incomes) due to:
1. **Economies of scale** — Large-scale production reduces costs
2. **Consumer love for variety** — People want choices even in similar products

**Why Germany and France trade cars with each other** (both have capital, technology):
- German car brands (BMW, Mercedes) and French brands (Renault, Peugeot) are differentiated
- Consumers in both countries want **variety** → They trade similar but differentiated goods
- This is **Intra-Industry Trade** (trading the same category of goods)

**First Mover Advantage**: The country/firm that enters an industry first gains economies of scale and market dominance — even if another country could theoretically do it more efficiently.
- Example: Boeing (USA) vs Airbus (Europe) — both viable, but early entrants with scale advantages

---

### Theory 7: Porter's Diamond (Competitive Advantage of Nations, 1990)

**Michael Porter** argued that national competitiveness depends on **four interrelated factors**:

```
                 Factor
                Conditions
                   │
                   │
Demand    ────── DIAMOND ────── Related &
Conditions                      Supporting
                   │            Industries
                   │
               Firm Strategy,
               Structure &
               Rivalry
```

| Diamond Factor | What It Includes |
|---|---|
| **Factor Conditions** | Skilled labor, infrastructure, capital, technology (created, not just inherited) |
| **Demand Conditions** | Sophisticated, demanding domestic customers push firms to innovate |
| **Related & Supporting Industries** | Strong supplier and related industries create clusters |
| **Firm Strategy, Structure & Rivalry** | Intense domestic competition breeds globally competitive firms |

**Government** and **Chance** act as external catalysts.

**India's Diamond:**
- **Strong factors**: Large pool of English-speaking engineers → IT sector
- **Demand**: Domestic demand for frugal innovation → Cheap smartphones, telemedicine
- **Clusters**: Bengaluru IT cluster, Mumbai financial cluster, Surat textile cluster

---

### Trade Policy Tools

| Tool | Definition | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Tariff** | Tax on imported goods | ↑ Price of imports → ↓ Imports → Protects domestic industry but ↑ consumer prices |
| **Import Quota** | Limit on quantity of imports | Similar to tariff but quantity-based; importers earn "quota rent" |
| **Export Subsidy** | Govt. payment to exporters | ↓ Export price → ↑ Exports; can trigger retaliation |
| **Voluntary Export Restraint (VER)** | Exporting country agrees to limit exports | Used to avoid tariffs; example: Japan auto VER to USA |
| **Embargo** | Complete ban on trade with a country | Political/strategic tool; Russia sanctions |
| **Dumping** | Selling exports below cost to capture market | Predatory pricing; WTO allows anti-dumping duties |
| **Non-tariff barriers** | Standards, regulations, licensing that restrict trade | Subtle but effective protectionism |

**WTO (World Trade Organization)**:
- Governs international trade rules
- Most Favoured Nation (MFN) principle: Equal treatment to all trading partners
- Dispute settlement mechanism for trade conflicts
- India is a founding member (1995)

## 7.4 Balance of Payments (BOP)

### Definition

The **Balance of Payments (BOP)** is a **systematic record of all economic transactions** between residents of a country and the rest of the world during a given period (usually one year).

> 📌 *Think of BOP as the country's "international bank statement."*

### BOP Structure

The BOP has **THREE main accounts**:

```
BALANCE OF PAYMENTS
├── 1. CURRENT ACCOUNT (CA)
│   ├── Trade in Goods (Merchandise)
│   ├── Trade in Services
│   ├── Primary Income (factor income)
│   └── Secondary Income (transfers)
│
├── 2. CAPITAL ACCOUNT
│   └── Capital transfers, acquisition of non-produced assets
│
└── 3. FINANCIAL ACCOUNT
    ├── Foreign Direct Investment (FDI)
    ├── Portfolio Investment (FPI/FII)
    ├── Other Investment (loans, banking)
    └── Reserve Assets (forex reserves)
```

> **BOP always balances (by accounting identity):**
> ```
> Current Account + Capital Account + Financial Account = 0
> ```
> (Any deficit in one must be financed by surplus in another)

---

### 1. Current Account (CA)

Records **flows of goods, services, and income** between countries.

#### A. Trade in Goods (Merchandise / Visible Trade)

- **Exports of goods**: Credit (+)
- **Imports of goods**: Debit (−)
- **Trade Balance (BOT)** = Merchandise Exports − Merchandise Imports

```
Trade Deficit: Imports > Exports → Negative Trade Balance
Trade Surplus: Exports > Imports → Positive Trade Balance
```

**India's Trade Balance (2023-24)**:
- Merchandise Exports: ~$437 billion
- Merchandise Imports: ~$678 billion
- **Trade Deficit: ~$240 billion** (mostly due to oil and electronics imports)

#### B. Trade in Services (Invisible Trade)

- Software services, IT-BPO
- Tourism (inbound = export, outbound = import)
- Financial services, education
- Transportation, insurance

**India's Services Surplus** (~$140+ billion) — largely offsets goods deficit
- India is world's #1 IT services exporter

#### C. Primary Income

- **Factor income flows**: Wages sent home by workers, dividends, interest on investments
- **Remittances received** from Indian diaspora abroad → Credit (+)
- **India receives largest remittances** in the world (~$120 billion in 2023)

#### D. Secondary Income (Unrequited Transfers)

- **Private remittances**: Money sent home by migrant workers
- **Official transfers**: Foreign aid, grants (not loans)

**Current Account Balance (CAB)**:
```
CAB = Trade Balance (Goods) + Services Balance + Primary Income + Secondary Income
```

**Current Account Deficit (CAD)**:
- India typically runs a **CAD** (imports > exports in overall current account)
- India's CAD (2023-24): ~1.2% of GDP (manageable range; 3%+ is concerning)

---

### 2. Capital Account

Records **capital transfers** and transactions involving:
- Debt forgiveness
- Migrants' transfers (assets brought when migrating)
- Acquisition/disposal of non-produced, non-financial assets (patents, land)

> **Small in size** for most countries including India — often combined with Financial Account in analysis

---

### 3. Financial Account

Records **cross-border financial investments** — changes in ownership of financial assets and liabilities.

#### A. Foreign Direct Investment (FDI)

- **FDI Inflows**: Foreigners invest in Indian companies/factories (long-term, controlling stake ≥10%)
- **FDI Outflows**: Indian companies invest abroad
- **Net FDI = FDI Inflows − FDI Outflows**

**India's FDI (2023-24)**: ~$70-80 billion gross inflows; largest sources: Singapore, USA, Mauritius

#### B. Foreign Portfolio Investment (FPI/FII)

- Short-term financial investments in **stocks, bonds** (no management control)
- **More volatile** than FDI — "hot money" that can flee quickly
- India's FPI flows are monitored closely (sudden outflows can crash markets and weaken ₹)

#### C. Other Investment

- **Bank loans**, trade credits, currency deposits
- ECBs (External Commercial Borrowings) by Indian companies

#### D. Reserve Assets (Official Settlements)

- **Change in forex reserves** — RBI buys/sells foreign currency to balance BOP
- ↑ Reserves = RBI buying foreign currency (surplus elsewhere in BOP)
- ↓ Reserves = RBI selling foreign currency (funding a deficit)

**India's Forex Reserves**: ~$640-650 billion (among world's top 5)

---

### BOP Equilibrium and Disequilibrium

**BOP Equilibrium**: No net change in official reserves — all transactions financed without government intervention

**BOP Surplus**: Country earns more from rest of world than it spends → Accumulates forex reserves
- Example: China ran persistent BOP surpluses → Accumulated $3+ trillion in reserves

**BOP Deficit**: Country spends more with rest of world than it earns → Depletes forex reserves
- Example: Sri Lanka's BOP crisis (2022) — ran out of reserves → Couldn't import essentials

---

### Causes of BOP Disequilibrium

| Type | Causes | Solution |
|---|---|---|
| **Cyclical** | Recession reduces exports; boom raises imports | Temporary; self-corrects |
| **Structural** | Weak export base, import dependence on oil/tech | Long-term reform — diversify exports |
| **Speculative** | Capital flight, hot money outflows | Capital controls, forex intervention |
| **Inflation-induced** | High domestic inflation → Exports uncompetitive | Monetary tightening, currency depreciation |

---

### Methods to Correct BOP Deficit

| Method | Mechanism | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Currency Depreciation** | ↑ Exports (cheaper), ↓ Imports (dearer) → ↑NX | India 2013 (Taper Tantrum — ₹ fell sharply) |
| **Deflation (↓ AD)** | ↓ Incomes → ↓ Import demand | Painful — causes recession |
| **Trade Controls** | ↑ Tariffs, import quotas | Reduces imports but causes inefficiency |
| **Exchange Controls** | Govt. rationing of foreign exchange | Used in severe crises (Sri Lanka 2022) |
| **Borrowing** | IMF loans, ECBs to finance deficit | Temporary; adds to external debt |
| **Attract FDI/FPI** | Better investment climate → Capital inflows | India's preferred approach |

---

### India's BOP — Complete Picture

| Account | India's Typical Position | Key Items |
|---|---|---|
| **Trade Balance (Goods)** | Large Deficit (~$240 billion) | Oil, Gold, Electronics imports |
| **Services Balance** | Large Surplus (~$140 billion) | IT/BPO, software exports |
| **Remittances** | Large Surplus (~$120 billion) | Indian diaspora in USA, Gulf |
| **Current Account Balance** | Moderate Deficit (~1-2% GDP) | Services + remittances partially offset trade deficit |
| **FDI Inflows** | Positive (~$70-80 billion) | Technology, retail, infrastructure |
| **FPI Flows** | Volatile (can be negative) | Equity and bond markets |
| **Overall BOP** | Generally Positive (reserves growing) | RBI accumulates forex |

**India's Key Trade Partners:**
- **Exports to**: USA, UAE, Netherlands, China, Bangladesh
- **Imports from**: China (largest!), UAE, Russia (oil), USA, Iraq

**India's Key Exports**: IT services, petroleum products, pharmaceuticals, textiles, gems & jewelry
**India's Key Imports**: Crude oil, gold, electronics, machinery, fertilizers

---

### Current Account Deficit (CAD) — India's Chronic Issue

**Why India runs CAD:**
1. **Energy dependence**: ~85% crude oil imported (~$130 billion/year)
2. **Gold imports**: India is world's largest gold importer (~$35-40 billion/year)
3. **Electronics deficit**: Large imports of smartphones, semiconductors
4. **Low manufacturing exports**: "Make in India" aims to fix this

**Financing CAD:**
- Remittances (large and stable)
- FDI inflows
- FPI inflows (volatile)
- NRI deposits
- ECBs

**Sustainable CAD level**: Generally considered safe below **3% of GDP**

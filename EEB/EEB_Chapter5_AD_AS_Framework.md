## 5.2 Introduction

### What is the AD-AS Framework?

The **AD-AS (Aggregate Demand – Aggregate Supply) model** is the primary tool for analyzing:
- **Short-run** fluctuations (recessions, booms)
- **Long-run** economic growth and price stability
- The effects of **government policies** on output and price level

```
AD Curve  → Represents the DEMAND side of the economy
SRAS Curve → Represents the SUPPLY side in the SHORT RUN
LRAS Curve → Represents the SUPPLY side in the LONG RUN (potential output)
```

> 📌 *Think of AD-AS as the "big picture" model — it's IS-LM plus the price level.*

### AD-AS vs IS-LM

| Feature | **IS-LM** | **AD-AS** |
|---|---|---|
| Price Level | Fixed (P is given) | Variable (P adjusts) |
| Focus | Interest rate & Output | Price level & Output |
| Time Horizon | Short run only | Short run + Long run |
| Policy Analysis | Fiscal & Monetary (in detail) | All policies + supply shocks |

## 5.3 Supply-Side Economics

### What is Supply-Side Economics?

**Supply-Side Economics** focuses on increasing the **productive capacity** (LRAS) of the economy rather than just stimulating demand. It argues that long-run growth comes from removing barriers to production.

> Associated with **Ronald Reagan** (USA) and **Margaret Thatcher** (UK) in the 1980s — "Reaganomics"

### Core Beliefs of Supply-Side Economics

| Belief | Policy Prescription |
|---|---|
| High taxes reduce incentive to work/invest | **Cut taxes** (especially corporate and income taxes) |
| Government regulation stifles business | **Deregulation** — reduce bureaucracy and red tape |
| Government is inefficient | **Privatization** — sell PSUs to private sector |
| Labor market rigidities cause unemployment | **Labor market reforms** — flexible hiring/firing |
| Free trade increases efficiency | **Trade liberalization** — reduce tariffs and quotas |

### The Laffer Curve (Supply-Side's Key Concept)

The **Laffer Curve** shows the relationship between **tax rate** and **tax revenue**:

```
Tax
Revenue
    │        ● (optimal tax rate — max revenue)
    │      /    \
    │    /        \
    │  /            \
    │/                \
    └──────────────────────► Tax Rate (%)
   0%                    100%

At 0% tax → Zero revenue
At 100% tax → Zero revenue (no one works)
Somewhere in between → Maximum revenue
```

**Supply-Side Argument**: If tax rate is beyond the optimal point → **Cut taxes** → People work more → Tax base expands → Revenue can actually **increase**

**Criticism of Supply-Side Economics:**
- Tax cuts mostly benefit the rich (trickle-down effect is weak)
- Can increase budget deficits if growth doesn't materialize
- Ignores demand-side problems (especially in recessions)

### Supply-Side Policies in India

| Policy | Effect |
|---|---|
| **GST Implementation (2017)** | Unified market, reduced cascading taxes → TFP boost |
| **PLI (Production Linked Incentive) Schemes** | Incentivizes manufacturing → Shifts LRAS right |
| **IBC (Insolvency & Bankruptcy Code)** | Faster resolution → Better capital allocation |
| **Privatization of PSUs** | Efficiency improvement → LRAS expansion |
| **Ease of Doing Business reforms** | Reduced red tape → More investment |

## 5.4 Difference Between Short Run and Long Run

### The Key Distinction — Price/Wage Flexibility

| Feature | **Short Run** | **Long Run** |
|---|---|---|
| **Wages** | Sticky/Rigid (contracts, minimum wage) | Fully flexible |
| **Prices** | Partially sticky | Fully flexible |
| **Output** | Can deviate from potential | Returns to potential (Y*) |
| **Unemployment** | Can deviate from natural rate | At natural rate (NAIRU) |
| **SRAS slope** | Upward sloping | Vertical (at Y*) |

### Short Run vs Long Run — What Changes?

**Short Run:**
- Wages fixed by contracts → Firms can hire more workers by raising prices slightly
- Output can be above or below potential (GDP gap exists)
- Economy doesn't self-correct quickly

**Long Run:**
- Wages and prices fully adjust
- Output always equals **Potential GDP (Y*)** — also called **Full Employment Output**
- Economy self-corrects automatically (but slowly — takes years)

### The GDP Gap

```
GDP Gap = Actual GDP − Potential GDP

Positive Gap (Inflationary Gap): Actual Y > Y* → Economy overheating, inflation
Negative Gap (Recessionary Gap): Actual Y < Y* → Recession, unemployment
```

### Natural Rate of Unemployment (NAIRU)

- Also called **Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of Unemployment**
- The unemployment rate when economy is at potential output
- Consists of frictional + structural unemployment (NOT cyclical)
- India's estimated NAIRU: ~5-7%

## 5.5 The Model of Aggregate Supply and Aggregate Demand

### 🔵 AGGREGATE DEMAND (AD) CURVE

**Definition**: AD shows the **total quantity of goods and services** that all sectors of the economy want to buy at different **price levels**, holding everything else constant.

```
AD = C + I + G + (X − M)
```

#### Why Does AD Slope Downward?

Three effects explain why ↑P → ↓ quantity demanded:

| Effect | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Wealth Effect (Pigou Effect)** | ↑P → Real value of money holdings falls → People feel poorer → ↓C |
| **Interest Rate Effect (Keynes Effect)** | ↑P → ↑Money demand → ↑r (from LM) → ↓I → ↓AD |
| **Exchange Rate Effect (Mundell-Fleming)** | ↑P → Exports more expensive → ↓X, Imports cheaper → ↑M → ↓NX |

```
Price
Level (P)
    │
  P₁│ • A (High P, Low Y demanded)
    │   \
  P₂│    • B (Low P, High Y demanded)
    │     \
    │      \ AD
    └─────────────────────────► Output (Y)
         Y₁  Y₂
```

#### What SHIFTS the AD Curve?

| **AD Shifts RIGHT (↑ at every P)** | **AD Shifts LEFT (↓ at every P)** |
|---|---|
| ↑ Government Spending (G) | ↓ Government Spending |
| ↓ Taxes (T) | ↑ Taxes |
| ↑ Money Supply (Ms) | ↓ Money Supply |
| ↑ Consumer/Investor confidence | ↓ Consumer/Investor confidence |
| ↑ Exports (global boom) | ↓ Exports (global recession) |
| ↓ Interest rates | ↑ Interest rates |

---

### 🟡 SHORT-RUN AGGREGATE SUPPLY (SRAS)

**Definition**: SRAS shows the **total quantity of goods and services** firms are willing to produce at different **price levels** in the short run, when wages and some prices are sticky.

#### Why Does SRAS Slope Upward?

| Reason | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Sticky Wage Theory** | Wages fixed by contracts; ↑P → ↑Profits (prices rise but wages don't) → Firms produce more |
| **Sticky Price Theory** | Some firms keep prices fixed (menu costs) → ↑AD met by ↑Output |
| **Misperception Theory** | Firms see their own price rise; assume relative price rise → ↑Production |

```
Price
Level (P)
    │               / SRAS
  P₂│           • B /
    │          /
  P₁│      • A
    │       /
    │      /
    └─────────────────────────► Output (Y)
         Y₁  Y₂
```

#### What SHIFTS the SRAS Curve?

| **SRAS Shifts RIGHT (↑ supply at every P)** | **SRAS Shifts LEFT (↓ supply at every P)** |
|---|---|
| ↓ Input prices (oil, raw materials) | ↑ Input prices (oil shock) |
| ↓ Wages (wage moderation) | ↑ Wages (wage demands) |
| ↑ Technology / Productivity | ↓ Productivity |
| Favorable weather (agriculture) | Drought, floods, natural disasters |
| ↓ Business taxes/regulation | ↑ Business taxes/regulation |

---

### 🔴 LONG-RUN AGGREGATE SUPPLY (LRAS)

**Definition**: LRAS shows the level of output the economy produces when wages and prices are **fully flexible** and all resources are **fully employed** — i.e., at **Potential GDP (Y*)**.

#### Why is LRAS Vertical?

In the long run, output is determined by **real factors** (capital, labor, technology) — NOT by the price level.

- ↑P → Wages also rise proportionally → No change in real wages → No change in employment or output
- **Output always returns to Y\* regardless of price level**

```
Price
Level (P)
    │          │ LRAS (vertical at Y*)
  P₂│          │
    │          │
  P₁│          │
    │          │
    └─────────────────────────► Output (Y)
                Y* (Potential GDP)
```

**LRAS shifts ONLY when potential output changes:**
- ↑ Capital stock (investment)
- ↑ Labor force (population, immigration)
- ↑ TFP / Technology
- ↑ Human capital (education, health)
- Better institutions

---

### Complete AD-AS Diagram

```
Price
Level (P)
    │          │LRAS
    │          │     SRAS
    │          │    /
  P*│ ─────────•──────
    │          │ /\
    │          │/  \
    │         /│    AD
    └──────────────────────────► Output (Y)
               Y* (Equilibrium = Potential GDP)
```

**Long-run equilibrium** is where **AD = SRAS = LRAS** — at (Y*, P*)

---

### Demand and Supply Shocks

#### Case 1: Positive Demand Shock (↑AD) — e.g., ↑G

**Short Run:**
```
↑AD → AD shifts right → ↑Y (above Y*) and ↑P
→ Inflationary Gap (Y > Y*)
```

**Long Run (Self-Correction):**
```
Economy above Y* → Low unemployment → Workers demand higher wages
→ Wages rise → SRAS shifts LEFT → P rises further, Y returns to Y*
→ New LR Equilibrium: Same Y*, Higher P
```

```
SR: ↑Y, ↑P
LR: Y back to Y*, P even higher
```

#### Case 2: Negative Demand Shock (↓AD) — e.g., Recession

**Short Run:**
```
↓AD → AD shifts left → ↓Y (below Y*) and ↓P
→ Recessionary Gap (Y < Y*)
```

**Long Run (Self-Correction):**
```
Economy below Y* → High unemployment → Workers accept lower wages
→ Wages fall → SRAS shifts RIGHT → P falls further, Y returns to Y*
→ New LR Equilibrium: Same Y*, Lower P
```

> **Problem**: This self-correction is very slow! (wages are sticky downward)
> **Keynes**: "In the long run, we are all dead." — Government should intervene NOW!

#### Case 3: Negative Supply Shock (↓SRAS) — e.g., Oil Price Rise

```
↑Oil prices → SRAS shifts LEFT → ↑P AND ↓Y simultaneously
→ STAGFLATION (Inflation + Recession at same time)
```

**Stagflation:**
```
Price
Level (P)
    │          │LRAS
    │          │   SRAS₂ (shifted left)
  P₂│ ─────────•  / SRAS₁
    │          |\/
  P₁│ ─────────•
    │          │\
    │          │ AD
    └───────────────────────► Output (Y)
              Y₂ Y₁=Y*
```

**India's Stagflation Episodes**: 1973-74 Oil Crisis, 1979-80, COVID supply chain disruptions 2021-22

**Why stagflation is the policymaker's nightmare:**
- ↑ G or ↑ Ms → Fixes output but worsens inflation
- ↓ G or ↓ Ms → Fixes inflation but worsens recession
- Cannot solve both simultaneously with demand-side tools!

## 5.6 Policy Effectiveness and AD-AS

### Policy Matrix in AD-AS

| Problem | Appropriate Policy | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Recessionary Gap** (Y < Y*) | Expansionary Fiscal or Monetary | AD shifts right → ↑Y, ↑P |
| **Inflationary Gap** (Y > Y*) | Contractionary Fiscal or Monetary | AD shifts left → ↓Y, ↓P |
| **Stagflation** (↓Y + ↑P) | Supply-Side Policy | SRAS/LRAS shifts right → ↑Y, ↓P |
| **Long-run growth** | Supply-side, investment in capital/TFP | LRAS shifts right → ↑Y*, stable P |

---

## 5.7 Fiscal Policy in AD-AS

### Expansionary Fiscal Policy (↑G or ↓T)

**Mechanism:**
```
↑G → ↑AD → AD curve shifts RIGHT
Short Run: ↑Y and ↑P (movement from recession toward Y*)
Long Run (if Y > Y*): Wages rise → SRAS shifts LEFT → Y returns to Y*, P higher
```

**Recessionary Gap — Fiscal Policy:**
```
P │      │LRAS
  │      │    SRAS
P₂│      │   /  •B (new SR equilibrium)
  │      │  /  /
P₁│      │ • A/ ← AD₂ (after ↑G)
  │      │   AD₁
  └──────────────────► Y
        Y₁ Y*
```
- Y rises from Y₁ toward Y*
- P rises from P₁ to P₂
- If policy brings Y = Y*, inflation stabilizes

**Limitations of Fiscal Policy in AD-AS:**
1. **Crowding out** (as seen in IS-LM)
2. **Time lags** — recognition lag, legislative lag, implementation lag
3. **Debt burden** — deficit spending increases national debt
4. **Inflationary pressure** — if economy is already near Y*, fiscal expansion mainly raises P

---

### Contractionary Fiscal Policy (↓G or ↑T)

Used when economy is in **inflationary gap** (Y > Y*):
```
↓G or ↑T → ↓AD → AD shifts LEFT → ↓Y and ↓P → Brings Y back to Y*
```

---

## 5.8 Monetary Policy in AD-AS

### Expansionary Monetary Policy (↑Ms)

**Mechanism (linking IS-LM to AD-AS):**
```
↑Ms → ↓r (LM shifts right in IS-LM)
→ ↓r → ↑I → ↑AD
→ In AD-AS: AD curve shifts RIGHT
→ Short Run: ↑Y and ↑P
```

**Advantages over Fiscal Policy:**
- **Faster implementation** — RBI can act in 2-month MPC cycle
- **No crowding out** — ↑Ms actually lowers r, stimulating investment
- **Reversible** — easier to reverse than fiscal commitments

**Limitations:**
1. **Liquidity Trap** — at zero interest rate, ↑Ms doesn't lower r → No ↑AD
2. **Long and variable lags** — monetary transmission takes 6-18 months to affect inflation
3. **Transmission mechanism problems** — banks may not pass rate cuts to borrowers
4. **Cannot target specific sectors** — affects entire economy

### Contractionary Monetary Policy (↓Ms or ↑r)

Used to fight **inflation** (inflationary gap or cost-push):
```
↓Ms → ↑r → ↓I → ↓AD → AD shifts LEFT → ↓P and ↓Y
```

**India Context — RBI's Rate Hike Cycle (2022-23):**
- Inflation crossed 7% (above RBI's 6% upper tolerance band)
- RBI raised repo rate by 250 basis points (2.5%)
- AD shifted left → Inflation came down to ~5%
- Trade-off: GDP growth slowed slightly

## 5.9 Trade and Exchange Rate Policy

### Open Economy Extensions to AD-AS

In an open economy, AD includes **Net Exports (NX = X − M)**:
```
AD = C + I + G + NX
```

Changes in **exchange rate** and **trade policy** affect NX → Shift AD curve

---

### Exchange Rate — Key Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Exchange Rate (e)** | Price of one currency in terms of another |
| **Appreciation** | Domestic currency becomes STRONGER (↑ value vs foreign currency) |
| **Depreciation** | Domestic currency becomes WEAKER (↓ value vs foreign currency) |
| **Revaluation** | Official increase in fixed exchange rate |
| **Devaluation** | Official decrease in fixed exchange rate |

---

### Effect of Currency Depreciation on AD-AS

**Example: Indian Rupee depreciates (₹ weakens vs USD)**

```
₹ depreciates → Indian exports CHEAPER for foreigners → ↑X
               → Imports MORE EXPENSIVE for Indians → ↓M
               → ↑NX → ↑AD → AD shifts RIGHT → ↑Y and ↑P
```

**J-Curve Effect:**
- In the **short run**: Depreciation may WORSEN trade balance (contracts fixed in old prices)
- In the **medium/long run**: Trade balance IMPROVES (exports and imports adjust)
- Adjustment path looks like the letter **"J"**

```
Trade
Balance
    │              /
    │             /  (Long Run: Improvement)
    │            /
    │───────────•─────── (Initial level)
    │          \  
    │           \ (Short Run: Worsens first)
    └──────────────────────────────► Time
         Depreciation
         happens here
```

---

### Currency Appreciation — Effect on AD-AS

```
↑ Value of ₹ → Indian exports MORE expensive → ↓X
             → Imports CHEAPER → ↑M
             → ↓NX → ↓AD → AD shifts LEFT → ↓Y, ↓P (deflationary)
```

**Used when**: Economy is overheating (inflationary gap) — allow currency to appreciate

---

### Trade Policy — Tariffs and Quotas

| Policy | Effect on NX | Effect on AD |
|---|---|---|
| **↑ Tariffs (import tax)** | ↓M → ↑NX | AD shifts RIGHT (↑Y, ↑P) |
| **↓ Tariffs / Free Trade** | ↑M → ↓NX | AD shifts LEFT (↓Y, ↓P) |
| **Import Quotas** | ↓M → ↑NX | AD shifts RIGHT |
| **Export Subsidies** | ↑X → ↑NX | AD shifts RIGHT |

**BUT**: Trade protection has trade-offs:
- Protects domestic industries short-term
- Raises prices for consumers
- Invites retaliation from trading partners
- Reduces economic efficiency (no comparative advantage)

---

### Exchange Rate Regimes

| Regime | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Fixed Exchange Rate** | Government pegs currency to another (e.g., gold or USD) | China (historically), Saudi Arabia |
| **Floating Exchange Rate** | Market determines exchange rate | USA, Eurozone |
| **Managed Float** | Mostly market-determined, but RBI intervenes occasionally | **India (current system)** |

**India's Exchange Rate Policy:**
- India follows a **managed float** (also called dirty float)
- RBI intervenes to **prevent excessive volatility** — buys/sells USD in forex market
- India's forex reserves: ~$640-650 billion (2024) — used as buffer

---

### Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)

**PPP Theory**: In the long run, exchange rates adjust so that identical goods cost the same everywhere.

```
PPP Exchange Rate = Domestic Price Level / Foreign Price Level
e_PPP = P_India / P_USA
```

**Big Mac Index** (The Economist): Uses price of a Big Mac in different countries to assess currency over/under-valuation.

**India's GDP — Market Rate vs PPP:**
- GDP at Market Rate: ~$3.7 trillion (nominal) — 5th largest
- GDP at PPP: ~$14 trillion — 3rd largest (after USA and China)
- Large difference because prices in India are much lower than in the West

### ⚡ Quick Formula Sheet

```
AD = C + I + G + NX

GDP Gap = Actual GDP − Potential GDP (Y*)
Inflationary Gap: Actual Y > Y*
Recessionary Gap: Actual Y < Y*

Stagflation: ↑P + ↓Y (due to negative supply shock)

J-Curve: Trade balance worsens before improving after depreciation

PPP Rate = P_domestic / P_foreign

AD shifts RIGHT → ↑Y, ↑P (in SR)
AD shifts LEFT  → ↓Y, ↓P (in SR)
SRAS shifts LEFT  → ↓Y, ↑P (stagflation)
SRAS shifts RIGHT → ↑Y, ↓P (supply boom)
LRAS shifts RIGHT → ↑Y* (long-run growth)
```

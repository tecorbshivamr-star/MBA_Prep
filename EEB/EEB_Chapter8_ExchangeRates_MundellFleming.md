## 8.2 Introduction

### Why Exchange Rates Matter

**Exchange rates** are the **prices of currencies** — they connect every country's economy to the rest of the world. They affect:

| What is Affected | How |
|---|---|
| **Export competitiveness** | Weak currency → Cheaper exports → ↑ demand |
| **Import costs** | Weak currency → Dearer imports → ↑ inflation |
| **Inflation** | Currency depreciation imports inflation |
| **Foreign debt burden** | Weak ₹ → More rupees needed to repay USD debt |
| **FDI/FPI attractiveness** | Stable, appreciating currency attracts capital |
| **Business profitability** | Importers hurt by depreciation; exporters benefit |

---

## 8.3 Exchange Rates and the Foreign Exchange Market

### Key Definitions

| Term | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Nominal Exchange Rate (e)** | Price of one currency in terms of another | 1 USD = ₹83.50 |
| **Real Exchange Rate (q)** | Nominal rate adjusted for relative prices | q = e × (P*/P) |
| **Appreciation** | Domestic currency gains value | ₹80 → ₹75 per USD (fewer ₹ per $) |
| **Depreciation** | Domestic currency loses value | ₹80 → ₹85 per USD (more ₹ per $) |
| **Revaluation** | Official ↑ in value (fixed rate system) | Govt. declares new higher peg |
| **Devaluation** | Official ↓ in value (fixed rate system) | Govt. declares new lower peg |

> ⚠️ **India Notation**: In India, exchange rate quoted as **₹ per USD** (direct quote)
> - If USD/₹ goes from 80 to 85 → Rupee has **DEPRECIATED** (weaker ₹)
> - If USD/₹ goes from 85 to 80 → Rupee has **APPRECIATED** (stronger ₹)

### Real Exchange Rate

```
Real Exchange Rate (q) = e × (P* / P)

Where:
e  = Nominal exchange rate (domestic currency per foreign currency)
P* = Foreign price level
P  = Domestic price level
```

- **q > 1**: Domestic goods relatively expensive → Less competitive exports
- **q < 1**: Domestic goods relatively cheap → More competitive exports
- **↑ q (real depreciation)**: ↑ Export competitiveness (exports cheaper for foreigners)

---

### The Foreign Exchange Market

**Participants in Forex Market:**
1. **Commercial Banks** — Largest players; facilitate client transactions + speculate
2. **Central Banks (RBI)** — Intervene to stabilize currency
3. **Corporations** — Import/export payments, hedging
4. **Investors/Hedge Funds** — FPI flows, speculation
5. **Individuals** — Travel, remittances (smallest participants)

**India's Forex Market:**
- Daily turnover: ~$70-80 billion
- Key pairs: USD/INR, EUR/INR, GBP/INR
- RBI intervenes via **spot and forward markets**
- Market open 9 AM – 5 PM IST (weekdays)

---

### Exchange Rate Determination Theories

#### Theory 1: Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)

**Absolute PPP**: Exchange rate should equalize price levels across countries.
```
e = P / P*  (Domestic Price / Foreign Price)
```

**Relative PPP**: % change in exchange rate equals the inflation differential.
```
% change in e = π (domestic inflation) − π* (foreign inflation)

If India's inflation = 6%, USA's = 2%:
₹ should depreciate by 4% against USD
```

**Evidence**: PPP holds **in the long run** but deviates significantly in short run.

**Big Mac Index** (The Economist): Practical PPP test — compares Big Mac prices globally.

---

#### Theory 2: Interest Rate Parity (IRP)

**Core Idea**: Returns on financial assets in different countries should be equal when adjusted for exchange rate changes — otherwise **arbitrage** opportunities exist and capital flows until parity is restored.

**Uncovered Interest Rate Parity (UIP):**
```
i = i* + (eᵉ − e) / e

Domestic Interest Rate = Foreign Interest Rate + Expected Currency Depreciation
```

**Example:**
- USA interest rate (i*) = 5%
- India interest rate (i) = 8%
- IRP implies: ₹ expected to depreciate by ~3% against USD

**Logic**:
- If i > i* → India offers higher returns → Capital flows INTO India → ↑ demand for ₹ → ₹ appreciates
- This continues until higher returns are offset by expected ₹ depreciation
- **Equilibrium**: Interest differential = Expected depreciation

**Covered Interest Rate Parity (CIP):**
```
i = i* + Forward Premium/Discount

Forward Rate − Spot Rate / Spot Rate = i − i*
```

---

### Exchange Rate Regimes

#### 1. Fixed Exchange Rate (Pegged)

- Government/Central Bank **fixes** the exchange rate at a declared level
- RBI must **buy or sell forex reserves** to maintain the peg
- **Advantage**: Certainty for trade and investment; controls imported inflation
- **Disadvantage**: Lose monetary policy independence; costly to defend; crisis-prone

**Historical Examples**: Bretton Woods system (1944–1971), China's RMB peg to USD

**Defending a Fixed Rate:**
- Currency under depreciation pressure → RBI **sells USD, buys ₹** (uses reserves)
- Currency under appreciation pressure → RBI **buys USD, sells ₹** (accumulates reserves)

---

#### 2. Floating Exchange Rate (Flexible)

- Exchange rate determined **freely by market** forces of supply and demand
- No government intervention in principle
- **Advantage**: Automatic adjustment; preserves monetary policy independence
- **Disadvantage**: Volatility; uncertainty for trade; speculative attacks

**Examples**: USA (USD), Eurozone (EUR), UK (GBP)

---

#### 3. Managed Float (Dirty Float) — India's System

- **Primarily market-determined** but with **selective RBI intervention**
- RBI intervenes to **smooth excessive volatility** — not to target a specific level
- Most emerging market economies use this
- India's ₹ is a **managed float**

---

### Factors Affecting Exchange Rate

| Factor | Effect on ₹ |
|---|---|
| **↑ Indian Inflation** (vs global) | ₹ Depreciates (PPP) |
| **↑ Indian Interest Rates** | ₹ Appreciates (capital inflows — IRP) |
| **↑ India's Current Account Deficit** | ₹ Depreciates (more demand for foreign currency) |
| **↑ FDI/FPI inflows** | ₹ Appreciates (supply of foreign currency rises) |
| **↑ Global risk aversion (flight to USD)** | ₹ Depreciates |
| **RBI intervention (buying USD)** | ₹ Depreciates (sells ₹) |
| **Strong economic growth** | ₹ Appreciates (investment attractiveness) |
| **↑ Oil prices** | ₹ Depreciates (India imports most oil — ↑ USD demand) |

## 8.4 Capital Account Convertibility

### Definition

**Capital Account Convertibility (CAC)** means the freedom to **convert domestic financial assets into foreign financial assets** (and vice versa) at market-determined exchange rates **without restrictions**.

```
Full CAC: No limits on:
→ FDI in/out
→ FPI in/out
→ External borrowing
→ Indians investing abroad
→ Repatriation of profits
```

### Types of Convertibility

| Type | Meaning | India's Status |
|---|---|---|
| **Current Account Convertibility** | Free conversion for trade in goods, services, remittances | ✅ **FULLY convertible** (since 1994) |
| **Capital Account Convertibility** | Free conversion for financial investments | ⚠️ **PARTIAL** (selective restrictions) |

---

### India's Position on Capital Account Convertibility

India has **partial/limited CAC** — moving toward fuller CAC gradually.

**What is ALLOWED in India (Liberalized):**
- FDI in most sectors (automatic route)
- FPI investment in equity and bonds (with limits)
- NRI deposits (FCNR, NRE accounts)
- ECBs by Indian companies (with RBI approval)
- Indian companies investing abroad (ODI — Overseas Direct Investment)

**What is RESTRICTED:**
- Full capital account convertibility for individuals (LRS limit: $250,000/year for individuals)
- Speculative forex trading by individuals
- Free movement of short-term capital (to prevent sudden outflows)

---

### The Tarapore Committee (Key Indian Policy Recommendation)

The **Tarapore Committee (1997 and 2006)** recommended **gradual full CAC** for India with preconditions:

**Preconditions for Full CAC:**
1. Fiscal deficit < 3.5% of GDP
2. Inflation < 3-5%
3. Strong banking sector (low NPAs)
4. Adequate forex reserves
5. Strong current account position

> **India has NOT yet achieved full CAC** — ongoing debate between:
> - **Proponents**: More FPI, lower cost of capital, deeper markets
> - **Opponents**: Vulnerability to capital flight, exchange rate volatility, loss of control

**Lessons from Asian Crisis (1997)**:
- Countries with full CAC and weak fundamentals suffered massive capital outflows
- Thailand, Indonesia, South Korea — currency crashed when foreign capital fled
- **India was less affected** partly due to capital controls → Vindicated partial CAC

## 8.5 The Mundell-Fleming Model

### Overview

The **Mundell-Fleming Model** (Robert Mundell + Marcus Fleming, 1960s) is the **IS-LM model for an open economy**. It extends IS-LM by:
- Adding the **foreign sector** (trade and capital flows)
- Including the **exchange rate**
- Analyzing policy effectiveness under **fixed vs floating exchange rates**

**Key Assumption**: **Perfect capital mobility** — capital flows freely across borders chasing the highest return.

```
Perfect Capital Mobility:
→ If domestic r > world r (r*) → Capital floods IN → ↑ Demand for ₹ → ₹ Appreciates
→ If domestic r < r* → Capital flees OUT → ↓ Demand for ₹ → ₹ Depreciates
→ Equilibrium: Domestic r must equal world r (r = r*)
```

---

### The Three Curves in Mundell-Fleming

| Curve | Represents | Slope |
|---|---|---|
| **IS*** | Goods market equilibrium (open economy) | Downward (↑e → ↓NX → ↓Y, or ↑r → ↓I) |
| **LM*** | Money market equilibrium | Vertical (Ms/P fixed; Y determined by Ms) |
| **BP (Balance of Payments)** | External balance | Horizontal (under perfect capital mobility — r = r*) |

**Open Economy IS Curve (IS\*):**
```
Y = C + I + G + NX(e)

NX = f(e) → ↑e (depreciation) → ↑NX → ↑Y
         → ↓e (appreciation) → ↓NX → ↓Y
```

**Key Variable**: Under perfect capital mobility, **r is fixed at r\*** (world interest rate) — Central Bank cannot independently set r.

---

### Mundell-Fleming Under FLOATING Exchange Rates

In a **floating rate** regime, exchange rate adjusts freely. The **key mechanism** is:

- **Fiscal Expansion (↑G)**: IS shifts right → Upward pressure on r → Since r must = r*, capital floods IN → Currency **APPRECIATES** → NX falls → IS shifts back LEFT → **No net effect on Y**

- **Monetary Expansion (↑Ms)**: LM shifts right → Downward pressure on r → Capital flows OUT → Currency **DEPRECIATES** → ↑NX → IS shifts RIGHT → **↑Y**

#### Case 1: Fiscal Policy — Floating Rate

```
↑G → IS shifts RIGHT
   → Upward pressure on r (above r*)
   → Capital inflows (capital attracted by higher r)
   → ↑ Demand for domestic currency
   → Exchange rate APPRECIATES (↓e)
   → ↑e → ↓NX (exports dearer, imports cheaper)
   → IS shifts back LEFT (NX effect offsets G effect)
   → Y returns to original level!
```

**Result: Fiscal Policy is COMPLETELY INEFFECTIVE under floating exchange rates**

```
Output (Y): No change
Exchange Rate: APPRECIATES
NX: Falls (crowded out by appreciation)
```

> This is **"International Crowding Out"** — instead of crowding out investment via r, govt. spending crowds out exports via exchange rate appreciation.

---

#### Case 2: Monetary Policy — Floating Rate

```
↑Ms → LM shifts RIGHT
    → Downward pressure on r (below r*)
    → Capital outflows (lower r drives capital away)
    → ↓ Demand for domestic currency
    → Exchange rate DEPRECIATES (↑e)
    → ↑e → ↑NX (exports cheaper, imports dearer)
    → IS shifts RIGHT
    → ↑Y
```

**Result: Monetary Policy is HIGHLY EFFECTIVE under floating exchange rates**

```
Output (Y): RISES significantly
Exchange Rate: DEPRECIATES
NX: RISES
Interest Rate: Stays at r* (no change)
```

---

### Mundell-Fleming Under FIXED Exchange Rates

In a **fixed rate** regime, the Central Bank **commits to maintaining the exchange rate**. This means:
- If currency pressure to appreciate → RBI **buys foreign currency, sells ₹** → Ms increases
- If currency pressure to depreciate → RBI **sells foreign currency, buys ₹** → Ms decreases
- **Ms is endogenous** — RBI cannot independently control it under fixed rates

#### Case 3: Fiscal Policy — Fixed Rate

```
↑G → IS shifts RIGHT
   → Upward pressure on r (above r*)
   → Capital inflows
   → ↑ Demand for domestic currency → ↑ Pressure for appreciation
   → RBI must PREVENT appreciation (fixed rate commitment)
   → RBI buys foreign currency, SELLS domestic currency
   → Ms INCREASES → LM shifts RIGHT
   → r returns to r*, Y rises
```

**Result: Fiscal Policy is HIGHLY EFFECTIVE under fixed exchange rates**

```
Output (Y): RISES (full multiplier effect!)
Exchange Rate: Maintained (fixed — no change)
Ms: Increases automatically (RBI intervention)
```

---

#### Case 4: Monetary Policy — Fixed Rate

```
↑Ms → LM shifts RIGHT
    → Downward pressure on r (below r*)
    → Capital outflows
    → ↓ Demand for domestic currency → Pressure for DEPRECIATION
    → RBI must PREVENT depreciation (fixed rate commitment)
    → RBI sells foreign currency, BUYS domestic currency
    → Ms DECREASES → LM shifts back LEFT to original position
    → Y returns to original level!
```

**Result: Monetary Policy is COMPLETELY INEFFECTIVE under fixed exchange rates**

```
Output (Y): No change
Exchange Rate: Maintained (fixed)
Ms: Returns to original (sterilized by RBI)
```

---

### The Master Summary Table — Mundell-Fleming Policy Effectiveness

| Policy | **Floating Exchange Rate** | **Fixed Exchange Rate** |
|---|---|---|
| **Fiscal Policy (↑G)** | ❌ **INEFFECTIVE** (currency appreciation kills NX) | ✅ **EFFECTIVE** (Ms expands automatically) |
| **Monetary Policy (↑Ms)** | ✅ **EFFECTIVE** (currency depreciation boosts NX) | ❌ **INEFFECTIVE** (Ms sterilized by RBI to maintain peg) |

> 📌 **Memory Trick**: Under each regime, the **other** policy works!
> - Floating → Monetary works, Fiscal doesn't
> - Fixed → Fiscal works, Monetary doesn't

### The Impossible Trinity (Mundell's Trilemma)

One of the most important concepts in international economics:

> **A country CANNOT simultaneously have all three of:**
> 1. **Fixed Exchange Rate**
> 2. **Free Capital Mobility (Full CAC)**
> 3. **Independent Monetary Policy**
>
> *It can only choose TWO out of three.*

```
                    Fixed Exchange Rate
                           ▲
                          / \
                         /   \
                        /     \
                       /       \
         ─────────────•─────────•─────────────
        Free Capital             Independent
        Mobility                 Monetary Policy
        (Full CAC)

Choose any two sides of the triangle — cannot have all three!
```

**How Countries Choose:**

| Country | What They Chose | What They Gave Up |
|---|---|---|
| **Eurozone** | Fixed rate (Euro) + Free capital flow | Independent monetary policy (ECB sets rates for all) |
| **China (historically)** | Fixed rate (RMB peg) + Monetary independence | Free capital mobility (capital controls) |
| **USA, UK, India (managed float)** | Free capital + Monetary independence | Perfect fixed exchange rate |
| **Hong Kong** | Fixed rate + Free capital | Monetary independence (mirrors US Fed) |

**India's Position:**
- Monetary independence ✅ (RBI sets repo rate independently)
- Managed float (not fully fixed) ✅
- Partial capital mobility (not full CAC) ✅
- India sacrifices **some** capital mobility to maintain the other two — justified given Asian crisis lessons

---

### Mundell-Fleming and India

**India has a managed float with partial CAC** — so the pure Mundell-Fleming results don't fully apply, but the intuitions hold:

| Policy | India's Experience |
|---|---|
| **Fiscal expansion** | Works in India (partially) — but risks CAD and inflation |
| **Monetary expansion (↓Repo)** | Works — ↓r → ↑I + some depreciation → ↑NX |
| **Capital controls** | Gives RBI partial insulation — can run monetary policy more independently |
| **Taper Tantrum (2013)** | US Fed signaled ↑ rates → Capital fled India → ₹ crashed 20% → Shows MF dynamics in action |

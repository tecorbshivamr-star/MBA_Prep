## 2.2 Introduction

### What are National Accounts?

**National Accounts** (also called National Income Accounts or System of National Accounts — SNA) are a set of **systematic records** that measure the economic activity of a country over a given period.

> 📌 Think of National Accounts as the **"financial statements of a country"** — just like a company has a balance sheet and P&L, a country has National Accounts.

### Why We Need to Measure the Economy:
- To assess **economic performance** and track growth
- To **compare economies** across countries and time
- To help **government plan** spending, taxation, and fiscal policy
- To help **businesses** make investment decisions
- To design **international trade** and aid policies

### Historical Background:
- **Simon Kuznets** (Nobel Prize, 1971) — Pioneer of National Income Accounting in the USA (1930s)
- **India's CSO** (Central Statistics Office, now MoSPI) publishes India's National Accounts
- **SNA 2008** — International standard for national accounting (UN framework followed by most countries)

---

## 2.3 Need of National Income Accounts

### Why Do We Need National Income Accounts?

#### 1. 📊 Measuring Economic Performance
- Tells us how much the economy has **grown or shrunk**
- GDP growth rate is the primary indicator of economic health
- Helps compare performance **year-over-year**

#### 2. 🏛️ Policy Formulation
- Government uses national accounts to design **fiscal and monetary policy**
- Helps decide: Should we increase spending? Reduce taxes? Raise interest rates?
- **Planning Commission / NITI Aayog** uses it for Five-Year Plans

#### 3. 🌍 International Comparisons
- Allows comparison of India's economy with USA, China, etc.
- Used by **World Bank, IMF** for ranking and aid allocation
- Basis for calculating **PPP (Purchasing Power Parity)** adjusted comparisons

#### 4. 💼 Business & Investment Decisions
- Firms use GDP data to gauge **market size and demand**
- Investors use GDP growth to assess **investment attractiveness**
- MNCs use it to decide **which countries to enter**

#### 5. 📈 Tracking Standard of Living
- Per capita income (NI/Population) is used as a proxy for **standard of living**
- Helps identify **inequality and poverty** trends over time

#### 6. 🔗 Identifying Structural Changes
- Shows how the share of **Agriculture, Industry, Services** in GDP has changed
- India's shift from agriculture-dominated to services-dominated economy is visible through NI data

## 2.4 Approach to Measure Economic Activities

There are **three equivalent approaches** to measure GDP. In theory, all three give the **same answer**.

```
EXPENDITURE APPROACH = INCOME APPROACH = OUTPUT (VALUE ADDED) APPROACH
         GDP = GDP = GDP
```

| Approach | What it Measures | Key Formula |
|---|---|---|
| **Expenditure Approach** | Total spending on final goods & services | GDP = C + I + G + (X − M) |
| **Income Approach** | Total income earned by factors of production | GDP = W + R + I + P + Taxes − Subsidies + Depreciation |
| **Output / Value-Added Approach** | Total value added at each stage of production | GDP = Σ (Output − Intermediate inputs) |

---

### 🔍 Approach 1: Expenditure Method

**GDP = C + I + G + (X − M)**

| Component | Full Form | Description | India Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **C** | Private Consumption | Spending by households on goods/services | Buying food, clothes, phones |
| **I** | Gross Investment | Business spending on capital goods + change in inventories | Factory machinery, new buildings |
| **G** | Government Expenditure | Govt. spending on goods/services (NOT transfers) | Salaries to govt. employees, infrastructure |
| **X** | Exports | Value of goods/services sold to foreigners | IT exports, textiles |
| **M** | Imports | Value of goods/services bought from foreigners | Crude oil, electronics |
| **(X−M)** | Net Exports (NX) | Trade balance | India often has negative NX (imports > exports) |

> ⚠️ **Common Mistake**: Transfer payments (pensions, subsidies, unemployment benefits) are **NOT included** in G — they are not payments for current production.

**Gross vs Net Investment:**
```
Net Investment = Gross Investment − Depreciation
```
- **Gross Investment**: Total new capital formation
- **Net Investment**: Actual addition to capital stock (after accounting for wear & tear)

---

### 🔍 Approach 2: Income Method

**GDP = Compensation of Employees + Gross Operating Surplus + Gross Mixed Income + Taxes on Production − Subsidies**

Or simplified:
```
GDP = Wages (W) + Rent (R) + Interest (I) + Profit (P) + Depreciation + (Indirect Taxes − Subsidies)
```

| Income Component | Description |
|---|---|
| **Wages & Salaries** | Payment for labor services |
| **Rent** | Payment for use of land/property |
| **Interest** | Payment for use of capital/financial assets |
| **Profit** | Return to entrepreneurs after all costs |
| **Depreciation** | Capital consumption allowance |
| **Indirect Taxes − Subsidies** | Adjusts from factor cost to market price |

> 📌 **Key Note**: GDP at Factor Cost + Indirect Taxes − Subsidies = GDP at Market Price

---

### 🔍 Approach 3: Output / Value-Added Method (Production Method)

```
GDP = Sum of Value Added by all sectors in the economy
Value Added = Value of Output − Value of Intermediate Inputs
```

**Why Value Added?** — To avoid **double counting**.

**Example: Bread Production Chain**

| Stage | Producer | Output Value | Intermediate Input | Value Added |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Farmer | ₹50 | ₹0 | **₹50** |
| 2 | Flour Mill | ₹80 | ₹50 | **₹30** |
| 3 | Bakery | ₹120 | ₹80 | **₹40** |
| 4 | Retailer | ₹150 | ₹120 | **₹30** |
| **GDP** | | | | **₹150** |

> ✅ Total Value Added (₹150) = Final Price of Bread (₹150) — No double counting!

**India's GDP by Sector (Output Method):**
| Sector | Sub-sectors | Approx. Share of GDP |
|---|---|---|
| **Agriculture** | Farming, forestry, fishing | ~15-17% |
| **Industry** | Manufacturing, mining, construction, electricity | ~25-28% |
| **Services** | IT, banking, retail, transport, government | ~55-60% |

## 2.5 Measuring GDP (Gross Domestic Product)

### Formal Definition:
**GDP** is the **total market value** of all **final goods and services** produced **within the geographical boundaries** of a country during a **specific time period** (usually one year or one quarter).

### Key Words Explained:

| Key Word | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Market Value** | Measured in monetary terms using market prices |
| **Final Goods & Services** | Only end products; intermediate goods excluded to prevent double counting |
| **Produced** | Only current production counts; second-hand sales excluded |
| **Within Boundaries** | Geographical territory — includes foreigners working in India, excludes Indians abroad |
| **Specific Time Period** | Usually calendar year or financial year (India: April–March) |

---

### What is INCLUDED in GDP?

✅ Market transactions with monetary value
✅ Government services (valued at cost)
✅ Imputed rent (owner-occupied housing)
✅ Goods produced and sold in the same year
✅ New construction

### What is EXCLUDED from GDP?

❌ **Intermediate goods** (raw materials, parts used in production)
❌ **Second-hand goods** (used car sales — already counted when first sold)
❌ **Financial transactions** (buying shares, bonds — not production)
❌ **Transfer payments** (pensions, welfare — no goods/services exchanged)
❌ **Household services** (cooking, cleaning at home — not market transactions)
❌ **Underground/Black economy** (illegal activities)
❌ **Volunteer work**

---

### GDP at Market Price vs GDP at Factor Cost

```
GDP at Market Price = GDP at Factor Cost + Indirect Taxes − Subsidies

OR

GDP at Factor Cost = GDP at Market Price − Indirect Taxes + Subsidies
```

| Concept | Includes | Excludes |
|---|---|---|
| **GDP at Factor Cost** | Factor payments (wages, rent, interest, profit) | Indirect taxes, subsidies |
| **GDP at Market Price** | Factor cost + Indirect taxes − Subsidies | — |

> **India shifted** from GDP at Factor Cost to **Gross Value Added (GVA) at Basic Prices** as the primary measure in 2015 (base year changed to 2011-12).

---

### Nominal GDP vs Real GDP (Recap)

| | **Nominal GDP** | **Real GDP** |
|---|---|---|
| **Prices Used** | Current Year | Base Year (fixed) |
| **Reflects** | Volume + Price changes | Volume changes only |
| **Formula** | GDP = Σ (P_current × Q_current) | GDP = Σ (P_base × Q_current) |

```
Real GDP = (Nominal GDP / GDP Deflator) × 100

GDP Deflator = (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) × 100
```

## 2.6 Practical Exercise (Numericals)

### 📐 Numerical 1: GDP using Expenditure Method

**Given:**
- Private Consumption (C) = ₹800 crore
- Gross Investment (I) = ₹200 crore
- Government Expenditure (G) = ₹150 crore
- Exports (X) = ₹100 crore
- Imports (M) = ₹120 crore

**Find GDP:**
```
GDP = C + I + G + (X − M)
GDP = 800 + 200 + 150 + (100 − 120)
GDP = 800 + 200 + 150 + (−20)
GDP = 1150 − 20
GDP = ₹1,130 crore
```

---

### 📐 Numerical 2: Real GDP from Nominal GDP

**Given:**
- Nominal GDP = ₹2,000 crore
- GDP Deflator = 125

**Find Real GDP:**
```
Real GDP = (Nominal GDP / GDP Deflator) × 100
Real GDP = (2000 / 125) × 100
Real GDP = 16 × 100
Real GDP = ₹1,600 crore
```

**Interpretation**: Prices have risen by 25% since the base year. The economy's actual output is worth ₹1,600 crore in real terms.

---

### 📐 Numerical 3: National Income Hierarchy

**Given:**
- GDP at Market Price = ₹5,000 crore
- Net Factor Income from Abroad (NFIA) = +₹200 crore
- Depreciation = ₹300 crore
- Indirect Taxes = ₹400 crore
- Subsidies = ₹100 crore
- Corporate Taxes = ₹150 crore
- Retained Earnings = ₹100 crore
- Transfer Payments = ₹50 crore
- Personal Direct Taxes = ₹200 crore

**Step-by-step:**
```
GNP (Market Price) = GDP + NFIA
                   = 5000 + 200 = ₹5,200 crore

NNP (Market Price) = GNP − Depreciation
                   = 5200 − 300 = ₹4,900 crore

National Income   = NNP − Indirect Taxes + Subsidies
(NNP at Factor Cost) = 4900 − 400 + 100 = ₹4,600 crore

Personal Income   = NI − Corporate Taxes − Retained Earnings + Transfer Payments
                  = 4600 − 150 − 100 + 50 = ₹4,400 crore

Disposable Income = Personal Income − Personal Direct Taxes
                  = 4400 − 200 = ₹4,200 crore
```

---

### 📐 Numerical 4: Value-Added Method

**Given (Steel Industry Chain):**
| Stage | Output (₹) | Raw Material Used (₹) |
|---|---|---|
| Iron Ore Mining | 1,000 | 0 |
| Steel Production | 3,000 | 1,000 |
| Auto Parts Manufacturing | 7,000 | 3,000 |
| Car Assembly | 12,000 | 7,000 |

**Find GDP Contribution:**
```
Value Added at each stage:
Iron Ore Mining     = 1,000 − 0     = ₹1,000
Steel Production    = 3,000 − 1,000 = ₹2,000
Auto Parts          = 7,000 − 3,000 = ₹4,000
Car Assembly        = 12,000 − 7,000 = ₹5,000

Total GDP = 1,000 + 2,000 + 4,000 + 5,000 = ₹12,000
```
✅ **Same as the final price of the car = ₹12,000** — No double counting!

---

### 📐 Numerical 5: GDP Deflator and Inflation Rate

**Given:**
- Year 2022: Nominal GDP = ₹1,500 crore, Real GDP = ₹1,200 crore
- Year 2023: Nominal GDP = ₹1,800 crore, Real GDP = ₹1,350 crore

**Find:**
1. GDP Deflator for 2022 and 2023
2. Inflation Rate between 2022 and 2023

```
GDP Deflator 2022 = (1500 / 1200) × 100 = 125
GDP Deflator 2023 = (1800 / 1350) × 100 = 133.3

Inflation Rate = [(133.3 − 125) / 125] × 100
              = [8.3 / 125] × 100
              = 6.64%
```

## 2.7 Other Important Concepts

### The Complete National Income Hierarchy

```
GDP at Market Price
    + NFIA (Net Factor Income from Abroad)
    ─────────────────────────────────────
GNP at Market Price
    − Depreciation (Capital Consumption)
    ─────────────────────────────────────
NNP at Market Price
    − Net Indirect Taxes (Indirect Taxes − Subsidies)
    ─────────────────────────────────────
NNP at Factor Cost = National Income (NI)
    − Corporate Taxes
    − Undistributed Profits / Retained Earnings
    + Transfer Payments
    ─────────────────────────────────────
Personal Income (PI)
    − Personal Direct Taxes
    ─────────────────────────────────────
Disposable Personal Income (DPI)
    − Consumption (C)
    ─────────────────────────────────────
Personal Savings (S)
```

---

### Key Concept Definitions

| Concept | Full Form | Definition |
|---|---|---|
| **GNP** | Gross National Product | GDP + Income earned by residents abroad − Income earned by foreigners domestically |
| **NNP** | Net National Product | GNP − Depreciation |
| **NI** | National Income | NNP at Factor Cost = NNP − Indirect Taxes + Subsidies |
| **PI** | Personal Income | Income received by households from all sources |
| **DPI** | Disposable Personal Income | PI − Personal taxes (income available to spend/save) |
| **NFIA** | Net Factor Income from Abroad | Factor income received from abroad − Factor income paid to foreigners |
| **Depreciation** | Capital Consumption Allowance | Reduction in value of capital due to wear and tear |

---

### Per Capita Income

```
Per Capita Income = National Income / Total Population
```

- Used as a measure of **average standard of living**
- India's per capita income (2023-24): ~₹1.7 lakh per annum
- **Limitation**: Doesn't capture **inequality** (Gini coefficient needed for that)

---

### Savings, Investment & the Multiplier

**MPC (Marginal Propensity to Consume)**:
```
MPC = Change in Consumption / Change in Income
```

**MPS (Marginal Propensity to Save)**:
```
MPS = 1 − MPC
```

**Multiplier (k)**:
```
k = 1 / MPS = 1 / (1 − MPC)
```

**Example**: If MPC = 0.8, then:
```
MPS = 1 − 0.8 = 0.2
Multiplier = 1 / 0.2 = 5

→ A ₹100 crore increase in Government spending will increase GDP by ₹500 crore
```

## 2.8 Problems Associated with GDP Calculation in India

> 📌 *This is a HIGH-PRIORITY section for exams — expect a 5–10 mark question on this!*

### India-Specific Challenges in GDP Measurement

---

#### 1. 🏪 Large Informal / Unorganized Sector

- India has one of the world's **largest informal economies**
- ~90% of workers are in the informal sector (street vendors, small shops, domestic workers)
- Informal sector transactions are **not recorded** → Significant **undercounting** of GDP
- **Demonetization (2016)** impact was hard to measure due to this

> **Impact**: India's actual GDP is likely **much higher** than officially reported.

---

#### 2. 🌾 Agriculture Measurement Issues

- **Subsistence farming**: Farmers who produce for self-consumption — difficult to value
- **Imputed value** is used (estimated market price), which is inaccurate
- Seasonal variation makes quarterly estimates unreliable
- Large number of small, dispersed farms make **data collection difficult**

---

#### 3. 💰 Black Economy / Underground Economy

- Illegal activities (drug trade, corruption, hawala) are **completely excluded** from GDP
- Tax evasion means a large portion of legitimate income is **not reported**
- India's black economy estimated at **~20–25% of GDP**
- This leads to a **significant underestimation** of true economic output

---

#### 4. 📊 Data Collection and Reliability

- India is a **vast and diverse** country — data collection is extremely challenging
- Many regions (rural, tribal, remote) have **poor data infrastructure**
- Different states have **varying quality** of statistical systems
- Frequent **revisions** to GDP data — often large and significant
- **Base year revisions**: India changed base year from 2004-05 to **2011-12** in 2015

---

#### 5. 🏠 Non-Market Activities

- **Household work** (cooking, cleaning, childcare) is NOT counted in GDP
- **Volunteer work** and community services excluded
- In India, a large share of economic activity is non-monetized
- Women's contribution through unpaid household work is invisible in GDP data

> **Example**: If a woman cooks at home — not counted in GDP. If she works in a restaurant — counted in GDP. Same activity, different treatment!

---

#### 6. 🔄 Informal Credit and Barter

- **Barter transactions** (exchanging goods without money) not captured
- Rural India still has significant **non-cash transactions**
- Informal lending (money lenders, chit funds) not properly recorded

---

#### 7. 📱 Digital Economy Challenges

- **Free digital services** (Google Search, WhatsApp, YouTube) add immense value but have **zero market price** → Not counted in GDP
- E-commerce, gig economy (Ola, Swiggy) workers — classified differently in different surveys
- **Cryptocurrency transactions** — not tracked at all

---

#### 8. ⚖️ Price Index Problems

- Constructing an appropriate **price deflator** for India's diverse economy is extremely difficult
- Different regions have very different **price levels**
- WPI and CPI can give conflicting signals about inflation

---

#### 9. 🔢 Revision Problem

- India's GDP estimates undergo **multiple revisions** (Advance Estimate → First Revised → Second Revised → Final)
- Sometimes revisions are significantly different from initial estimates
- **2011-12 base year revision** shocked many as it significantly changed India's GDP growth story

---

### Summary Table: GDP Problems in India

| Problem | Nature | Impact on GDP |
|---|---|---|
| Informal Sector | Unrecorded transactions | **Underestimated** |
| Black Economy | Unreported income | **Underestimated** |
| Non-Market Activities | Not counted | **Underestimated** |
| Subsistence Agriculture | Hard to value | **Inaccurate** |
| Data Quality | Poor collection | **Unreliable** |
| Free Digital Services | Zero price | **Underestimated** |
| Price Index Issues | Deflator errors | **Real GDP distorted** |

## 2.9 Green GDP

### What is Green GDP?

**Green GDP** adjusts conventional GDP to account for **environmental costs** and the **depletion of natural resources** in the production process.

```
Green GDP = GDP − Environmental Costs − Resource Depletion Costs
```

Or:

```
Green GDP = GDP − Cost of Environmental Degradation
                − Cost of Natural Capital Depletion
                + Value of Environmental Services
```

---

### Why is Green GDP Needed?

**Problem with Traditional GDP:**
- GDP counts cutting down forests as **positive** economic activity (timber value added)
- GDP counts pollution cleanup as **positive** (money spent on cleanup = GDP)
- Oil extraction increases GDP, but depletes a non-renewable resource — **not captured**
- Environmental damage (floods, droughts, pollution-related illness) reduces welfare but may **increase** GDP (disaster relief, hospital spending)

> 📌 *"Cutting down a forest increases GDP twice — once when you sell the timber, and again when you spend money planting trees."*

---

### Components of Environmental Adjustment

| Adjustment | What it Accounts For |
|---|---|
| **Resource Depletion** | Oil, coal, minerals, forests used up in production |
| **Pollution Costs** | Air, water, soil pollution caused by economic activity |
| **Ecosystem Services** | Value of services provided by nature (clean air, water purification, biodiversity) |
| **Carbon Costs** | Economic cost of greenhouse gas emissions |

---

### Green GDP vs Traditional GDP

| Feature | **Traditional GDP** | **Green GDP** |
|---|---|---|
| **Environmental costs** | Ignored | Deducted |
| **Resource depletion** | Not counted | Subtracted |
| **Nature's services** | Excluded | Can be added |
| **Result** | Higher number | Lower but more accurate |
| **Policy implication** | Promotes growth at any cost | Promotes sustainable growth |

---

### India & Green GDP

- **India's GDP growth** often comes at **significant environmental cost** (deforestation, river pollution, air quality)
- The **ENRAP (Environment and Natural Resource Accounting Project)** in India attempts to build environmental accounts
- **Genuine Progress Indicator (GPI)** is a broader alternative that also adjusts for inequality, crime, and leisure

**Countries attempting Green GDP:**
- **China** was an early adopter but faced political resistance (Green GDP showed negative growth in some provinces)
- **Europe**: Uses **SEEA (System of Environmental-Economic Accounting)**
- **India**: Still working on comprehensive green accounting framework

---

### Alternatives to GDP as a Welfare Measure

| Measure | Full Form | What it Captures |
|---|---|---|
| **HDI** | Human Development Index | Income + Education + Health |
| **GPI** | Genuine Progress Indicator | GDP adjusted for inequality, crime, environment |
| **GNH** | Gross National Happiness | Bhutan's measure — well-being, culture, environment |
| **ISEW** | Index of Sustainable Economic Welfare | Economic welfare minus social/environmental costs |
| **Green GDP** | Environmentally Adjusted GDP | GDP minus environmental degradation costs |

> **HDI Formula**: HDI = ∛(Income Index × Education Index × Health Index)
> India's HDI Rank (2023): **134 out of 193** countries

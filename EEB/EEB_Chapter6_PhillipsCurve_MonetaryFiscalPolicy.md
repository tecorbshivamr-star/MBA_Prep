## 6.3 Phillips Curve

### The Original Phillips Curve (A.W. Phillips, 1958)

**A.W. Phillips** studied UK data (1861–1957) and found a **stable inverse relationship** between **wage inflation** (later generalized to price inflation) and **unemployment**.

> 📌 *"You can have low unemployment OR low inflation — but not both at the same time."*

### The Trade-Off

```
↓ Unemployment → ↑ Inflation   (economy heats up, workers demand higher wages)
↑ Unemployment → ↓ Inflation   (slack in economy, less wage pressure)
```

**Short-Run Phillips Curve (SRPC) Diagram:**

```
Inflation
Rate (π)
    │
  8%│ • A (Low unemployment, High inflation)
    │  \
  5%│   • B
    │    \
  2%│     • C (High unemployment, Low inflation)
    │      \
    │       \ SRPC
    └──────────────────────────────────► Unemployment Rate (u)
         3%   5%    7%
```

- Moving from C → A: Policy **stimulates economy** → ↓u but ↑π
- Moving from A → C: Policy **tightens economy** → ↑u but ↓π

### Policy Implication of Original Phillips Curve

Governments thought they could **choose** any point on the SRPC:
- Want low unemployment? Accept higher inflation → **Expansionary policy**
- Want low inflation? Accept higher unemployment → **Contractionary policy**

**This seemed to work in the 1950s and 1960s...**

### The Breakdown — 1970s Stagflation

In the 1970s (especially after the 1973 OPEC oil shock), many countries experienced **both high inflation AND high unemployment** simultaneously — something the original Phillips Curve said was **impossible**.

```
The trade-off DISAPPEARED:
→ Inflation rose to 10-15%
→ Unemployment also rose
→ SRPC shifted UPWARD and OUTWARD
```

This shattered faith in the original Phillips Curve and led to the **Expectation-Augmented version**.

## 6.4 Expectation-Augmented Phillips Curve

### Friedman-Phelps Critique (1968)

**Milton Friedman** and **Edmund Phelps** independently predicted (before the 1970s crisis!) that the original Phillips Curve would break down. Their key insight:

> **"The Phillips Curve trade-off is only temporary. In the long run, there is NO trade-off between inflation and unemployment."**

### The Role of Expectations

Workers and firms care about **real wages**, not nominal wages. They form **expectations about inflation** (πᵉ) when negotiating:

```
Expectation-Augmented Phillips Curve:
π = πᵉ − β(u − u*)

Where:
π  = actual inflation
πᵉ = expected inflation
β  = sensitivity of inflation to unemployment gap
u  = actual unemployment rate
u* = natural rate of unemployment (NAIRU)
```

**Key Insight**:
- When **u = u\*** → π = πᵉ (inflation equals expected inflation)
- When **u < u\*** → π > πᵉ (surprise: actual inflation exceeds expectations)
- When **u > u\*** → π < πᵉ (inflation lower than expected)

---

### Short-Run vs Long-Run Phillips Curve

**Short Run**: SRPC exists — trade-off between π and u

**Long Run**: LRPC is **VERTICAL** at u* (Natural Rate of Unemployment)

```
Inflation
Rate (π)
    │        │ LRPC (vertical at u*)
    │        │
 10%│  SRPC₃ │ • C (πᵉ=10%)
    │   \    │
  6%│ SRPC₂ │ • B (πᵉ=6%)
    │    \   │
  3%│SRPC₁  │ • A (πᵉ=3%)
    │  \     │
    │   \    │
    └───────────────────────────► Unemployment (u)
              u* (Natural Rate)
```

**Each SRPC corresponds to a different level of expected inflation (πᵉ)**

---

### The Adaptive Expectations Story (How SRPC Shifts)

**Step-by-step process:**

1. **Start** at A (u=u*, π=πᵉ=3%) on SRPC₁
2. **Government expands** demand → u falls below u* (short-run, move along SRPC₁) → π rises to 6%
3. **Workers update expectations**: πᵉ rises from 3% to 6%
4. **SRPC shifts UP** to SRPC₂ (now built on πᵉ=6%)
5. **To keep u below u***, must accept even higher inflation (π=10%)
6. **Expectations rise again** → SRPC₃... and so on
7. **Result**: Accelerating inflation with no permanent gain in employment

> **Conclusion**: Attempting to keep unemployment below u* permanently leads to ever-accelerating inflation (the **Accelerationist Hypothesis**)

---

### Rational Expectations (Lucas Critique)

**Robert Lucas** (Nobel Prize 1995) argued that people don't just adapt expectations slowly — they form **rational expectations** using all available information:

- If people **perfectly anticipate** a policy → Policy has **NO short-run effect** either
- Surprise expansionary policy → Short-run ↓u; Anticipated policy → No effect even in SR
- **Policy credibility matters enormously**

---

### Disinflation — Bringing Inflation Down

To **reduce inflation** (move down LRPC toward lower π at u*):
- Accept a **temporary period of high unemployment** (u > u*)
- SRPC shifts DOWN as inflationary expectations fall
- **Sacrifice Ratio**: GDP loss (or extra unemployment) per 1% reduction in inflation

```
Sacrifice Ratio = (% extra unemployment × years) / % reduction in inflation
```

**India's Experience**: RBI's inflation targeting framework (4% ± 2% target) anchors expectations → Prevents SRPC from shifting up → Keeps inflation manageable

## 6.5 Fiscal Policy

### Definition

**Fiscal Policy** = Government's use of **taxation (T)** and **spending (G)** to influence aggregate demand and economic activity.

### Tools of Fiscal Policy

| Tool | Expansionary (Stimulus) | Contractionary (Austerity) |
|---|---|---|
| **Government Spending** | ↑G (more roads, hospitals, salaries) | ↓G (spending cuts) |
| **Taxation** | ↓T (tax cuts, rebates) | ↑T (higher tax rates) |
| **Transfer Payments** | ↑ (MGNREGA, PM-Kisan, subsidies) | ↓ (reduce welfare spending) |

### Fiscal Multiplier (Recap)

```
Government Spending Multiplier = 1 / (1 − MPC)
Tax Multiplier                 = −MPC / (1 − MPC)
Balanced Budget Multiplier     = 1
```

**Example**: MPC = 0.75
```
Spending Multiplier = 1/(1-0.75) = 4
Tax Multiplier      = -0.75/0.25 = -3
```
→ ₹100 crore extra G → ↑GDP by ₹400 crore
→ ₹100 crore extra T → ↓GDP by ₹300 crore

### Types of Fiscal Policy

**Discretionary Fiscal Policy**: Deliberate government decisions
- Budget changes, new schemes, tax reforms

**Automatic Stabilizers**: Automatic without government action
| Stabilizer | Recession | Boom |
|---|---|---|
| **Progressive income tax** | Tax revenue falls automatically (less income) | Tax revenue rises automatically |
| **Unemployment benefits** | Rise automatically (more unemployed) | Fall automatically |
| **Corporate tax** | Falls (lower profits) | Rises (higher profits) |

> Automatic stabilizers reduce the **amplitude** of business cycles without any government decision

### Limitations of Fiscal Policy

| Limitation | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Time Lags** | Recognition lag + Legislative lag + Implementation lag (can total 12-18 months) |
| **Crowding Out** | ↑G → ↑r → ↓I (private investment falls) |
| **Ricardian Equivalence** | Rational people save tax cuts to pay future taxes → No consumption effect |
| **Political Constraints** | Governments may over-stimulate before elections (political business cycle) |
| **Debt Sustainability** | Persistent deficits → ↑National debt → Future tax burden |
| **Inflationary Risk** | Excessive stimulus when economy already at Y* |

### India's Fiscal Policy Framework

| Concept | Details |
|---|---|
| **FRBM Act (2003)** | Fiscal Responsibility & Budget Management — targets 3% fiscal deficit/GDP |
| **Budget** | Union Budget presented in February each year |
| **Revenue Deficit** | Revenue expenditure > Revenue receipts |
| **Fiscal Deficit** | Total expenditure − Total receipts (excl. borrowings) |
| **Primary Deficit** | Fiscal Deficit − Interest Payments |

## 6.6 Monetary Policy

### Definition

**Monetary Policy** = Central Bank's (RBI) management of **money supply** and **interest rates** to achieve macroeconomic objectives.

### Objectives of Monetary Policy

1. **Price stability** (control inflation) — RBI's PRIMARY mandate
2. **Economic growth** (support GDP growth)
3. **Financial stability** (prevent banking crises)
4. **Exchange rate stability** (maintain ₹ value)
5. **Full employment** (indirect objective)

### Tools of Monetary Policy

#### Quantitative Tools (Direct Control of Money Supply)

| Tool | Definition | Expansionary | Contractionary |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Repo Rate** | Rate at which RBI lends to banks short-term | ↓ Repo Rate | ↑ Repo Rate |
| **Reverse Repo Rate** | Rate at which RBI borrows from banks | ↓ Rev. Repo | ↑ Rev. Repo |
| **CRR** | % of deposits banks must hold with RBI as cash | ↓ CRR | ↑ CRR |
| **SLR** | % of deposits banks must hold in govt. securities | ↓ SLR | ↑ SLR |
| **OMO** | RBI buys/sells government securities in open market | Buy securities (↑Ms) | Sell securities (↓Ms) |
| **MSF** | Marginal Standing Facility — emergency overnight lending | — | ↑ MSF rate |

#### Qualitative Tools (Selective Credit Control)

- **Credit Rationing**: Limits on lending to certain sectors
- **Moral Suasion**: RBI advises/persuades banks (informal)
- **Margin Requirements**: % of loan that must be collateral
- **Selective Credit Control**: Restrict loans to certain commodities (e.g., food grains during shortage)

---

### Monetary Policy Transmission Mechanism

How a change in repo rate reaches the real economy:

```
RBI cuts Repo Rate
        ↓
Banks' borrowing cost falls
        ↓
Banks reduce lending rates (MCLR, repo-linked rates)
        ↓
Cheaper credit for businesses and consumers
        ↓
↑ Investment (firms borrow to invest)
↑ Consumption (individuals borrow more)
        ↓
↑ Aggregate Demand (AD shifts right)
        ↓
↑ Output (Y) and ↑ Employment
        ↓
Possible ↑ Inflation (if near Y*)
```

**Transmission Lag**: 6 to 18 months for full effect on inflation

### Monetary Policy in India — Key Numbers (2024)

| Rate | Value |
|---|---|
| Repo Rate | 6.50% |
| Reverse Repo Rate | 3.35% |
| CRR | 4.5% |
| SLR | 18% |
| Inflation Target | 4% (±2% band: 2-6%) |

## 6.7 Neutrality of Money

### Definition

**Neutrality of Money** states that changes in the **money supply affect only nominal variables** (prices, nominal wages, nominal GDP) but have **NO effect on real variables** (real GDP, employment, real wages) in the **long run**.

```
In the Long Run:
↑ Money Supply → ↑ All prices proportionally → ↑ Nominal GDP
BUT: Real GDP, Real Wages, Employment → UNCHANGED
```

### Quantity Theory of Money

```
MV = PY

Where:
M = Money Supply
V = Velocity of money (how fast money circulates)
P = Price Level
Y = Real Output (GDP)
```

If **V and Y are constant** (long-run assumptions):
```
↑M → ↑P proportionally (1% ↑M → 1% ↑P)
```

**Implication**: Inflation is always and everywhere a **monetary phenomenon** (Milton Friedman)

### Short Run vs Long Run Neutrality

| Time Horizon | Money Neutral? | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| **Short Run** | ❌ No — Money is NOT neutral | Prices and wages are sticky; ↑Ms → ↓r → ↑I → ↑Y (real effects) |
| **Long Run** | ✅ Yes — Money IS neutral | Prices fully adjust; real variables unchanged |

**Super-Neutrality**: Even the **growth rate** of money supply has no long-run real effect (only affects the inflation rate, not real growth)

### Implications of Neutrality of Money

1. **Monetary policy cannot raise long-run output** — only TFP and capital accumulation can
2. **Central banks should focus on price stability**, not output maximization
3. **Printing money** only causes inflation in the long run, not real growth
4. **Hyperinflation** results from excessive money printing (Zimbabwe, Venezuela examples)

## 6.8 Crowding Out

### Definition

**Crowding Out** occurs when **expansionary fiscal policy** (↑G) **raises interest rates**, which **reduces private investment**, partially (or fully) offsetting the initial stimulus.

### Mechanism

```
↑G (Fiscal Expansion)
    ↓
Government borrows more (issues bonds)
    ↓
↑ Demand for loanable funds
    ↓
↑ Interest Rate (r)
    ↓
↓ Private Investment (I) — borrowing becomes costlier
    ↓
Partial offset of initial ↑G → Less-than-full multiplier effect
```

### Types of Crowding Out

| Type | Description | Degree |
|---|---|---|
| **Financial Crowding Out** | ↑r reduces private investment (standard IS-LM mechanism) | Partial |
| **Resource Crowding Out** | Govt. uses up real resources (workers, materials) → Less for private sector | Partial |
| **Complete Crowding Out** | Every ₹1 of G displaces ₹1 of private I | 100% (Classical case — vertical LM) |
| **Zero Crowding Out** | LM is flat (Liquidity Trap) — ↑G doesn't affect r → No reduction in I | 0% |

### Factors Determining Degree of Crowding Out

| Factor | More Crowding Out | Less Crowding Out |
|---|---|---|
| **LM slope** | Steep LM (inelastic money demand) | Flat LM (elastic money demand) |
| **IS slope** | Flat IS (investment sensitive to r) | Steep IS (investment insensitive to r) |
| **Economy state** | Near full employment (Y ≈ Y*) | Large output gap (deep recession) |
| **Monetary policy** | No monetary accommodation | RBI also expands Ms simultaneously |

### Crowding In

The **opposite** of crowding out — when government spending **stimulates** private investment:
- Govt. builds roads → Private logistics firms invest more
- Govt. funds basic research → Private firms commercialize innovations
- Often observed in **developing countries** where public infrastructure is complementary to private investment

> **India Example**: Govt. investment in highways (NHAI) crowded IN private investment in real estate, logistics, and manufacturing along corridors.

## 6.9 Liquidity Trap

### Definition

A **Liquidity Trap** occurs when interest rates are so **low** (near zero) that:
- People prefer to hold cash rather than bonds (expecting r to rise → bond prices to fall)
- Money demand becomes **perfectly elastic** (horizontal LM)
- Further increases in money supply **cannot lower r further**
- **Monetary policy becomes ineffective**

### The Mechanism

```
RBI ↑ Ms (money supply)
    ↓
Normally: ↓r → ↑I → ↑AD
BUT in Liquidity Trap:
r is already at zero floor → Cannot fall further
→ Extra money just sits idle (people hoard cash)
→ No ↑ Investment → No ↑ AD → No ↑ Y
```

### IS-LM Diagram in Liquidity Trap

```
Interest
Rate (r)
    │
    │    IS
  r₁│   \
    │    \
    │     \
  0%│──────────────────── LM (horizontal — liquidity trap zone)
    │
    └──────────────────────────────────► Output (Y)
         Y₁   Y*
         (stuck here)
```

- AD remains at Y₁ < Y* despite ↑Ms
- Only fiscal policy (shifting IS right) can raise Y toward Y*

### Policy Implications in Liquidity Trap

| Policy | Effectiveness | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| **Monetary Policy** | ❌ Ineffective | r can't fall below zero; money hoarded |
| **Fiscal Policy** | ✅ Fully effective | No crowding out (r doesn't rise at zero bound) |
| **Unconventional Monetary Policy** | ✅ Partial | Quantitative Easing, forward guidance, helicopter money |

### Unconventional Monetary Policy (Zero Lower Bound Solutions)

When conventional rate cuts fail:

| Tool | How It Works |
|---|---|
| **Quantitative Easing (QE)** | Central bank buys long-term bonds/assets → Injects money, lowers long-term rates |
| **Negative Interest Rates** | Central bank charges banks for excess reserves → Forces lending (Japan, ECB) |
| **Forward Guidance** | Central bank promises to keep rates low long into future → Lowers long-term expectations |
| **Helicopter Money** | Direct cash transfers to citizens (fiscal+monetary hybrid) |

**Real-World Examples:**
- **Japan (1990s–2000s)**: Classic liquidity trap — despite near-zero rates, economy stagnated
- **USA (2008–2015)**: Fed used QE (bought $3.5 trillion in assets) to fight liquidity trap
- **India (COVID 2020)**: RBI cut rates aggressively + used targeted LTROs (Long Term Repo Operations)

## 6.10 Role of the Central Bank

### Reserve Bank of India (RBI) — Overview

- **Established**: 1935 (nationalized 1949)
- **Headquarters**: Mumbai
- **Governor**: Currently Sanjay Malhotra (as of 2024)
- **Legal Framework**: RBI Act, 1934

### Core Functions of RBI

#### 1. 🏦 Monetary Authority
- Formulates and executes **monetary policy**
- Sets **Repo Rate, CRR, SLR**
- **Inflation Target**: 4% CPI (±2% band) — mandated by government
- **Monetary Policy Committee (MPC)**: 6 members (3 RBI + 3 Govt. nominees); meets every 2 months

#### 2. 💰 Currency Issuer
- Sole authority to **issue currency notes** (except ₹1 coin — issued by Government)
- Manages **currency in circulation**
- Responsible for note printing and distribution

#### 3. 🏛️ Banker to the Government
- Manages **Government's accounts** (receipts and payments)
- Manages **public debt** (issues govt. bonds/T-bills)
- Provides **Ways and Means Advances** (short-term credit to govt.)
- Advises govt. on financial matters

#### 4. 🏢 Banker to Banks (Lender of Last Resort)
- Maintains **accounts of commercial banks** (CRR balances)
- Provides emergency liquidity to banks in crisis (**Lender of Last Resort**)
- Operates **clearing house** for inter-bank settlements
- RTGS and NEFT settlement backbone

#### 5. 📊 Regulator of Financial System
- Regulates and supervises **commercial banks, NBFCs, cooperative banks**
- Issues **banking licenses**
- Handles **Prompt Corrective Action (PCA)** for weak banks
- Oversees **NPA (Non-Performing Assets)** management

#### 6. 🌍 Foreign Exchange Manager
- Manages India's **Foreign Exchange Reserves** (~$640+ billion)
- Intervenes in forex market to stabilize **Rupee**
- Administers **FEMA (Foreign Exchange Management Act)**
- Regulates **FDI and FPI flows**

#### 7. 📈 Development Role
- Promotes **financial inclusion** (Jan Dhan Yojana support)
- Develops **payment systems** (UPI, NEFT, RTGS, IMPS)
- Sets up **priority sector lending** norms
- Regulates **microfinance institutions**

---

### RBI's Monetary Policy Framework

**Inflation Targeting (adopted 2016):**
```
Target: CPI Inflation = 4%
Tolerance Band: 2% to 6%
If inflation > 6% for 3 consecutive quarters → RBI must explain to government
```

**Taylor Rule** (conceptual framework for setting rates):
```
Nominal Interest Rate = r* + π* + α(π − π*) + β(Y − Y*)

Where:
r*  = Real neutral interest rate
π*  = Inflation target (4% for India)
π   = Actual inflation
Y   = Actual output
Y*  = Potential output
α,β = Weights (typically 0.5 each)
```

---

### Independence of Central Bank

**Why Central Bank Independence Matters:**

| With Independence | Without Independence |
|---|---|
| Focuses on long-run price stability | Pressured to print money before elections |
| Credible inflation commitment | Inflation expectations unanchored |
| Lower inflation on average | Political business cycle |
| Better growth long-term | Hyperinflation risk |

**RBI's Independence:**
- Operationally independent (sets rates independently)
- BUT inflation target set by **Government** (not RBI alone)
- Government-RBI tensions have occurred (2018-19 — governance reserves controversy)

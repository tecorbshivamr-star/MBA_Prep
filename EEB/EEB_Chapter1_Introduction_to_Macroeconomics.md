## 1.2 Introduction

### What is Macroeconomics?

**Macroeconomics** is the branch of economics that studies the **economy as a whole** — focusing on aggregate-level phenomena rather than individual markets or consumers.

| Feature | **Macroeconomics** | **Microeconomics** |
|---|---|---|
| **Scope** | Entire economy | Individual firms/consumers |
| **Focus** | GDP, inflation, unemployment | Price, supply, demand |
| **Goal** | National well-being | Efficiency of individual decisions |
| **Example** | Why does a country face inflation? | Why does the price of rice rise? |

### Why Does Macroeconomics Matter for MBA?

- Helps managers understand the **external economic environment**
- Shapes **business strategy** (investment, hiring, pricing)
- Influences **government policy** — which directly impacts businesses
- Explains **recessions, booms, inflation** — all of which affect profitability

### Key Macroeconomic Questions:
1. What determines a country's **standard of living**?
2. Why does **unemployment** rise and fall?
3. What causes **inflation**?
4. How can the **government** use policy to stabilize the economy?

## 1.3 Macroeconomic Variables: Meaning and Relationships

### The Big 4 Macroeconomic Variables

| Variable | Definition | How Measured |
|---|---|---|
| **GDP (Output)** | Total market value of all final goods & services produced in a country in a given year | Rs. / $ terms |
| **Unemployment** | % of labor force actively looking for work but unable to find it | Unemployment Rate (%) |
| **Inflation** | Sustained increase in the general price level | CPI / WPI / GDP Deflator |
| **Interest Rate** | Cost of borrowing money | % per annum |

---

### 🔄 The Circular Flow of Income (KEY DIAGRAM)

The economy consists of two main sectors: **Households** and **Firms**.

```
         GOODS & SERVICES MARKET
         ┌─────────────────────┐
         │   Firms sell goods  │
         │ Households buy goods│
         └─────────────────────┘
              ↑               ↓
   FIRMS ←←← Revenue    Expenditure →→→ HOUSEHOLDS
              ↓               ↑
         ┌─────────────────────┐
         │  FACTOR MARKET      │
         │ Households sell     │
         │ Labor/Capital/Land  │
         │ Firms pay wages/rent│
         └─────────────────────┘
```

**Key Flows:**
- **Households → Firms**: Labor, Capital, Land (Factor Inputs)
- **Firms → Households**: Wages, Rent, Interest, Profit (Factor Payments = National Income)
- **Firms → Households**: Goods & Services
- **Households → Firms**: Expenditure on goods & services (= GDP)

> **💡 Key Insight**: In a closed economy (no government, no trade), **GDP = National Income = Total Expenditure**. All three are equal.

---

### Leakages and Injections (Open Economy with Government)

When we add **Government** and **Foreign Sector**:

| **Leakages** (Money flowing OUT of circular flow) | **Injections** (Money flowing IN) |
|---|---|
| Savings (S) | Investment (I) |
| Taxes (T) | Government Spending (G) |
| Imports (M) | Exports (X) |

**Equilibrium Condition:**
```
S + T + M = I + G + X
```

---

### Relationships Between Key Variables

#### 1. GDP & Unemployment (Okun's Law)
- **Inverse Relationship**: When GDP grows, unemployment falls
- Every **1% increase in unemployment** → **~2% decrease in GDP**

#### 2. Inflation & Unemployment (Phillips Curve)
- **Short-run Trade-off**: Low unemployment → High inflation (and vice versa)
- Policymakers face a dilemma — cannot eliminate both simultaneously in the short run

#### 3. Interest Rates & Investment
- **Inverse Relationship**: Higher interest rates → Costlier loans → Less investment → Lower GDP
- Central Bank (RBI in India) uses interest rates as a tool to control inflation

#### 4. Government Spending & GDP
- **Direct Relationship**: Increase in G → Increase in GDP (Multiplier Effect)
- **Multiplier = 1 / (1 - MPC)** where MPC = Marginal Propensity to Consume

## 1.4 Fiscal Policy, Budget Deficits, and Budget Surplus

### Fiscal Policy

**Definition**: The use of **government spending (G)** and **taxation (T)** to influence the economy.

**Objective**: Stabilize output, employment, and prices.

| Type | Action | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Expansionary Fiscal Policy** | ↑ G or ↓ T | Stimulates economy; used in recession |
| **Contractionary Fiscal Policy** | ↓ G or ↑ T | Cools down economy; used during inflation |

---

### Government Budget

```
Government Budget = Tax Revenue (T) − Government Expenditure (G)
```

| Situation | Condition | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Budget Surplus** | T > G | Government earns more than it spends; economy might be over-taxed or under-invested |
| **Balanced Budget** | T = G | Revenue equals expenditure |
| **Budget Deficit** | T < G | Government spends more than it earns; borrows to meet shortfall |

---

### Budget Deficit — Deep Dive

**How is it Financed?**
1. **Borrowing** (Government issues bonds — debt financing)
2. **Printing Money** (Monetization — leads to inflation)

**Fiscal Deficit vs Budget Deficit:**
| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Budget Deficit** | Total revenue - Total expenditure (if negative) |
| **Fiscal Deficit** | Budget Deficit + Off-budget borrowings |
| **Primary Deficit** | Fiscal Deficit − Interest Payments |

**Crowding Out Effect:**
- When govt. borrows heavily → ↑ demand for funds → ↑ interest rates
- Higher interest rates → private investment falls
- Government "crowds out" private sector investment

**India's Fiscal Deficit Target** (FRBM Act): 3% of GDP

---

### Automatic Stabilizers vs Discretionary Policy

| **Automatic Stabilizers** | **Discretionary Policy** |
|---|---|
| No deliberate action needed | Requires legislative/executive decision |
| E.g., Unemployment benefits, progressive taxes | E.g., Stimulus package, new tax scheme |
| Activates automatically during recession | Planned and time-consuming |

## 1.5 Business Cycle

### Definition
A **business cycle** (also called trade cycle) refers to the **recurring pattern of expansion and contraction** in economic activity over time.

> 📌 *"Economy never grows in a straight line — it fluctuates around its long-term trend."*

---

### Phases of the Business Cycle

```
GDP / Output
     │                 PEAK
     │              ╱       ╲
     │           ╱             ╲         PEAK (next)
     │        ╱                  ╲     ╱
     │  ╱                          ╲ ╱
     │╱  RECOVERY    RECESSION        RECOVERY
     │  EXPANSION                TROUGH
     └──────────────────────────────────────► Time

         TROUGH = Bottom of recession
         PEAK   = Top of expansion
```

| Phase | Also Called | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Expansion** | Boom / Recovery | Rising GDP, rising employment, rising income, consumer confidence high |
| **Peak** | Upper Turning Point | Maximum output; inflation pressures build; unemployment at lowest |
| **Contraction** | Recession / Downswing | Falling GDP, rising unemployment, falling investment |
| **Trough** | Lower Turning Point | Lowest point; GDP bottoms out; begins recovery |

> **Recession Definition**: Two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth.
> **Depression**: A severe and prolonged recession (e.g., Great Depression of 1930s).

---

### Leading, Lagging, and Coincident Indicators

| Type | Definition | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Leading Indicators** | Change BEFORE economy changes (predict future) | Stock market, building permits, new orders |
| **Coincident Indicators** | Change ALONG WITH economy (real-time) | GDP, employment level, industrial production |
| **Lagging Indicators** | Change AFTER economy changes (confirm trend) | Unemployment rate, loan defaults, inflation |

---

### Causes of Business Cycles

1. **Demand-side shocks**: Change in consumer spending, investment, exports
2. **Supply-side shocks**: Oil price changes, natural disasters, technology shocks
3. **Monetary factors**: Changes in money supply, interest rate decisions
4. **Animal Spirits**: Business and consumer confidence/sentiment (Keynes)

## 1.6 National Income Accounts

### What is National Income?

**National Income** is the **total monetary value of all final goods and services** produced by the residents of a country during a given period (usually one year).

---

### Key Concepts and Definitions

| Concept | Definition | Formula |
|---|---|---|
| **GDP** | Market value of all final goods/services produced **within a country's territory** in a year | C + I + G + (X−M) |
| **GNP** | Market value of all final goods/services produced **by a country's residents** (anywhere in world) | GDP + Net Factor Income from Abroad |
| **NNP** | GNP minus depreciation (wear & tear of capital) | GNP − Depreciation |
| **National Income (NI)** | NNP at Factor Cost | NNP − Indirect Taxes + Subsidies |
| **Personal Income (PI)** | Income actually received by individuals | NI − Corporate Taxes − Retained Earnings + Transfer Payments |
| **Disposable Income (DI)** | Income left after paying personal taxes | PI − Personal Taxes |

---

### The Hierarchy (Important for Exam!)

```
GDP (Market Price)
    ↓ + Net Factor Income from Abroad (NFIA)
GNP (Market Price)
    ↓ − Depreciation
NNP (Market Price)
    ↓ − Indirect Taxes + Subsidies
National Income / NNP at Factor Cost
    ↓ − Corporate Taxes − Retained Earnings + Transfer Payments
Personal Income
    ↓ − Personal Direct Taxes
Disposable Personal Income
```

---

### Three Approaches to Measuring GDP

#### 1️⃣ Expenditure Approach (Most Common)

```
GDP = C + I + G + (X − M)
```

| Component | Full Form | What it Includes |
|---|---|---|
| **C** | Consumption | Household spending on goods & services |
| **I** | Investment | Business spending on machinery, buildings, inventories |
| **G** | Government Spending | Public spending (not transfers like pensions) |
| **X** | Exports | Goods/services sold abroad |
| **M** | Imports | Goods/services bought from abroad |
| **(X-M)** | Net Exports | Also called Trade Balance |

> **Transfer Payments** (pensions, subsidies) are NOT included in G as they don't represent production.

---

#### 2️⃣ Income Approach

```
GDP = Wages + Rent + Interest + Profit + (Indirect Taxes − Subsidies) + Depreciation
```

- Adds up all **factor incomes** earned in production
- Also called **GDP at Factor Cost** (before taxes, after subsidies)

---

#### 3️⃣ Output/Value-Added Approach (Production Approach)

```
GDP = Sum of Value Added at each stage of production
Value Added = Output Value − Input (Intermediate Goods) Cost
```

**Example:**
| Stage | Output Value | Intermediate Cost | Value Added |
|---|---|---|---|
| Farmer (wheat) | ₹100 | ₹0 | ₹100 |
| Miller (flour) | ₹150 | ₹100 | ₹50 |
| Baker (bread) | ₹200 | ₹150 | ₹50 |
| **Total GDP** | | | **₹200** |

> *Only the **final value** (₹200) counts — avoids **double counting**.*

---

### GDP vs GNP — When to Use Which?

| Measure | Best For | India Context |
|---|---|---|
| **GDP** | Measuring production within borders | Preferred in India |
| **GNP** | Measuring output by citizens (including diaspora) | Relevant for remittances |

> **India's NFIA is Positive** because Indian workers abroad (USA, Gulf) send remittances home → GNP > GDP for India.
> **USA's NFIA is Negative** as more foreign nationals work in USA → GDP > GNP for USA.

## 1.7 Measuring Price Changes: Real vs Nominal GDP

### The Problem with Nominal GDP

**Nominal GDP** uses **current prices** → If prices rise but quantity stays same, Nominal GDP rises — which is misleading!

> **Example**: If rice price doubles but quantity produced is same → Nominal GDP doubles, but no real growth happened.

---

### Real vs Nominal GDP

| | **Nominal GDP** | **Real GDP** |
|---|---|---|
| **Price Used** | Current Year Prices | Base Year Prices (constant prices) |
| **Reflects** | Both volume AND price changes | Only volume/quantity changes |
| **Used For** | Current economic snapshot | Comparing growth across years |
| **Problem** | Overestimates growth during inflation | Best measure of actual output growth |

**Formula:**
```
Real GDP = (Nominal GDP / GDP Deflator) × 100
```

---

### GDP Deflator

**Definition**: A price index that measures the change in prices of **all** goods and services included in GDP.

```
GDP Deflator = (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) × 100
```

- If GDP Deflator = 120 → Prices have risen by 20% since base year
- GDP Deflator covers ALL goods in the economy (broader than CPI)

---

### Consumer Price Index (CPI)

**Definition**: Measures the average change in prices paid by **consumers** for a fixed basket of goods and services.

```
CPI = (Cost of basket in current year / Cost of basket in base year) × 100
```

**Inflation Rate:**
```
Inflation Rate = [(CPI this year − CPI last year) / CPI last year] × 100
```

---

### CPI vs GDP Deflator — Key Differences

| Feature | **CPI** | **GDP Deflator** |
|---|---|---|
| **Coverage** | Fixed basket of consumer goods | All goods & services in GDP |
| **Imports** | Included (consumers buy imported goods) | Excluded (not domestically produced) |
| **Investment Goods** | Not included | Included |
| **Base** | Fixed basket (Laspeyres Index) | Changes with production (Paasche Index) |
| **Purpose** | Cost of living, wage indexation | Broader measure of economy-wide inflation |

---

### Wholesale Price Index (WPI)

- Measures price changes at the **wholesale level** (before retail)
- Used as an **early indicator** of inflationary pressures
- **India**: WPI is a key inflation measure (unlike US which uses PPI)

| Index | Measures | Used By |
|---|---|---|
| CPI | Consumer retail prices | RBI for inflation targeting in India |
| WPI | Wholesale prices | Government for pricing policy |
| GDP Deflator | Economy-wide price changes | National income calculations |

---

### Real GDP per Capita — Standard of Living

```
Real GDP per Capita = Real GDP / Population
```

- Best simple measure of **average standard of living**
- Still has limitations: ignores inequality, non-market activities, environmental costs

---

### GDP Growth Rate

```
GDP Growth Rate = [(Real GDP this year − Real GDP last year) / Real GDP last year] × 100
```

> **India's GDP Growth Rate**: ~6-7% (one of the fastest growing major economies)

## 1.8 Summary

### 🔑 Key Takeaways — Chapter 1

| Topic | What to Remember |
|---|---|
| **Macroeconomics** | Studies economy as a whole — GDP, unemployment, inflation, interest rates |
| **Circular Flow** | Income = Output = Expenditure in equilibrium |
| **Fiscal Policy** | Govt uses G & T to stabilize economy. Expansionary (recession) vs Contractionary (inflation) |
| **Budget** | Surplus (T>G), Deficit (T<G), Balanced (T=G) |
| **Business Cycle** | Expansion → Peak → Contraction → Trough → Recovery |
| **GDP Approaches** | Expenditure (C+I+G+NX), Income, Output (Value Added) |
| **Real vs Nominal** | Real GDP = Adjusted for prices. Always use Real GDP for comparison |
| **GDP Deflator** | Nominal GDP / Real GDP × 100. Broader than CPI |
| **CPI** | Fixed basket. Used for cost of living and inflation targeting |

## 1. 📖 INTRODUCTION — KEY CONCEPTS

### Why Sample? Why Not Study Everyone?

> Imagine you want to know if Indians prefer Pepsi or Coke. There are **140 crore people** — you CAN'T ask all of them. So you pick a smaller, representative group — a **Sample**.

### Fundamental Terms:

| Term | Definition | Example |
|------|------------|---------|
| **Population (Universe)** | The ENTIRE group of interest to the researcher | All MBA students in India |
| **Sample** | A SUBSET of the population selected for study | 500 MBA students from 10 colleges |
| **Sampling Unit** | The individual element being sampled | One MBA student |
| **Sampling Frame** | The list from which sample is drawn | Enrollment list of all MBA colleges |
| **Sample Size** | Number of units in the sample | 500 |
| **Census** | Studying the ENTIRE population | Govt's decennial census |
| **Parameter** | Characteristic of the POPULATION | True average income of all Indians |
| **Statistic** | Characteristic of the SAMPLE | Average income of 500 sampled Indians |

### Census vs Sample:

| Feature | Census | Sample |
|---------|--------|--------|
| Coverage | Entire population | Subset |
| Cost | Very high | Lower |
| Time | Very long | Shorter |
| Accuracy | 100% complete | Depends on design |
| Feasibility | Often impractical | Practical |
| Used when | Population is small | Population is large |

> [!TIP]
> **Mnemonic: "PUFF-SS"** — Key sampling terms
> - **P**opulation
> - **U**nit (sampling unit)
> - **F**rame (sampling frame)
> - **F**inish (sample size)
> - **S**ample
> - **S**tatistic

## 2. 🪜 STEPS IN SAMPLE DESIGN

> [!IMPORTANT]
> This is a **guaranteed exam question** — know all steps in order!

> **Mnemonic: "DUSTY-SAR"**
> - **D**efine the target population
> - **U**nit of sampling — identify sampling unit
> - **S**ampling frame — specify it
> - **T**echnique — select sampling technique
> - **Y**ield — determine sample size
> - **S**elect the sample
> - **A**dminister the sample (execute fieldwork)
> - **R**eview / Evaluate for non-response issues

---

### Step 1: 🔵 Define the Target Population

- **Who** exactly are you studying?
- Must be defined in terms of:
  - **Element** — Who is the sampling unit? (individual, household, store)
  - **Extent** — What geographical area? (Mumbai, India, Global)
  - **Time** — What time period? (Current users? Past 3 months?)

**Example:**
> Research Problem: "What is the attitude of smartphone users toward 5G?"
> Target Population: "Adults aged 18-45 who own a smartphone and have used the internet in the past 1 month, residing in Tier-1 and Tier-2 Indian cities"

**Errors in Population Definition:**
- Too **broad**: "All Indian consumers" → impractical
- Too **narrow**: "Only iPhone users in Delhi" → not generalizable

---

### Step 2: 🔵 Identify the Sampling Unit

- The **basic unit** being sampled — who/what will you actually select?
- May or may not be the same as the element

**Examples:**
- For a household product study: **Sampling unit = Household** (not individual)
- For a B2B study: **Sampling unit = Company** (not individual employee)
- For a student attitude study: **Sampling unit = Student**

---

### Step 3: 🔵 Specify the Sampling Frame

> **Sampling Frame** = The actual **list or map** from which sample units will be selected

- Must **closely match** the target population
- Examples:
  - Voter registration list
  - Telephone directory
  - Customer database / CRM
  - College enrollment list
  - Mall visitor footfall list

**Sampling Frame Error:**
> When the frame doesn't perfectly match the population
- **Undercoverage** — Some population members NOT in frame (e.g., people without phones not in phone directory)
- **Overcoverage** — Frame includes people NOT in population (e.g., deceased people still on voter rolls)

---

### Step 4: 🔵 Select Sampling Technique

**The Big Decision:**
```
Probability Sampling          OR          Non-Probability Sampling
(Random — everyone has              (Non-random — not everyone
a known chance of selection)         has equal chance)
```

*(Detailed explanation in Section 5 below)*

**Factors influencing technique choice:**
- Research objectives (exploratory vs conclusive)
- Availability of sampling frame
- Time and budget constraints
- Required precision and generalizability
- Nature of the population

---

### Step 5: 🔵 Determine Sample Size

**How big should the sample be?**

Key factors that influence sample size:

> **Mnemonic: "PPVC"**
> - **P**opulation size (larger pop = larger sample needed... up to a point)
> - **P**recision required (margin of error — smaller error = larger sample)
> - **V**ariance/Variability in population (more diverse = larger sample)
> - **C**onfidence level required (95% vs 99% confidence)

**General Rules of Thumb:**
| Study Type | Typical Sample Size |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Exploratory (Qualitative) | 6–30 |
| Descriptive Survey | 200–500 |
| National Consumer Survey | 1,000–2,000 |
| Focus Group (per group) | 6–12 |

**The Diminishing Returns Principle:**
> Beyond a certain point, doubling sample size gives only marginal improvement in precision. A well-designed sample of 400 can be more accurate than a poorly designed sample of 4,000!

---

### Step 6: 🔵 Select the Actual Sample

- Apply the chosen technique to the sampling frame
- Generate random numbers, systematically pick units
- Handle **substitutions** for non-responses (have replacement list ready)

---

### Step 7: 🔵 Administer / Execute Fieldwork

- Go out and collect data from the selected sample
- Monitor for: Non-response, refusals, incomplete forms
- **Response Rate** = (Completed responses / Total selected) × 100
- A good response rate: ≥60% for mail surveys, ≥80% for personal

---

### Step 8: 🔵 Evaluate for Non-Response Issues

- **Non-response bias:** People who don't respond may differ systematically from those who do
- Solutions:
  - Follow-up calls/emails
  - Incentives for participation
  - Compare early vs late responders (proxy for non-responders)

## 3. ✅ CHARACTERISTICS OF A GOOD SAMPLE DESIGN

> **Mnemonic: "GRAPES"**
> - **G**oal-oriented — Appropriate for research objectives
> - **R**epresentative — Mirrors the target population
> - **A**ccurate — Low systematic (non-random) error
> - **P**recise — Low random error; consistent results
> - **E**fficient — Best results at minimum cost and time
> - **S**ystematic — Follows a structured, documented procedure

---

### Detailed Explanation:

| Characteristic | Meaning | Example |
|---------------|---------|---------|
| **Representative** | Sample should reflect the population's key characteristics | If 60% of population is female, sample should be ~60% female |
| **Adequate Size** | Large enough to support valid conclusions | Min 200 for descriptive research |
| **Unbiased** | Every unit in population has a fair chance of selection | Random selection process |
| **Accurate** | Low systematic error / bias | Proper sampling frame used |
| **Precise** | Consistent results if study repeated | Low standard error |
| **Efficient** | Best precision at minimum cost | Stratified sampling vs pure random |
| **Feasible** | Can actually be executed within constraints | Frame exists, respondents accessible |
| **Measurable** | Sampling error can be calculated | Probability sampling enables this |

## 4. 🧭 SELECTING A SAMPLING PROCEDURE

**Two major categories:**

```
Sampling Procedures
│
├── PROBABILITY SAMPLING
│   (Everyone has a KNOWN, NON-ZERO chance of selection)
│   ├── 1. Simple Random Sampling (SRS)
│   ├── 2. Systematic Sampling
│   ├── 3. Stratified Sampling
│   ├── 4. Cluster Sampling
│   └── 5. Multi-Stage Sampling
│
└── NON-PROBABILITY SAMPLING

    ├── 1. Convenience Sampling
    ├── 2. Judgmental / Purposive Sampling
    ├── 3. Quota Sampling
    ├── 4. Snowball Sampling
    └── 5. Panel Sampling
```

## 5. 📊 SAMPLING DESIGN METHODS — DEEP DIVE

---

## A. ✅ PROBABILITY SAMPLING METHODS

### 🔵 1. Simple Random Sampling (SRS)

> **Definition:** Every unit in the population has an **equal and independent chance** of being selected.

**Methods:**
- **Lottery Method** — Names/numbers on chits drawn randomly
- **Random Number Table** — Use published tables of random numbers
- **Computer-generated random numbers**

**Example:**
> From a class of 200 students, select 40 using random numbers. Every student has 40/200 = **20% probability** of selection.

**Advantages:**
- Unbiased — no researcher judgment
- Easy to understand
- Sampling error can be calculated

**Disadvantages:**
- Needs **complete sampling frame** (list of all elements)
- Impractical for **large, dispersed populations**
- May miss small sub-groups (minorities underrepresented)

---

### 🔵 2. Systematic Sampling (Nth Element Sampling)

> **Definition:** Select **every Nth element** from a list after a random start.

**How it works:**
```
Sampling Interval (k) = Population Size (N) / Sample Size (n)

Random start: Pick any number between 1 and k
Then select: Start, Start+k, Start+2k, Start+3k...
```

**Example:**
> Population = 1,000; Sample = 100; k = 1000/100 = **10**
> Random start = 7 → Select: 7, 17, 27, 37, 47... (every 10th)

**Advantages:**
- Simpler to execute than SRS
- No need for complete numbered list
- Spreads sample evenly across population

**Disadvantages:**
- **Periodicity problem** — If list has a hidden pattern every kth element, bias occurs
- Example: If a factory list alternates supervisor-worker-supervisor-worker and k=2, you may select only supervisors!

---

### 🔵 3. Stratified Sampling

> **Definition:** Population is divided into **mutually exclusive, homogeneous subgroups (strata)**, then a **random sample is drawn from each stratum**.

**Key Logic:** Divide and conquer — make sub-groups similar within, different between.

**Types:**

| Type | How Sample is Allocated | When to Use |
|------|------------------------|-------------|
| **Proportionate** | Sample from each stratum proportional to stratum size | When strata sizes are important |
| **Disproportionate** | More sample from smaller/more variable strata | When some strata are rare or critical |

**Example:**
> Study attitude of MBA students. Strata = Year 1 (400 students) + Year 2 (600 students)
>
> Proportionate (n=100): Year 1 = 40 samples, Year 2 = 60 samples
> Disproportionate: Year 1 = 50, Year 2 = 50 (equal representation)

**Stratification Variables:** Gender, Age group, Income class, Geography, Product usage

**Advantages:**
- **More representative** than SRS — ensures all subgroups included
- **More precise** — reduces variability within strata
- Can study each stratum **separately**

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires **knowledge of population characteristics** to form strata
- More **complex** and expensive
- If strata are wrong, accuracy suffers

---

### 🔵 4. Cluster Sampling

> **Definition:** Population is divided into **clusters** (naturally existing groups), a **random sample of clusters is selected**, and then **ALL or some elements in selected clusters are studied**.

**Key Difference from Stratified:**
| | Stratified | Cluster |
|--|-----------|---------|
| Sub-groups | Homogeneous WITHIN | Heterogeneous WITHIN |
| Selection | Sample from ALL groups | Sample SOME groups |
| Logic | All strata → small sample from each | Few clusters → study all/most in them |

**Example:**
> To survey customers in Mumbai, divide city into 50 wards (clusters). Randomly select 10 wards. Survey ALL customers in those 10 wards.

**Types:**
- **Single-Stage:** Select clusters → study entire cluster
- **Two-Stage:** Select clusters → then randomly sample within clusters
- **Area Sampling:** Clusters are geographic areas (most common type)

**Advantages:**
- **Cost-effective** for geographically dispersed populations
- No need for complete population list (only cluster list needed)
- Easy for **field execution**

**Disadvantages:**
- **Less precise** than SRS — high sampling error if clusters are similar to each other
- **Cluster effect** — if cluster members are very alike, little new info per cluster

---

### 🔵 5. Multi-Stage Sampling

> **Definition:** Sampling is done in **multiple stages**, with different sampling techniques possibly applied at each stage.

**How it works (example — national survey):**
```
Stage 1: Select States randomly (cluster sampling)
         ↓
Stage 2: Select Districts from chosen States (cluster sampling)
         ↓
Stage 3: Select Households from chosen Districts (systematic sampling)
         ↓
Stage 4: Select individual from household (SRS)
```

**Real-world Use:**
- NSSO (National Sample Survey) uses multi-stage sampling
- All large-scale national surveys use this approach

**Advantages:**
- Practical for **massive national studies**
- Can use **different methods** at different stages
- No need for full national list — only progressively smaller lists

**Disadvantages:**
- **Cumulative sampling error** — errors multiply across stages
- Complex to design and execute

## B. ❌ NON-PROBABILITY SAMPLING METHODS

### 🟡 1. Convenience Sampling (Accidental Sampling)

> **Definition:** Researcher selects sample units that are **most easily accessible** — whoever is available and willing.

**Examples:**
- Interviewing shoppers at a mall entrance
- Asking friends/colleagues to fill a survey
- Online survey open to anyone who clicks the link
- Street intercept interviews

**Advantages:**
- **Easiest and cheapest** to execute
- Quick — for pilot testing or exploratory research
- No sampling frame needed

**Disadvantages:**
- **Most biased** — sample may not represent population at all
- Cannot calculate sampling error
- Results **cannot be generalized**

**When to use:** Only for preliminary/exploratory research or pre-testing

---

### 🟡 2. Judgmental / Purposive Sampling

> **Definition:** Researcher uses **personal judgment** to select units that are believed to be **most representative or most appropriate** for the study.

**Examples:**
- Selecting "typical" cities for a national survey (Delhi, Mumbai, Chennai as representatives)
- Choosing expert respondents for a study (e.g., only senior marketing managers)
- Selecting "best practice" companies for case study

**Advantages:**
- Uses researcher expertise for **targeted selection**
- Useful for **expert opinion studies**
- Good for **hypothesis generation**

**Disadvantages:**
- **Researcher bias** — "typical" is subjective
- Cannot measure or calculate sampling error
- **Not generalizable**

**When to use:** Expert surveys, test market selection, pilot studies

---

### 🟡 3. Quota Sampling

> **Definition:** Population is divided into **sub-groups (quotas)** based on known characteristics, and researchers select a **pre-specified number** from each sub-group using non-random methods.

**Looks like Stratified, but key difference:**
| | Stratified | Quota |
|--|-----------|-------|
| Selection within group | **Random** | **Non-random** (interviewer chooses) |
| Type | Probability | Non-probability |
| Generalizability | Yes | No |

**Example:**
> Survey 200 people: 100 Male, 100 Female; 60 under 30 yrs, 140 above 30 yrs.
> Interviewer fills these quotas by approaching anyone who fits the category.

**Advantages:**
- **Faster and cheaper** than stratified sampling
- Ensures **representation** of key sub-groups
- No need for sampling frame

**Disadvantages:**
- Selection within quota is **non-random** → biased
- Interviewers may pick **easy-to-reach** people within quota (convenience bias)
- Cannot calculate sampling error

**When to use:** Commercial market research, public opinion polls, ad hoc surveys

---

### 🟡 4. Snowball Sampling (Chain Referral Sampling)

> **Definition:** Initial respondents are selected, and they **refer/recommend other respondents** who meet the criteria, growing the sample like a rolling snowball.

**How it grows:**
```
Researcher finds → Respondent 1
Respondent 1 refers → Respondents 2, 3, 4
Respondent 2 refers → Respondents 5, 6
Respondent 4 refers → Respondents 7, 8, 9
... and so on (until target size reached)
```

**When to use:**
- **Hard-to-find populations** — drug users, rare disease patients, undocumented immigrants, LGBTQ+ communities in conservative areas
- Networks of professionals (e.g., CXOs, elite athletes)
- Niche hobby groups

**Advantages:**
- Only way to reach **hidden or rare populations**
- Cost-effective — respondents do the recruiting
- Good for **network studies**

**Disadvantages:**
- **Network bias** — people refer similar people (limited diversity)
- Cannot generalize — highly self-selected
- Researcher **loses control** over sample composition

---

### 🟡 5. Panel Sampling

> **Definition:** A **pre-recruited, fixed group** of respondents who agree to provide data **repeatedly over time**.

**Types:**
- **Consumer Panel** — Households track purchases (e.g., Nielsen Homescan)
- **Retail Panel** — Stores report sales data (e.g., Nielsen Retail Audit)
- **Online Panel** — Pre-registered participants for multiple surveys

**Advantages:**
- **Longitudinal data** — shows changes over time
- Panel members are **pre-qualified** — faster execution
- Cost-effective for tracking studies

**Disadvantages:**
- **Panel conditioning** — repeated measurement changes behaviour
- **Panel mortality** — members drop out over time (attrition)
- **Non-representativeness** — willing panel members may differ from general population

## 📊 PROBABILITY vs NON-PROBABILITY — MASTER COMPARISON TABLE

| Feature | Probability | Non-Probability |
|---------|------------|----------------|
| **Selection** | Random — known probability | Non-random — researcher chooses |
| **Sampling Error** | Can be calculated | Cannot be calculated |
| **Generalizability** | High | Low |
| **Cost** | Higher | Lower |
| **Time** | More | Less |
| **Representativeness** | High | Uncertain |
| **Sampling Frame** | Required | Not required |
| **Statistical Analysis** | Full inferential stats | Limited |
| **Best For** | Conclusive, descriptive research | Exploratory, qualitative research |
| **Examples** | SRS, Stratified, Cluster | Convenience, Quota, Snowball |

---

## 📊 ALL PROBABILITY METHODS — QUICK COMPARISON

| Method | Key Feature | Best For | Main Risk |
|--------|------------|----------|-----------|
| **SRS** | Equal probability, lottery | Small, accessible populations | Needs complete frame |
| **Systematic** | Every Nth element | Long lists, ordered populations | Periodicity bias |
| **Stratified** | Divide into strata, random from each | Heterogeneous populations | Need population data |
| **Cluster** | Random sample of clusters | Large, dispersed populations | High sampling error |
| **Multi-Stage** | Multiple rounds of sampling | National-level surveys | Cumulative error |

## 📊 ALL NON-PROBABILITY METHODS — QUICK COMPARISON

| Method | Key Feature | Best For | Main Risk |
|--------|------------|----------|-----------|
| **Convenience** | Whoever is available | Pilot testing, exploratory | Most biased |
| **Judgmental** | Researcher picks "best" units | Expert studies, typical cases | Researcher bias |
| **Quota** | Fill pre-set sub-group counts | Commercial surveys | Non-random within quota |
| **Snowball** | Referral chains | Hidden/rare populations | Network homogeneity |
| **Panel** | Fixed group over time | Tracking studies | Panel conditioning, attrition |

## ⚡ QUICK REVISION FLASHCARDS

**Q: What is a Sampling Frame? Give example.**
A: The list from which the sample is actually drawn. Example: Voter list, college enrollment list, customer database

**Q: Systematic Sampling formula?**
A: Sampling Interval k = N/n. Start randomly, then pick every kth element

**Q: Key difference — Stratified vs Cluster?**
A: Stratified = homogeneous WITHIN groups, sample from ALL groups. Cluster = heterogeneous WITHIN clusters, select SOME clusters

**Q: When is Snowball sampling used?**
A: For hard-to-find/hidden populations — drug users, rare disease patients, niche communities

**Q: Quota vs Stratified sampling — main difference?**
A: Both divide population into sub-groups. Stratified = random selection within strata (probability). Quota = non-random selection within quotas (non-probability)

**Q: What is Panel Conditioning?**
A: When repeated measurement changes the respondent's natural behaviour — a limitation of panel sampling

**Q: Which sampling method is most accurate?**
A: Stratified Random Sampling — reduces within-group variance, most efficient probability method

**Q: What is Periodicity Problem in Systematic Sampling?**
A: If the list has a hidden recurring pattern that aligns with the sampling interval k, a biased sample results

**Q: GRAPES — characteristics of good sample?**
A: Goal-oriented, Representative, Accurate, Precise, Efficient, Systematic

**Q: Which sampling requires NO sampling frame?**
A: Non-probability methods (Convenience, Quota, Snowball) — do not need a list

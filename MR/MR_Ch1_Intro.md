## 🟢 BEGINNER LEVEL — "What is this about?"

If a company launches a new Mango juice and nobody buys it, they lose ₹50 Crores. 
Instead of guessing what customers want and risking ₹50 Crores, smart companies spend ₹1 Crore on **Marketing Research** *before* they launch the product. 

They ask people: "Do you like mango juice? Would you pay ₹50 for it? Do you prefer a glass bottle or a plastic bottle?" 
Marketing Research is simply the systematic process of gathering data from the market to help managers make less risky, highly profitable decisions.

## 🟡 INTERMEDIATE LEVEL — "Key Concepts & Frameworks"

### 1. Market Research vs Marketing Research
- **Market Research (Narrow):** Studying a specific market. (e.g., "How many smartphones are sold in India every year?").
- **Marketing Research (Broad):** Studying the entire marketing process. (e.g., "Will this new TV advertisement make people buy more smartphones?").

### 2. The 6-Step Research Process
1. **Define the Problem:** The most critical step. (Are sales dropping because of high price, or bad taste?).
2. **Develop the Research Plan:** How will we get the data?
3. **Collect Data:** Go out and talk to people.
4. **Analyze Data:** Use statistics to find patterns.
5. **Present Findings:** Write a report for the CEO.
6. **Make the Decision:** Launch the product, or kill it.

### 3. Three Types of Research Designs
- **Exploratory:** Used when the problem is vague. (e.g., "Why are sales dropping?"). You do Focus Groups.
- **Descriptive:** Used to answer Who, What, Where, When. (e.g., "What percentage of our buyers are teenagers?"). You do large Surveys.
- **Causal (Experimental):** Used to prove Cause-and-Effect. (e.g., "If I lower the price by ₹10, will sales go up by 20%?"). You run an Experiment.

## 🔴 ADVANCED / EXAM LEVEL — "How to write it in the exam?"

### Q. Define Marketing Research. Explain the different types of research designs (Exploratory, Descriptive, and Causal) with suitable examples. (15 Marks)

**1. Definition of Marketing Research:**
According to the American Marketing Association (AMA), Marketing Research is the function that links the consumer, customer, and public to the marketer through information. This information is systematically gathered, analyzed, and interpreted to identify marketing opportunities and problems, generate marketing actions, and monitor performance. It reduces managerial uncertainty.

**2. Classification of Research Designs:**
Research design is the master plan specifying the methods and procedures for collecting and analyzing the needed information. It is broadly classified into three categories based on the fundamental objective of the research.

**A. Exploratory Research:**
*   **Objective:** To discover ideas, gain insights, and formulate hypotheses when the problem is highly unstructured or vaguely defined.
*   **Characteristics:** Highly flexible, unstructured, and qualitative. Small, non-representative samples are used. It does not provide conclusive answers but points the researcher in the right direction.
*   **Methods:** Focus Group Discussions (FGDs), In-Depth Interviews (IDIs), Expert surveys, Secondary data analysis.
*   **Example:** Zomato notices a sudden drop in app usage among college students. They conduct Focus Groups (Exploratory) to find out *why*. (They might discover students think the delivery fees are too high).

**B. Descriptive Research:**
*   **Objective:** To describe market characteristics, consumer profiles, or market functions. It answers the "Who, What, When, Where, and How" (but not 'Why').
*   **Characteristics:** Highly structured, rigid, and quantitative. Requires large, representative samples. It assumes the researcher already has a clear understanding of the problem.
*   **Methods:** Cross-sectional surveys (questionnaires), Longitudinal panels, Observational studies.
*   **Example:** After the focus group, Zomato runs a national descriptive survey of 10,000 students to quantify exactly what percentage of users consider delivery fees the primary barrier to ordering.

**C. Causal (Experimental) Research:**
*   **Objective:** To obtain conclusive evidence of cause-and-effect relationships between variables. 
*   **Characteristics:** Highly structured. Requires the manipulation of one or more independent variables (like price) to measure the effect on a dependent variable (like sales), while tightly controlling all extraneous variables.
*   **Methods:** Laboratory experiments, Field experiments (A/B Testing).
*   **Example:** Zomato runs a causal experiment (A/B test). They show free delivery to 50% of users, and a ₹20 fee to the other 50%. They measure the exact statistical difference in order volume to prove that cutting fees *causes* an increase in revenue.

**Conclusion:**
A robust marketing research project often uses all three designs sequentially: starting with exploratory to define the problem, moving to descriptive to quantify it, and finishing with causal to test the ultimate marketing solution.

## 1. 📖 INTRODUCTION

- Data is the **backbone** of Marketing Research
- Without data → No analysis → No insights → No decisions
- Researchers must decide: **What data to collect? From where? How?**
- Two fundamental types: **Primary Data** and **Secondary Data**

### The Data Collection Decision Tree:
```
Research Problem Identified
        ↓
Is relevant Secondary Data available?
   YES ↓                    ↓ NO
Use Secondary Data     Collect Primary Data
(cheaper, faster)      (expensive, time-consuming but specific)
```

> [!TIP]
> **Golden Rule:** Always check Secondary Data first. Only collect Primary Data if Secondary Data is unavailable, outdated, or insufficient.

## 2. 🔄 PRIMARY DATA vs SECONDARY DATA

### 📌 PRIMARY DATA

> **Definition:** Data collected **freshly and for the first time**, specifically for the current research problem.

- Also called **Field Data**
- Collected directly from the **source (respondents)**
- Tailored exactly to the researcher's need
- **More relevant** but **more expensive and time-consuming**

**Methods of Primary Data Collection:**
1. Survey / Questionnaire
2. Observation
3. Experiment (Test Markets)
4. Interview (Personal, Telephone, Online)
5. Focus Group Discussion (FGD)
6. Projective Techniques
7. Panel Research

---

### 📌 SECONDARY DATA

> **Definition:** Data that has **already been collected** by someone else for some **other purpose**, but is being used by the current researcher.

- Also called **Desk Data** (collected at a desk, not in the field)
- **Cheaper and faster** to obtain
- May not perfectly fit the research problem
- May be **outdated or incomplete**

---

### 📊 PRIMARY vs SECONDARY — Master Comparison Table

| Feature | Primary Data | Secondary Data |
|---------|-------------|----------------|
| **Source** | Collected directly from respondents | Already available from other sources |
| **Purpose** | Specific to current research | Collected for some other purpose |
| **Cost** | High | Low |
| **Time** | Long | Short |
| **Relevance** | Very High | May be moderate |
| **Accuracy** | Researcher controls quality | Depends on original collector |
| **Freshness** | Current/Up-to-date | May be outdated |
| **Flexibility** | Highly flexible | Fixed format |
| **Volume** | Limited | Abundant |
| **Examples** | Surveys, FGD, Observation | Census, Trade reports, Journals |

> [!TIP]
> **Mnemonic for Primary Data advantages: "FRAC"**
> - **F**resh & up-to-date
> - **R**elevant to specific problem
> - **A**ccurate (researcher controls)
> - **C**ustomized to needs
>
> **Mnemonic for Secondary Data advantages: "CLEF"**
> - **C**heap
> - **L**ess time-consuming
> - **E**asily accessible
> - **F**oundation for primary research

## 3. 🌐 SOURCES OF SECONDARY DATA

Secondary data comes from **two broad sources:**

```
Secondary Data Sources
├── A. Internal Sources (Within the organization)
└── B. External Sources (Outside the organization)
    ├── Published Sources
    ├── Syndicated/Computerized Sources
    └── Online Databases
```

---

### A. 🏢 INTERNAL SECONDARY DATA SOURCES

Data available **within the company itself:**

> **Mnemonic: "SAC-RIP"**
> - **S**ales records
> - **A**ccounting/Financial records
> - **C**ustomer databases (CRM)
> - **R**esearch reports (past MR studies)
> - **I**nventory records
> - **P**roduction reports

| Internal Source | What It Contains |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Sales Records** | Sales by region, product, salesperson, time period |
| **Customer Database/CRM** | Customer profiles, purchase history, complaints |
| **Financial Records** | Revenue, costs, profit margins by product |
| **Past Research Reports** | Previous marketing research findings |
| **Inventory Records** | Stock levels, turnover rates |
| **Employee Records** | Sales force performance data |

**Advantages of Internal Data:**
- Free of cost
- Easily accessible
- Company-specific and highly relevant

**Disadvantages:**
- May not answer all questions
- Data may be in incompatible formats
- May be incomplete or not maintained properly

---

### B. 📚 EXTERNAL SECONDARY DATA SOURCES

Data available **outside the organization:**

#### 1. Published Sources (Government & Non-Government)

**Government Sources:**
- **Census of India** — Population, demographics, household data
- **Economic Survey of India** — GDP, inflation, employment data
- **RBI Reports** — Banking, credit, monetary policy data
- **NSSO (National Sample Survey)** — Income, expenditure, consumer data
- **Ministry of Commerce** — Export-import data
- **CSO (Central Statistics Office)** — National accounts, industrial data

**Non-Government / Association Sources:**
- **ASSOCHAM, FICCI, CII** reports
- **Trade and Industry journals** (e.g., Business Today, Economic Times)
- **Academic Journals** (Journal of Marketing, MR journals)
- **Nielsen, IMRB, ORG** syndicated research reports

#### 2. Syndicated Data Sources
- Data collected by **commercial research agencies** and sold to clients
- Examples:
  - **Nielsen Retail Audit** — tracks retail sales of FMCG products
  - **BARC (Broadcast Audience Research Council)** — TV viewership data
  - **IRS (Indian Readership Survey)** — readership of publications
  - **TGI (Target Group Index)** — consumer lifestyle data

#### 3. Online / Computerized Databases
- **CMIE (Centre for Monitoring Indian Economy)**
- **Prowess Database** — Corporate financial data
- **EBSCO, JSTOR** — Academic research papers
- **Google Trends** — Search trends data
- **Statista** — Statistical data on industries

### ✅ Evaluating Secondary Data (TREAT)

Before using secondary data, always evaluate it:

> **Mnemonic: "TREAT"**
> - **T**imely — Is it current/up-to-date?
> - **R**elevant — Does it match the research problem?
> - **E**rror-free — Is it accurate and reliable?
> - **A**dequate — Is the sample size and coverage sufficient?
> - **T**ransparent — Is the methodology clearly stated?

## 4. 📖 CONDUCTING REVIEW OF LITERATURE (ROL)

> **Definition:** Review of Literature (ROL) is a **systematic examination of published and unpublished sources** related to a research topic to understand the current state of knowledge.

### Purpose of Literature Review:
1. Understand what has **already been studied**
2. Identify **gaps** in existing research
3. Build **theoretical framework** for the study
4. Refine and **sharpen the research problem**
5. Avoid **duplication** of research
6. Identify **appropriate methodology** used by others

---

### Steps in Conducting a Review of Literature:

> [!TIP]
> **Mnemonic: "SIDES"**
> - **S**elect the topic and define scope
> - **I**dentify sources (journals, books, databases)
> - **D**ownload/collect relevant materials
> - **E**valuate and critically analyze
> - **S**ynthesize and write the review

#### Step 1: Define Scope
- What topic? What time period? What geography?
- Avoid being too broad or too narrow

#### Step 2: Identify Sources
- Books, journals, theses, conference papers, government reports
- Online databases: Google Scholar, JSTOR, EBSCO, PubMed

#### Step 3: Search and Collect
- Use **keywords** to search
- Use Boolean operators: AND, OR, NOT
- Example: "consumer behaviour" AND "social media" NOT "B2B"

#### Step 4: Evaluate Sources
- **Credibility:** Is it a peer-reviewed journal?
- **Recency:** Is it recent enough? (Usually last 5-10 years)
- **Relevance:** Does it address your research topic?
- **Methodology:** Is the research method sound?

#### Step 5: Write the Review
- **Don't just summarize** — critically analyze and synthesize
- Organize by theme/concept, not author-by-author
- Show **connections between studies**
- Identify **agreements, disagreements, gaps**

### Structure of Literature Review:
```
Introduction → Thematic Discussion → Critical Analysis → Gaps Identified → Conclusion
```

### Common Mistakes in ROL:
- Just listing summaries (annotated bibliography ≠ literature review)
- Using outdated sources
- Missing major works in the field
- No critical analysis — just description
- Poor organization (random, not thematic)

## 5. 🔬 QUANTITATIVE vs QUALITATIVE RESEARCH

This is the **BIG distinction** in research methodology!

```
Research Approaches
├── Quantitative Research (Numbers & Statistics)
└── Qualitative Research (Words & Meanings)
```

---

### 📊 QUANTITATIVE RESEARCH

> **Definition:** Research that **collects numerical data** and uses **statistical methods** to analyze it and draw conclusions.

- Answers: **"How many? How much? How often?"**
- Large sample sizes
- Results are **generalizable** to the population
- Based on **positivist philosophy** (objective reality)

**Methods of Quantitative Research:**

> **Mnemonic: "SOPE"**
> - **S**urvey Research
> - **O**bservation (structured)
> - **P**anel Research
> - **E**xperiment

#### 1. 📋 Survey Research (Most Common!)
- Structured questionnaire administered to a sample
- Types:
  - **Personal Interview** — Face-to-face; high response, expensive
  - **Telephone Interview** — Quick, moderate cost; declining response
  - **Mail Survey** — Low cost, low response rate
  - **Online Survey** — Fastest, cheapest; growing rapidly (Google Forms, SurveyMonkey)
  - **CAPI** — Computer Assisted Personal Interviewing
  - **CATI** — Computer Assisted Telephone Interviewing

| Survey Type | Cost | Speed | Response Rate | Best For |
|-------------|------|-------|---------------|---------|
| Personal Interview | High | Slow | Very High | Complex questions |
| Telephone | Medium | Fast | Medium | Quick studies |
| Mail | Low | Very Slow | Low (5-15%) | Wide geographic reach |
| Online | Very Low | Very Fast | Varies | Urban/educated sample |

#### 2. 👀 Observation Research
- Observing respondents **without direct questioning**
- Types:
  - **Natural vs Contrived** — Real setting vs artificial
  - **Disguised vs Undisguised** — Respondent knows or doesn't know
  - **Human vs Mechanical** — Eye tracking, people counter
- **Example:** Mystery shopping, retail shelf observation, heat maps

#### 3. 📅 Panel Research
- Same group of respondents surveyed **repeatedly over time**
- Shows **changes** in attitude/behaviour
- Types:
  - **Consumer Panel** — Households track purchases
  - **Retail Panel** — Retailers track sales (e.g., Nielsen Retail Audit)

#### 4. ⚗️ Experimental Research
- Researcher **manipulates one variable** (Independent Variable) and measures effect on another (Dependent Variable)
- Controls for extraneous variables
- **Test marketing** is a key example
- Best for establishing **cause-and-effect**

---

### 🎭 QUALITATIVE RESEARCH

> **Definition:** Research that collects **non-numerical data** (words, images, observations) to understand **meanings, motivations, attitudes, and behaviour** in depth.

- Answers: **"Why? How? What does this mean?"**
- Small sample sizes (but deep exploration)
- Results are **NOT generalizable** — rich and detailed
- Based on **interpretivist philosophy** (subjective reality)

**Methods of Qualitative Research:**

> **Mnemonic: "FDP-C"**
> - **F**ocus Group Discussion
> - **D**epth Interview
> - **P**rojective Techniques
> - **C**ase Study / Ethnography

---

#### 1. 👥 Focus Group Discussion (FGD) *(Most Common Qualitative Method)*

> **Definition:** A moderated group discussion with **6-12 participants** to explore attitudes, perceptions, and motivations on a topic.

- Led by a **trained moderator**
- Recorded (audio/video) for analysis
- Duration: **1.5 to 2 hours**
- Venue: Usually a special facility with one-way mirror

**Process:**
```
Recruit participants → Prepare discussion guide → Conduct session → Record → Analyze → Report
```

**Advantages:**
- Group **synergy** — people build on each other's ideas
- Flexible and exploratory
- Captures **emotional responses**
- Relatively quick and inexpensive (vs depth interviews)

**Disadvantages:**
- **Dominant participant** can bias discussion
- Not generalizable (small sample)
- **Moderator bias** possible
- Social desirability — people may not speak freely in groups
- **Groupthink** — pressure to conform

**When to use FGD:**
- New product concept testing
- Advertising copy testing
- Understanding consumer language/vocabulary
- Exploratory research

---

#### 2. 🎤 Depth Interview (In-Depth Interview)

> **Definition:** A **one-on-one**, unstructured or semi-structured conversation with a respondent to deeply explore their attitudes, motivations, and experiences.

- Duration: **45 minutes to 2+ hours**
- No group pressure — respondent speaks freely
- Interviewer uses **probing questions** ("Why do you feel that way? Tell me more...")

**Types:**
- **Structured:** Fixed questions, fixed order
- **Semi-structured:** Key topics fixed, but flexible probing
- **Unstructured:** Free-flowing conversation

**Advantages:**
- Deep, rich insights
- No peer pressure — candid responses
- Sensitive topics can be explored (money, health, embarrassment)
- Captures **personal motivations**

**Disadvantages:**
- Very **time-consuming and expensive**
- Requires skilled interviewer
- Small sample — not generalizable
- Interviewer bias possible

---

#### 3. 🎭 Projective Techniques

> **Definition:** Indirect methods where respondents **project their feelings onto an ambiguous stimulus** (a picture, story, or word), revealing unconscious attitudes.

- Used when respondents are **unwilling or unable** to express true feelings directly
- Borrowed from clinical psychology

**Types of Projective Techniques:**

> **Mnemonic: "SWAP-C"**
> - **S**entence Completion
> - **W**ord Association
> - **A**pperception Tests (TAT)
> - **P**icture/Cartoon Completion
> - **C**haracter Personality Mapping

| Technique | How it Works | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Word Association** | Say first word that comes to mind | "When I say Maggi, you say ___?" |
| **Sentence Completion** | Complete an unfinished sentence | "People who drink Pepsi are ____" |
| **TAT (Thematic Apperception Test)** | Describe what's happening in a picture | Show image of person in supermarket |
| **Cartoon Completion** | Fill in speech bubble of cartoon characters | Two people talking, one bubble empty |
| **Role Playing** | Act out a scenario | "You are a brand manager of Coke..." |
| **Brand Personification** | If this brand were a person, describe them | "If Amazon were a person, they'd be..." |

**Advantages:** Reveals hidden/unconscious attitudes, breaks social desirability bias
**Disadvantages:** Subjective interpretation, requires expert analysis, not scalable

---

#### 4. 🔍 Ethnography / Observation (Qualitative)

> **Definition:** Researcher **immerses themselves** in the natural environment of the consumer to observe behaviour as it naturally occurs.

- Example: A researcher lives with a family for 2 weeks to understand shopping habits
- Used by companies like **P&G, IKEA** to understand how people use products at home
- **"Living the consumer's life"** approach

---

### 📊 QUANTITATIVE vs QUALITATIVE — Master Comparison Table

| Feature | Quantitative | Qualitative |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| **Goal** | Measure, quantify | Understand, explore |
| **Question answered** | How much? How many? | Why? How? What? |
| **Data type** | Numbers, statistics | Words, images, narratives |
| **Sample size** | Large (100s–1000s) | Small (6-30) |
| **Approach** | Structured, rigid | Flexible, exploratory |
| **Generalizability** | High | Low |
| **Depth of insight** | Breadth (wide) | Depth (deep) |
| **Analysis** | Statistical (SPSS, Excel) | Thematic, interpretive |
| **Methods** | Survey, Experiment, Panel | FGD, Depth Interview, Projective |
| **Time** | Moderate | More per respondent |
| **Cost** | Moderate (economies of scale) | High per respondent |
| **Used when** | Hypothesis testing, measuring | Problem exploration, hypothesis generation |
| **Philosophy** | Positivism (objective) | Interpretivism (subjective) |

---

### 🔗 How They Work Together (Mixed Methods)

```
Research Process Flow:
Qualitative (Explore) → Quantitative (Confirm)
     ↓                          ↓
FGD / Depth Interview    →   Survey / Experiment
"What are consumers'          "How many consumers
thinking about X?"             actually prefer X?"
(Generate Hypotheses)          (Test Hypotheses)
```

> Most professional marketing research uses **BOTH** — qualitative first to understand, then quantitative to measure.

## ⚡ QUICK REVISION FLASHCARDS

**Q: Define Primary Data**
A: Data collected freshly and for the first time specifically for the current research problem

**Q: Define Secondary Data**
A: Data already collected by someone else for some other purpose, now used by current researcher

**Q: Golden Rule for data collection?**
A: Always check secondary data FIRST; collect primary only if secondary is unavailable/insufficient

**Q: What is TREAT in secondary data evaluation?**
A: Timely, Relevant, Error-free, Adequate, Transparent

**Q: Ideal size of FGD group?**
A: 6 to 12 participants, moderated session of 1.5-2 hours

**Q: Qualitative vs Quantitative — which comes first?**
A: Qualitative (exploration) → Quantitative (confirmation/measurement)

**Q: What is Ethnography?**
A: Researcher immerses in consumer's natural environment to observe real behaviour

**Q: What is a Syndicated Data Source? Give example.**
A: Commercial data collected by research agency and sold to clients. E.g., Nielsen Retail Audit, BARC

**Q: What does TAT stand for?**
A: Thematic Apperception Test — projective technique using pictures/images

**Q: Difference between Panel and Survey?**
A: Panel = same respondents studied REPEATEDLY over time; Survey = one-time data collection

## 1. 📖 INTRODUCTION

### What is a Research Report?

> **Definition:** A Research Report is a **formal written document** that presents the findings, conclusions, and recommendations of a research study in a **systematic, organized, and objective manner** to the intended audience.

### The Report — Final Deliverable of MR:

```
Research Problem → Data Collection → Analysis
                                         ↓
                                  RESEARCH REPORT
                                         ↓
                              Management Decisions
```

> The research report is the **only tangible output** of the entire research process. No matter how brilliant the research — if the report is poorly written, the value is lost!

### Key Principle:
> **"Research is only as good as its communication."**
> A researcher must bridge the gap between **technical findings** and **managerial understanding**.

## 2. 💡 IMPORTANCE OF REPORT WRITING

> **Mnemonic: "DISCO-CF"**
> - **D**ocumentation — Permanent record of research
> - **I**nformation transfer — Communicates findings to decision makers
> - **S**cientific credibility — Shows research was rigorous
> - **C**ommunication tool — Connects researcher and management
> - **O**rganizational memory — Future reference for similar problems
> - **C**redibility of researcher — Well-written report = professional image
> - **F**oundation for action — Basis for management decisions

### Detailed Importance:

1. **Decision Support:** Report gives management the **evidence** to take action
2. **Documentation:** Creates a **permanent record** — future researchers can reference it
3. **Accountability:** Shows how research funds were spent and what was found
4. **Communication Bridge:** Translates **technical jargon** into **managerial language**
5. **Evaluation Tool:** Management can assess the quality and relevance of research
6. **Legal / Ethical Record:** In regulated industries, reports serve as legal evidence
7. **Organizational Learning:** Avoids repeating the same research — institutional memory
8. **Publication / Academic Contribution:** Adds to the body of knowledge

## 3. 📋 TYPES OF RESEARCH REPORTS

### Classification 1: By LENGTH/FORMAT

> **Mnemonic: "BTS"** — Brief, Technical, Summary

| Type | Length | Audience | Content |
|------|--------|---------|---------|
| **Brief/Popular Report** | Short (5-10 pages) | Non-technical management | Key findings, recommendations, minimal jargon |
| **Technical Report** | Long (50-200+ pages) | Research experts, academics | Full methodology, detailed analysis, all data |
| **Summary Report** | Medium (10-20 pages) | Mixed audience | Condensed findings with some methodology |

---

### Classification 2: By PURPOSE

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|------------|---------|
| **Informational Report** | Presents facts and data without interpretation | Sales data summary for the board |
| **Analytical Report** | Presents data + analysis + conclusions | Market feasibility study |
| **Recommendation Report** | Presents data + analysis + specific action recommendations | "We recommend entering Market X because..." |

---

### Classification 3: By TIME HORIZON

| Type | Description |
|------|------------|
| **Preliminary Report** | Interim findings shared mid-study (progress update) |
| **Final Report** | Complete research findings at project end |
| **Follow-Up Report** | Post-implementation evaluation of recommendations |

---

### Classification 4: By AUDIENCE

| Type | Audience | Characteristics |
|------|---------|----------------|
| **Written for Managers** | Non-technical executives | Simple language, conclusions first, visual-heavy |
| **Written for Researchers** | Technical readers | Full methodology, statistical details, academic citations |
| **Written for Public** | General audience | Very simple, visual, journalistic style |

---

### Popular Report vs Technical Report — Key Comparison:

| Feature | Popular/Brief Report | Technical Report |
|---------|---------------------|-----------------|
| **Length** | Short | Long |
| **Language** | Simple, non-technical | Technical, precise |
| **Methodology** | Briefly mentioned | Full detail |
| **Statistics** | Minimal / summarized | Full statistical output |
| **Audience** | Management, clients | Researchers, academics |
| **Format** | Flexible, visual | Rigid, formal |
| **Focus** | Recommendations | Methodology + findings |
| **Appendices** | Few or none | Extensive |

## 4. 🏗️ STRUCTURE OF A RESEARCH REPORT

> [!IMPORTANT]
> This is the MOST asked question in exams — "Explain the structure of a research report" — memorize ALL components!

> **Mnemonic: "PELT — MFCA — AAB"**
> *"PELT the MFCA into a AAB"*
>
> **Prefatory Section:**
> - **P**reface / Letter of Transmittal
> - **E**xecutive Summary
> - **L**ist of Tables/Figures
> - **T**able of Contents
>
> **Main Body:**
> - **M**ethodology
> - **F**indings / Results
> - **C**onclusions
> - **A**ctions / Recommendations
>
> **Back Matter:**
> - **A**ppendices
> - **A**nnotated Bibliography / References
> - **B**ibliography/Glossary

---

### COMPLETE REPORT STRUCTURE (Detailed):

```
RESEARCH REPORT
│
├── PART 1: PREFATORY SECTION (Before the main content)
│   ├── 1. Title Page
│   ├── 2. Letter of Transmittal / Authorization
│   ├── 3. Executive Summary (Abstract)
│   ├── 4. Table of Contents
│   └── 5. List of Tables, Figures, and Abbreviations
│
├── PART 2: MAIN BODY (Core content)
│   ├── Chapter 1: Introduction
│   │   ├── Background of the study
│   │   ├── Statement of the problem
│   │   ├── Objectives of the research
│   │   ├── Scope and limitations
│   │   └── Significance of the study
│   │
│   ├── Chapter 2: Review of Literature
│   │   ├── Theoretical framework
│   │   └── Previous studies reviewed
│   │
│   ├── Chapter 3: Research Methodology
│   │   ├── Research design used
│   │   ├── Data collection methods
│   │   ├── Sample design and size
│   │   ├── Questionnaire/instrument design
│   │   └── Statistical tools used
│   │
│   ├── Chapter 4: Data Analysis & Findings
│   │   ├── Data presentation (tables, charts)
│   │   ├── Statistical analysis results
│   │   └── Key findings discussed
│   │
│   └── Chapter 5: Conclusions & Recommendations
│       ├── Summary of major findings
│       ├── Conclusions drawn
│       ├── Recommendations for action
│       └── Suggestions for future research
│
└── PART 3: BACK MATTER (Supplementary)
    ├── References / Bibliography
    ├── Appendices
    │   ├── Questionnaire / Data instruments
    │   ├── Raw data tables
    │   ├── Statistical output (SPSS, etc.)
    │   └── Maps, photos, interview transcripts
    └── Glossary (optional)
```

---

### Each Section Explained:

#### 📌 1. Title Page
- Report title (specific, descriptive)
- Prepared by (researcher/firm name)
- Prepared for (client/management)
- Date of submission

#### 📌 2. Letter of Transmittal
- Formal covering letter submitting the report
- Brief: "Herewith we submit the report on..."
- Highlights key findings in 1-2 sentences
- Formal sign-off

#### 📌 3. Executive Summary *(MOST READ section!)*
- **Condensed version** of the ENTIRE report (1-3 pages)
- Written for **busy senior executives** who may not read the full report
- Covers: Problem, Method (brief), Key Findings, Conclusions, Recommendations
- Written LAST but placed FIRST
- **Rule:** Executive Summary should stand alone — readable independently

#### 📌 4. Table of Contents
- Lists all chapters, sections, and page numbers
- Allows readers to navigate directly to areas of interest

#### 📌 5. Introduction
- **Background:** What is the context? Why was this research needed?
- **Problem Statement:** What specific problem/question is addressed?
- **Objectives:** What are the research goals? (SMART objectives)
- **Scope:** What is included? What is excluded?
- **Limitations:** Known constraints on the study

#### 📌 6. Review of Literature
- What do existing studies say about this topic?
- Theoretical frameworks used
- Identifies gaps the current study fills

#### 📌 7. Research Methodology
- **Research design** (Exploratory/Descriptive/Causal)
- **Sample design** (Who was surveyed? How many? How selected?)
- **Data collection** (Primary/Secondary? Survey/FGD/Observation?)
- **Instrument design** (Questionnaire details)
- **Analysis tools** (SPSS, Excel; which tests used?)

#### 📌 8. Data Analysis & Findings
- Most **data-rich** section
- Uses tables, graphs, and charts to present data clearly
- Statistical test results reported with test statistic and significance
- Findings presented **objectively** — no interpretations yet

#### 📌 9. Conclusions & Recommendations
- **Conclusions:** What do the findings MEAN? (interpretation)
- **Recommendations:** What SHOULD management DO? (action)
- **Future Research:** What further research is needed?
- Directly addresses the original research objectives

#### 📌 10. Appendices
- Supporting material too detailed for main body
- Questionnaire copy, raw data, technical statistical output
- Reader can verify findings independently

#### 📌 11. Bibliography / References
- All sources cited in the report
- Standard format: APA, MLA, or Harvard referencing

## 5. ✍️ GUIDELINES FOR EFFECTIVE REPORT WRITING

> **Mnemonic: "COATS-BVF"**
> - **C**larity
> - **O**bjectivity
> - **A**ccuracy
> - **T**arget audience focus
> - **S**implicity
> - **B**revity / Conciseness
> - **V**isual aids (charts, tables)
> - **F**low and logical organization

---

### Detailed Guidelines:

#### 1. 🎯 Know Your Audience
- Write for **the reader**, not for yourself
- **Managers:** Less jargon, more action-orientation, conclusions upfront
- **Researchers:** Full methodology, statistical detail acceptable
- **Tip:** Ask — "Will my client understand this?"

#### 2. 💬 Clarity and Simplicity
- Use **short sentences** (average 15-20 words)
- Avoid **jargon** or explain technical terms
- One idea per paragraph
- Use **active voice:** "The survey found..." not "It was found by the survey..."
- Avoid **ambiguity** — be precise

#### 3. 🎯 Objectivity
- Report facts as they are — even if findings are negative for the client
- **Don't spin data** to suit a pre-determined conclusion
- Acknowledge **limitations** honestly
- Distinguish between facts (findings) and interpretations (conclusions)

#### 4. ✅ Accuracy
- All data, numbers, and statistics must be **correct**
- Double-check all figures, percentages, and totals
- Cite sources for all secondary data used
- Ensure charts/tables match text descriptions

#### 5. 📏 Brevity and Conciseness
- **"If in doubt, leave it out"**
- Don't repeat the same information in multiple sections
- Avoid unnecessary padding (writing more to seem thorough)
- Executive Summary must be truly brief — max 2-3 pages

#### 6. 📊 Effective Use of Visual Aids
- Use tables for **detailed comparisons**
- Use charts/graphs for **trends and proportions**
- Every visual must have a **title, source, and legend**
- Don't overload — one key insight per visual
- Refer to all visuals in the text ("As shown in Figure 3...")

#### 7. 🔄 Logical Flow and Organization
- Report should have a clear **beginning, middle, end**
- Use **headings and subheadings** for navigation
- Ideas should flow logically — each section builds on previous
- Use **transitions**: "Building on these findings...", "In contrast..."

#### 8. 📐 Consistent Formatting
- Consistent font, font size, heading styles
- **Number all pages** (except title page)
- **Number all tables and figures** (Table 1, Figure 2...)
- Consistent citation style throughout (APA or Harvard)

#### 9. 🔍 Proofreading and Editing
- Never submit first draft — edit at least once
- Check for: spelling errors, grammar, punctuation
- Ensure data in text matches data in tables
- Have a colleague read for clarity ("Would a layperson understand this?")

#### 10. 📌 Recommendations Must Be Actionable
- Don't just say "The company should improve customer service"
- Say: **"Introduce a 24/7 customer helpline and train 50 agents by Q3, targeting a 20% reduction in complaints"**
- Recommendations must be: **Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound (SMART)**

---

### Common Report Writing Mistakes to AVOID:

| ❌ Mistake | ✅ Better Approach |
|-----------|------------------|
| Starting with methodology | Start with Executive Summary or findings |
| Presenting raw data without interpretation | Always interpret what the data means |
| Using passive voice throughout | Use active voice for clarity |
| Missing recommendations | Always end with actionable steps |
| Tables without titles/sources | Every visual must be labelled |
| No executive summary | Always include for busy readers |
| Copy-pasting SPSS output | Present only relevant statistics in clean tables |
| Vague conclusions | Be specific — what exactly does this mean for the client? |

## 6. 🌐 ORAL PRESENTATION OF RESEARCH REPORT

> Many research projects end with BOTH a written report AND an oral presentation to management.

### Tips for Oral Presentation:

> **Mnemonic: "PACE-VQ"**
> - **P**repare — Know your content thoroughly
> - **A**udience — Tailor to management level
> - **C**oncise — 20-30 minutes max
> - **E**ngaging — Use visuals, stories, real examples
> - **V**isuals — PowerPoint slides (max 20, clean design)
> - **Q**uestions — Anticipate and prepare for Q&A

### Structure of Oral Presentation:
1. **Opening** — Hook audience, state the problem and objective (2 min)
2. **Methodology** — Very brief, just enough for credibility (2 min)
3. **Key Findings** — 3-5 main findings, visual-heavy (15 min)
4. **Conclusions** — What it all means (3 min)
5. **Recommendations** — What to do about it (5 min)
6. **Q&A** — Open floor for questions (5-10 min)

## ⚡ QUICK REVISION FLASHCARDS

**Q: What is the most-read section of a research report?**
A: **Executive Summary** — written LAST but placed FIRST; stands alone; max 2-3 pages

**Q: What is a Letter of Transmittal?**
A: Formal covering letter that officially submits the report to the client/management

**Q: Popular Report vs Technical Report — key difference?**
A: Popular = short, simple language, for managers, conclusions-focused. Technical = long, detailed methodology, for researchers/academics

**Q: What should Recommendations be?**
A: **SMART** — Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound

**Q: Where does the Questionnaire go in a report?**
A: In the **Appendices** — not in the main body

**Q: What is the difference between Findings and Conclusions?**
A: Findings = WHAT the data shows (objective). Conclusions = WHAT IT MEANS (interpretation)

**Q: Name the three main parts of a research report.**
A: Prefatory Section → Main Body → Back Matter (Appendices/References)

**Q: What is the golden rule of Executive Summary?**
A: Must be readable **independently** — a reader who reads ONLY the executive summary should understand the full research story

**Q: Why must non-parametric tests be known for the report?**
A: Because methodology chapter must specify which statistical tests were used and why

**Q: Active vs Passive voice in reports?**
A: Always prefer **Active voice** — "The study found that..." NOT "It was found by the study that..."

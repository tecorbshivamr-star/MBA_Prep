## 📗 LESSON 1: INTRODUCTION TO MR

| Topic | Definition | Real-Life Example |
|-------|-----------|------------------|
| **Marketing Research** | Systematic & objective process of gathering, recording & analyzing data to solve marketing problems | HUL conducts MR before launching new Surf Excel variant to know if consumers want a "fabric softener" feature |
| **Market Research** | Study of a specific market — its size, trends & competitors | Reliance Jio studying the size of the prepaid mobile market in rural India |
| **MR vs Market Research** | MR is BROADER (covers all 4Ps); Market Research covers only the market | MR covers product, price, promotion for Jio; Market Research only covers "how big is telecom market" |
| **Research Problem Formulation** | Converting a vague management issue into a specific, researchable question | Management: "Sales fell 20%" → Research: "Why are Delhi consumers switching from Parle-G to Sunfeast in 2024?" |
| **Exploratory Research** | Flexible research to discover ideas when problem is vague | Swiggy conducts FGDs to understand WHY users uninstall the app |
| **Descriptive Research** | Structured research to describe market characteristics (who, what, how many) | Blinkit surveys 500 users: "What % of Mumbai households order groceries online?" |
| **Causal Research** | Experiments to establish cause-and-effect | Myntra A/B tests: Does showing "Only 2 left!" urgency message increase purchase rate? |
| **Research Design** | Blueprint/master plan for conducting research | Before surveying 1,000 people, Amul decides: what to ask, who, which city, which month |

## 📗 LESSON 2: SCALING TECHNIQUES

| Topic | Definition | Real-Life Example |
|-------|-----------|------------------|
| **Nominal Scale** | Labels/names only — numbers have no math meaning | Milk brands: Amul=1, Mother Dairy=2, Nandini=3 (2 is NOT greater than 1!) |
| **Ordinal Scale** | Ranks objects in order but NOT the distance between them | Zomato restaurant stars: 1★ < 2★ < 3★ — but gap between 1 and 2 is unknown |
| **Interval Scale** | Equal intervals but NO true zero | Customer satisfaction on 1-5 scale — "0" doesn't mean zero satisfaction |
| **Ratio Scale** | Equal intervals WITH a true zero — all math operations valid | Monthly income: ₹0 = no income; ₹50K is truly twice ₹25K |
| **Likert Scale** | 5 or 7-point agreement scale (SD→D→N→A→SA) | "Swiggy delivers food on time." ○SD ○D ○N ○A ○SA — in every brand satisfaction survey |
| **Semantic Differential** | Bipolar adjective pairs on 7-point scale to measure brand image | Flipkart: Fast←7-6-5-4-3-2-1→Slow; Cheap←→Expensive; Reliable←→Unreliable |
| **Stapel Scale** | Single adjective rated on -5 to +5 (no opposite adjective) | Rate "Ola" on "RELIABLE": +5 to -5 (used in quick phone surveys) |
| **Paired Comparison** | Compare two objects at a time and pick preferred one | "Which do you prefer — Domino's or Pizza Hut?" (for all brand pairs) |
| **Constant Sum Scale** | Distribute 100 points among attributes showing relative importance | "Divide 100 pts: Camera___ Battery___ Price___ Brand___" for smartphone |
| **Reliability** | Consistency — same scale gives same result when repeated | Customer rates Zomato 4/5 today and 4/5 after 2 weeks → scale is reliable |
| **Cronbach's Alpha** | Measures internal consistency of scale (α ≥ 0.7 acceptable) | 10-question "brand loyalty" scale for Tata Motors has α = 0.83 — good |
| **Validity** | Scale measures WHAT it is supposed to measure | A "customer satisfaction" scale should NOT accidentally measure "brand awareness" |
| **SUGGING** | Selling Under Guise of research | Insurance agent calls saying "We're doing a survey" but pitches ULIP policies |

## 📗 LESSON 3: DATA COLLECTION METHODS

| Topic | Definition | Real-Life Example |
|-------|-----------|------------------|
| **Primary Data** | Freshly collected specifically for the current research problem | Tata Salt does 500 home visits to understand how consumers use salt in cooking |
| **Secondary Data** | Already existing data collected by someone else for another purpose | Parle uses Census of India data to estimate school-going children (target for Parle-G) |
| **Internal Secondary Data** | Data available within the company itself | Amazon India uses its own CRM purchase data before launching "Prime Day" |
| **Syndicated Data** | Commercial research data sold by agencies | BARC sells TV viewership data to Star, Zee, Sony to plan programming |
| **Survey Research** | Structured questionnaire administered to a sample | Airtel conducts annual customer satisfaction survey via SMS to all subscribers |
| **Observation Research** | Watching behaviour without questioning | DMart observes which shelf position gets most customer attention using heat maps |
| **Panel Research** | Same group studied repeatedly over time | Nielsen tracks 5,000 Indian households' FMCG purchases every month for 12 months |
| **Focus Group Discussion** | 6-12 people in moderated group discussion | HUL conducts FGD with 8 homemakers before launching new floor cleaner |
| **Depth Interview** | One-on-one, extended conversation to deeply explore individual views | HDFC Bank interviews 20 HNI customers for 1.5 hours to understand private banking needs |
| **Word Association** | Say first word that comes to mind | Researcher says "PAYTM" → Customer says "wallet/recharge/cashback" |
| **Ethnography** | Researcher immerses in consumer's natural environment to observe real behaviour | P&G researchers lived with rural Indian families to study washing habits → Created Tide Naturals |
| **Netnography** | Online ethnography — studying communities, reviews, forums | Boat earphones studies Reddit and YouTube comments to understand user love/hate |
| **TREAT (Secondary Data Evaluation)** | Timely, Relevant, Error-free, Adequate, Transparent | Before using 2015 census data, researcher checks: Is it still current? Does it cover my population? |

## 📗 LESSON 4: QUALITATIVE RESEARCH TOOLS

| Topic | Definition | Real-Life Example |
|-------|-----------|------------------|
| **Qualitative Research** | Unstructured, exploratory research using non-numerical data | Cadbury studies EMOTIONAL reasons people gift chocolates — not just what they gift |
| **Theoretical Saturation** | Stop collecting when new sessions add NO new themes | After 6 FGDs for Byju's brand perception, same themes keep repeating → stop |
| **Laddering Technique** | Repeatedly asking "Why?" to uncover deep motivations | "Why prefer Tata Tea Gold?" → "Tastes stronger" → "Feel more awake" → "Feel productive and in control" |
| **Brand Personification** | "If this brand were a person, describe them" | "If Royal Enfield were a person?" → "35-yr-old adventurous man, rugged, travels mountains" |
| **TAT** | Show ambiguous picture, ask for story revealing hidden attitudes | Picture of woman choosing between 2 soaps → story reveals price sensitivity and decision process |
| **Discussion Guide** | Flexible topic list for FGD — NOT a rigid questionnaire | Moderator for Dabur Honey FGD has 5 flexible topics to cover in 2 hours |
| **Grounded Theory** | Building new theory directly from qualitative data | After studying 50 gig workers, researcher builds new theory on how platform trust develops |

## 📗 LESSON 5: SAMPLE DESIGN

| Topic | Definition | Real-Life Example |
|-------|-----------|------------------|
| **Population** | Entire group of interest | For Colgate survey: "All Indians aged 6+ who brush teeth" |
| **Sampling Frame** | Actual list from which sample is drawn | Colgate uses its CRM database of 5 lakh registered users |
| **Simple Random Sampling** | Every unit has equal probability — lottery method | Flipkart uses random number generator to select 1,000 from 10 lakh customers |
| **Systematic Sampling** | Select every Nth element after random start | Factory tests every 10th biscuit packet off production line (k=10) |
| **Stratified Sampling** | Divide into strata, randomly sample from each | Vodafone divides by plan type (Prepaid/Postpaid/Business), 200 from each |
| **Cluster Sampling** | Randomly select clusters, study all within them | To survey Mumbai residents, randomly select 20 wards, survey all households in those wards |
| **Convenience Sampling** | Select whoever is easily available | MBA student surveys classmates about smartphone usage |
| **Quota Sampling** | Fix quotas for sub-groups, fill non-randomly | Fix: 100 Male + 100 Female; interviewer approaches anyone fitting quota in a mall |
| **Snowball Sampling** | Initial respondents refer next ones | Researcher studying crypto investors starts with 3 known investors — each refers 2 more |
| **Panel Sampling** | Pre-recruited fixed group studied repeatedly | Nielsen HomeScan: 5,000 households scan all grocery purchases monthly for 1 year |

## 📗 LESSON 6: SAMPLE SIZE & DATA PREPARATION

| Topic | Definition | Real-Life Example |
|-------|-----------|------------------|
| **Margin of Error (e)** | Acceptable error range in estimate — typically ±5% | Zomato accepts ±5% error in estimating % of users who would pay for Zomato Pro |
| **Confidence Level** | Probability that true value falls within estimated range (usually 95%) | 95% means: repeat survey 100 times → 95 times result is within ±5% of truth |
| **Magic Number 384/385** | n for 95% confidence, 5% error, p=0.5 — most common standard | National consumer surveys typically aim for n≥385 for any single proportion estimate |
| **Pre-coding** | Codes assigned BEFORE data collection (on questionnaire) | Questionnaire printed: Gender: Male[1] Female[2] Other[3] |
| **Post-coding** | Codes assigned AFTER collecting open-ended responses | After "Why use Swiggy?" responses collected, researcher creates: 1=Speed, 2=Offers, 3=Variety |
| **Codebook** | Document listing all variables, codes and meanings | Variable Q7=Purchase frequency; Code 1=Daily, 2=Weekly, 3=Monthly |
| **Editing** | Checking questionnaires for completeness before coding | Field supervisor rejects 12 of 500 HUL questionnaires with >30% blanks |
| **Data Cleaning** | Detecting/correcting errors after data entry | SPSS shows Age=225 → flagged as error → corrected to 25 |
| **Cross-Tabulation** | Joint distribution of two categorical variables | Amazon: Cross-tabs Purchase Frequency vs Age Group → 25-34 yr olds buy most frequently |
| **Sampling Error** | Difference between sample result and true population value | Survey shows 60% prefer Lays Classic — true may be 58% — 2% is sampling error |
| **Non-Sampling Error** | Errors from questionnaire, interviewer, or respondent behaviour | Interviewer marks "Agree" when respondent said "Somewhat Agree" — recording error |
| **Social Desirability Bias** | Answering in a socially acceptable way rather than truthfully | Respondents say they "always recycle" in sustainability survey — actually rarely do |

## 📗 LESSON 7: PARAMETRIC HYPOTHESIS TESTING & ANOVA

| Topic | Definition | Real-Life Example |
|-------|-----------|------------------|
| **Null Hypothesis (H₀)** | Statement of no effect/difference — assumed true until proven otherwise | H₀: Zomato average delivery time = 30 minutes (no change from claimed) |
| **Alternative Hypothesis (H₁)** | What researcher wants to prove — opposite of H₀ | H₁: Delivery time ≠ 30 minutes |
| **Type I Error (α)** | Rejecting a TRUE H₀ — false alarm | Concluding Maggi has harmful lead when it actually doesn't → false ban |
| **Type II Error (β)** | Failing to reject a FALSE H₀ | Concluding Maggi is safe when it actually HAS harmful levels → missed detection |
| **z-Test** | Test for mean when population σ is KNOWN and n≥30 | P&G knows σ of shampoo bottle volume = 5ml; tests if new machine fills accurately |
| **t-Test** | Test for mean when population σ is UNKNOWN — uses sample SD (s) | CCD tests if new barista training improved coffee preparation speed (σ unknown) |
| **Paired t-Test** | Compares SAME group before and after intervention | Byju's measures student scores before and after 30 days of app usage — same 50 students |
| **Independent t-Test** | Compares TWO different independent groups | Does Swiggy's avg monthly order value differ between Delhi (n=50) and Chennai (n=50) users? |
| **One-Way ANOVA** | Tests if THREE+ group means differ significantly | Does brand recall differ across 3 ad types: Emotional vs Humorous vs Informational? |
| **F-Ratio** | MSB/MSW — ratio of between-group to within-group variation | F=12.5 > F_critical=3.89 → 3 ad types significantly differ in recall scores |
| **Post-Hoc Tests (Tukey's)** | After ANOVA, identifies WHICH specific groups differ | Emotional ad > Humorous ad = Informational ad (Tukey's HSD reveals this) |
| **Interaction Effect** | In Two-Way ANOVA — effect of Factor A depends on level of Factor B | Emotional ad works better for women; Humorous ad works better for men → Gender × Ad type interaction |
| **p-value** | Probability of observed result if H₀ were true; p < α → Reject H₀ | Flipkart satisfaction study: p=0.03 < α=0.05 → significant difference found → reject H₀ |

## 📗 LESSON 8: NON-PARAMETRIC TESTS

| Topic | Definition | Real-Life Example |
|-------|-----------|------------------|
| **Non-Parametric Tests** | Tests with no distributional assumption — for ordinal/nominal or small samples | Testing if customer satisfaction RANKING differs between metro and non-metro users |
| **Chi-Square Independence** | Tests if two categorical variables are related | Is gender related to preferred OTT platform? (Male/Female vs Netflix/Prime/Hotstar) |
| **Chi-Square Goodness of Fit** | Tests if observed distribution matches expected | Are 4 cola brands equally preferred? Expected=25% each; if not → χ² is significant |
| **Expected Frequency (E)** | Frequency expected in each cell if there is NO relationship | E = (Row Total × Column Total) / Grand Total |
| **Sign Test** | Simplest paired test — uses only direction (+/-) of change | Did store renovation improve experience? Count +ve vs -ve changes in 15 customers' ratings |
| **Mann-Whitney U** | Compares two INDEPENDENT groups using ranks | Do urban and rural consumers differ in rank-ordering of soap brands? |
| **Wilcoxon Signed-Rank** | Compares two RELATED groups using direction AND magnitude of ranks | Same 20 shoppers rank store experience before and after renovation — uses size of change too |
| **Kruskal-Wallis** | Compares 3+ independent groups using ranks — non-parametric ANOVA | Do satisfaction rank-scores differ across IndiGo, Air India, and Vistara passengers? |

## 📗 LESSON 9: REPORT WRITING

| Topic | Definition | Real-Life Example |
|-------|-----------|------------------|
| **Research Report** | Formal written document presenting MR findings, conclusions & recommendations | Nielsen submits 120-page annual report to HUL on FMCG category trends |
| **Executive Summary** | 1-3 page condensed version — written last, placed first, stands alone | Ipsos report to startup CEO opens with 2 pages: "Brand awareness = 34%; recommend social-first campaign" |
| **Technical Report** | Long, detailed report with full methodology — for researcher/academic audience | Doctoral research on "e-commerce trust in India" — 200 pages with full SPSS appendices |
| **Popular Report** | Short, non-technical report in simple language — for management | A 10-slide PPT deck Ipsos submits to a startup CEO after brand audit |
| **Letter of Transmittal** | Formal covering letter submitting the report | "Dear Mr. Kumar, We are pleased to submit the attached Q2 FY25 customer satisfaction report..." |
| **Findings vs Conclusions** | Findings = WHAT the data shows; Conclusions = WHAT IT MEANS | Finding: "68% rated delivery speed 4/5." Conclusion: "Speed is the #1 satisfaction driver." |
| **Recommendations** | Specific, SMART, actionable suggestions for management | "Launch express delivery (<20 min) in top 5 metros by Q3 2025, targeting 15% NPS improvement" |
| **Objectivity** | Presenting findings truthfully — even if negative for client | Research shows new Pepsi variant disliked by 70% — researcher must report this honestly |

## 📗 LESSON 10: APPLICATIONS & ETHICS

| Topic | Definition | Real-Life Example |
|-------|-----------|------------------|
| **Product Research** | MR used to develop, test, and refine products | ITC tests 8 Bingo chip variants before deciding final flavours for national launch |
| **Concept Testing** | Testing a new product idea with consumers before development | Jio showed "JioPhone" concept to rural users before manufacturing — confirmed demand |
| **Test Marketing** | Launching in ONE city before national rollout | Nestle tested Maggi Masala Noodles in UP before going national |
| **Copy Testing** | Testing ads BEFORE airing to check communication | Fevicol tests humorous ad concepts with focus groups before spending crores on media |
| **Mystery Shopping** | Researcher poses as customer to evaluate service quality | Researcher visits 20 Tanishq showrooms as regular customer to evaluate staff behaviour |
| **NPS** | "On 0-10 scale, how likely to recommend us?" — measures loyalty | Zepto asks after every delivery; NPS = % Promoters (9-10) − % Detractors (0-6) |
| **ABC Attitude Model** | Affective (feelings) + Behavioural (action) + Cognitive (beliefs) | Royal Enfield: A=Love the sound; B=Plan to buy next yr; C=Believe it's best premium bike |
| **India Challenge — Diversity** | 1.4B people with different languages, cultures, income, literacy | Patanjali survey must work for educated Delhi consumer AND Hindi-only rural UP buyer |
| **India Challenge — Literacy** | Large rural population can't complete written questionnaires | IFFCO uses picture-based surveys with farmers in Bihar — images instead of text |
| **Informed Consent** | Respondents must know about the study and voluntarily agree | Before FGD for Asian Paints: "This is research only, name is confidential, you can leave anytime" |
| **Confidentiality** | Respondent/client data not shared with unauthorized parties | Research agency must NOT sell ITC's consumer data to HUL — competitor firms |
| **SUGGING** | Selling Under Guise of research — major ethical violation | Telecom agent calls saying "We're doing a survey" but actually selling a data pack upgrade |
| **FRUGGING** | Fundraising Under Guise of research — major ethical violation | NGO calls saying "We're doing research on social issues" but actually asking for donations |
| **MRSI** | Market Research Society of India — sets ethical standards for MR in India | MRSI code prohibits SUGGING, mandates informed consent, requires data protection |

## 📌 CHAPTER 1: Introduction to Marketing Research

### 1.1 What is Marketing Research?

**Definition (AMA — American Marketing Association):**
Marketing Research is the function that links the consumer, customer, and public to the marketer through information — information used to identify and define marketing opportunities and problems; generate, refine, and evaluate marketing actions; monitor marketing performance; and improve understanding of marketing as a process.

**Simpler Definition:** Marketing Research is the systematic process of collecting, analyzing, and interpreting data to help managers make better marketing decisions.

---

### 1.2 Marketing Research vs Market Research

| Aspect | Marketing Research | Market Research |
|--------|------------------|----------------|
| Scope | Broad — all aspects of marketing | Narrow — specific market/industry |
| Focus | Any marketing problem | Market size, share, trends |
| Example | Why do customers churn? | What is India's ice cream market size? |

---

### 1.3 The Marketing Research Process (6 Steps)

```
Step 1: DEFINE THE PROBLEM & RESEARCH OBJECTIVES
           ↓
Step 2: DEVELOP THE RESEARCH PLAN (Design)
           ↓
Step 3: COLLECT DATA (Primary/Secondary)
           ↓
Step 4: ANALYZE DATA (Statistical)
           ↓
Step 5: INTERPRET & PRESENT FINDINGS
           ↓
Step 6: MAKE MARKETING DECISIONS
```

**Most Critical Step:** Problem Definition. A well-defined problem is half-solved.
"A problem well-stated is a problem half-solved." — Charles Kettering

---

### 1.4 Types of Marketing Research

**By Purpose:**

| Type | Purpose | When Used | Example |
|------|---------|-----------|---------|
| **Exploratory** | Explore; gain insight; generate hypotheses | When problem is vague/unclear | Focus groups on why app usage dropped |
| **Descriptive** | Describe characteristics; answer Who/What/When/Where | When you know what to measure | Survey: How many Indians use OTT? |
| **Causal/Experimental** | Determine cause-effect relationships | When you know what causes what | Does price cut increase sales? |

---

### 1.5 Research Design

**Definition:**
Research Design is the blueprint or framework for conducting the marketing research project. It details the procedures for obtaining the required information.

**Three Types:**
1. **Exploratory Design:** Secondary data, focus groups, depth interviews
2. **Descriptive Design:** Surveys (cross-sectional or longitudinal)
3. **Causal Design:** Experiments (laboratory or field)

---

### 1.6 Case Study — Hindustan Unilever (HUL): Marketing Research in Action

**Problem:** HUL's Sunsilk shampoo was losing market share to Pantene (P&G) despite similar pricing.

**Research Process:**
1. **Problem defined:** Why are consumers switching from Sunsilk to Pantene?
2. **Exploratory research:** Focus groups → Found consumers perceived Pantene as more "salon-grade"
3. **Descriptive research:** National survey of 2,000 women → Confirmed perception gap
4. **Causal research:** Experiment — Split test of new "salon-expert" campaign vs. old campaign

**Finding:** Pantene's white/gold packaging and "pro-vitamin" claim created a premiumness perception. Sunsilk was seen as a budget brand.

**Action:** HUL repositioned Sunsilk as a youth/fashion brand (different from Pantene's professional positioning) with celebrity associations (Sonam Kapoor) and vibrant packaging.

**Result:** Sunsilk market share recovered and grew. Research directly drove product repositioning strategy.

## 📌 CHAPTER 2: Scaling Techniques

### 2.1 What is Measurement and Scaling?

**Measurement:** Assigning numbers to objects/characteristics according to certain rules.

**Scaling:** Creating a continuum upon which measured objects are located.

---

### 2.2 Levels of Measurement (Stevens' Classification)

| Scale | Properties | Operations | Example |
|-------|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **Nominal** | Labels only; no order | Count (frequencies) | Gender (M/F), Product categories |
| **Ordinal** | Order/rank; no equal intervals | Rank, median | Customer satisfaction rank (1st, 2nd, 3rd) |
| **Interval** | Equal intervals; no true zero | Mean, SD, t-test | Temperature (°C), Likert scale |
| **Ratio** | Equal intervals + absolute zero | All statistics, ratios | Sales (₹), Weight (kg), Age |

**Memory trick:** "NOIR" — Nominal, Ordinal, Interval, Ratio (from weakest to strongest)

---

### 2.3 Scaling Techniques

#### A. Comparative Scales

**Paired Comparison:** Present two objects at a time; respondent chooses preferred one.
- "Do you prefer Pepsi or Coca-Cola?"
- Good for small number of objects (n objects need n(n-1)/2 pairs)

**Rank Order Scale:** Rank multiple objects simultaneously.
- "Rank the following brands 1-5 in order of preference."
- Fast, easy; ordinal data only

**Constant Sum Scale:** Allocate a fixed number of points among objects.
- "Distribute 100 points among these 4 features based on importance."
- Provides ratio-level data on relative importance

**Q-Sort:** Sort large number of statements into piles (most agree → most disagree)

---

#### B. Non-Comparative Scales

**1. Likert Scale (Most Common):**
- 5 or 7-point agreement scale
- "I am satisfied with Zomato's service: 1=Strongly Disagree … 5=Strongly Agree"
- Summated rating scale → Add up all item scores
- Interval-level data; can calculate mean and SD

**2. Semantic Differential Scale:**
- Bipolar adjectives on a 5 or 7-point scale
- "Maruti Suzuki service: Unreliable ___:___:___:___:___:___ Reliable"
- Good for measuring brand image

**3. Stapel Scale:**
- Single adjective; -5 to +5 scale (no neutral midpoint)
- "Evaluate Flipkart on FAST DELIVERY: -5 -4 -3 -2 -1 +1 +2 +3 +4 +5"

**4. Continuous Rating Scale (Graphic Scale):**
- Mark position on a continuous line
- Most sensitive but difficult to administer

---

### 2.4 Scale Evaluation

**Reliability:** Consistency of measurement over time and across raters.
- **Test-Retest Reliability:** Same respondents answer same questions at two different times
- **Cronbach's Alpha:** Measures internal consistency (α > 0.70 = acceptable)

**Validity:** Does the scale measure what it's supposed to measure?
- **Content Validity:** Do items represent the full domain? (Expert review)
- **Construct Validity:** Does scale relate correctly to other constructs?
- **Criterion Validity (Predictive):** Does scale predict a related outcome?

**Convergent vs Discriminant Validity:**
- Convergent: Scale correlated with similar scales (should be high)
- Discriminant: Scale NOT correlated with different constructs (should be low)

## 📌 CHAPTER 3: Data Collection Methods

### 3.1 Types of Data

**Primary Data:** Collected specifically for the current research project.
- More relevant but expensive, time-consuming

**Secondary Data:** Already collected for other purposes; researcher reuses it.
- Less expensive, faster but may not perfectly fit needs

**Secondary Data Sources in India:**
- **Government:** Census India, NSSO surveys, RBI reports, MCA21
- **Industry:** CII, FICCI, NASSCOM, SEBI annual reports
- **Syndicated:** Nielsen, Kantar, IMRB, Euromonitor
- **Academic:** JSTOR, SSRN, EBSCO

---

### 3.2 Survey Methods

| Method | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|--------|-----------|--------------|
| **Personal Interview** | High response, rich data, probing possible | Expensive, interviewer bias, slow |
| **Telephone Survey** | Fast, lower cost | Lower cooperation, limited questions |
| **Mail Survey** | Wide reach, no interviewer bias, anonymous | Very low response rate, slow |
| **Online Survey** | Fastest, cheapest, large scale | Digital divide; self-selection bias |
| **CAPI/CATI** | Automated; consistent administration | Requires technology |

---

### 3.3 Observation Methods

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Personal Observation** | Researcher observes behavior | Watching shoppers in a supermarket |
| **Mechanical/Electronic** | Eye tracking, cameras, traffic counters | Neuro-marketing; store cameras |
| **Ethnography** | Long-term immersion in natural setting | Living with rural consumers |
| **Content Analysis** | Systematic analysis of text/media | Analyzing social media mentions |
| **Trace Analysis** | Examining physical evidence | Library book wear pattern |

---

### 3.4 Experimental Research

**Definition:**
Experiment is the only research method that can establish CAUSE-EFFECT relationships.

**Key Concepts:**
- **Independent Variable (IV):** Manipulated by researcher (e.g., price, packaging)
- **Dependent Variable (DV):** Measured outcome (e.g., sales, satisfaction)
- **Control Group:** Does not receive treatment; comparison baseline
- **Experimental Group:** Receives the treatment/manipulation
- **Extraneous Variables:** Other factors that might affect DV; must be controlled

**Pre-Experimental Designs:**
- One-Shot Case Study: No control; O after X → Weak
- One Group Pre-Post: Measure before and after treatment; no control group

**True Experimental Designs:**
- **Randomized Control Trial (RCT):** Random assignment to groups → Gold standard
- **Completely Randomized Design**
- **Latin Square Design**

**Quasi-Experimental:**
- Non-equivalent control groups
- Time series designs

**India Example — A/B Testing (Digital Experiment):**
Flipkart tests two homepage designs (A = current; B = new) with random user split. Measures which drives higher conversion rate. This is an online RCT — widely used in digital marketing.

## 📌 CHAPTER 4: Qualitative Research Tools

### 4.1 What is Qualitative Research?

**Definition:**
Qualitative Research explores attitudes, behaviors, and experiences through non-numerical data. It seeks to understand the "why" and "how" behind consumer behavior.

**Qualitative vs Quantitative:**

| Aspect | Qualitative | Quantitative |
|--------|------------|--------------|
| Goal | Explore, understand | Measure, test |
| Data | Words, images, themes | Numbers, statistics |
| Sample | Small, purposive | Large, random |
| Methods | FGDs, IDIs, ethnography | Surveys, experiments |
| Output | Insights, hypotheses | Generalizable findings |
| Analysis | Thematic, interpretive | Statistical |

---

### 4.2 Focus Group Discussion (FGD)

**Definition:**
A Focus Group Discussion is a moderated discussion with 6-12 participants who share relevant characteristics, designed to generate qualitative insights on a topic.

**Structure:**
- **Moderator:** Trained facilitator who guides discussion
- **Screener:** Criteria to select right participants
- **Discussion Guide:** List of topics/questions (not rigid survey)
- **Recording:** Audio/video with consent
- **Observation Room:** One-way mirror for client team

**Advantages:**
- Group synergy → Ideas build on each other
- Natural conversation → More authentic responses
- Rich, nuanced data
- Quick to organize (2 hours per group)

**Disadvantages:**
- Groupthink — dominant voices suppress others
- Not generalizable (small sample)
- Moderator bias
- Cannot establish causality

**India Application:** Before launching Maggi Oats Noodles, Nestlé conducted FGDs with urban working women to understand breakfast behavior. Insights: Women wanted "healthy" but also "quick." Led to "Maggi Oats" positioning.

---

### 4.3 In-Depth Interview (IDI)

**Definition:**
An unstructured or semi-structured one-on-one interview conducted to explore a person's motivations, attitudes, and experiences in depth.

**Types:**
- **Structured:** Fixed questions; less probing
- **Semi-structured:** Guide questions; flexible follow-up
- **Unstructured:** Open-ended; conversation-style

**Projective Techniques (Used in IDIs):**

| Technique | Description | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Word Association** | "What word comes to mind when I say 'Reliance'?" | Powerful, India, Mukesh |
| **Sentence Completion** | "People who drink Bisleri are ___" | Rich, health-conscious |
| **Picture Interpretation (TAT)** | Show picture; tell story about it | Reveal hidden motivations |
| **Cartoon Completion** | Complete a cartoon dialogue | Non-threatening, indirect |
| **Role Playing** | Act as someone else in a scenario | Reveal true attitudes |

---

### 4.4 Ethnographic Research

**Definition:**
Ethnography involves the researcher immersing themselves in the consumer's natural environment over an extended period to understand behavior in context.

**Example:** Unilever sent researchers to rural India to LIVE with village families and observe how they washed clothes. This led to the development of Surf Excel (concentrated detergent for bucket washing), specifically designed for how rural Indians actually wash clothes (not machines).

## 📌 CHAPTER 5: Sample Design

### 5.1 Key Sampling Concepts

**Population (Universe):** The complete group of elements (people, firms) about which researcher wants information.

**Sample:** A subset of the population actually studied.

**Sampling Frame:** The list or source from which sample is drawn (e.g., voter list, phone directory, customer database).

**Sampling Unit:** The element being sampled (individual, household, firm).

**Sampling Error:** Error due to studying a sample rather than the whole population. Reduced by increasing sample size.

**Non-Sampling Error:** Errors NOT related to sample size — measurement error, interviewer bias, data entry errors. Cannot be reduced by larger sample.

---

### 5.2 Probability Sampling Methods

Every element has a known, non-zero chance of selection → Results can be generalized.

| Method | Process | Best For |
|--------|---------|---------|
| **Simple Random Sampling (SRS)** | Random selection from complete list | Homogeneous population |
| **Systematic Sampling** | Select every kth element (k = N/n) | Ordered lists |
| **Stratified Sampling** | Divide into strata; random sample from each | Heterogeneous population with known strata |
| **Cluster Sampling** | Divide into clusters; randomly select clusters; study all in cluster | Geographically dispersed population |
| **Multi-Stage Sampling** | Multiple stages of sampling | Large national surveys |

**Stratified vs Cluster:**
- Stratified: Strata are DIFFERENT from each other; elements within stratum are SIMILAR
- Cluster: Clusters are SIMILAR to each other; elements within cluster are DIFFERENT

---

### 5.3 Non-Probability Sampling Methods

Selection based on judgment/convenience; cannot calculate sampling error.

| Method | Process | When Used |
|--------|---------|-----------|
| **Convenience Sampling** | Select whoever is available | Exploratory research |
| **Judgement/Purposive Sampling** | Researcher's judgement selects "best" respondents | Expert opinions |
| **Quota Sampling** | Fill predetermined quotas (e.g., 50% male, 50% female) | Market research |
| **Snowball Sampling** | Each respondent refers next | Hard-to-reach populations (drug users, HNIs) |

## 📌 CHAPTER 6: Sample Size Determination & Data Preparation

### 6.1 Sample Size Determination

**For estimating a population mean:**
```
n = (Z × σ / E)²
Where: Z = Z-value for confidence level, σ = population SD, E = acceptable error
```

**For estimating a population proportion:**
```
n = Z² × p(1-p) / E²
Where: p = estimated proportion, E = acceptable error
```

**Common Z-values:**
- 90% confidence: Z = 1.645
- 95% confidence: Z = 1.96
- 99% confidence: Z = 2.576

**Worked Example:**
Estimate proportion of Delhi consumers who prefer organic food. Acceptable error = 5%; Confidence = 95%; p unknown (use p = 0.5 for maximum sample size).
```
n = (1.96)² × 0.5 × 0.5 / (0.05)²
  = 3.8416 × 0.25 / 0.0025
  = 0.9604 / 0.0025
  = 384 respondents
```

---

### 6.2 Data Preparation Steps

```
Step 1: QUESTIONNAIRE CHECKING → Check for completeness, legibility
Step 2: EDITING → Identify and correct errors; handle missing data
Step 3: CODING → Assign numerical codes to responses
Step 4: TRANSCRIPTION/DATA ENTRY → Enter data into software (SPSS, Excel)
Step 5: DATA CLEANING → Check for outliers, inconsistencies, duplicates
Step 6: ANALYSIS READY DATASET
```

**Handling Missing Data:**
- **Deletion:** Remove incomplete cases (list-wise or pair-wise)
- **Imputation:** Replace with mean, median, or predicted value
- **Multiple Imputation:** Statistical method for serious missing data

**Coding Example:**
Gender: Male = 1, Female = 2, Other = 3
Satisfaction: Strongly Disagree = 1 … Strongly Agree = 5

## 📌 CHAPTER 7: Parametric Hypothesis Testing and ANOVA

### 7.1 Hypothesis Testing Framework

**Null Hypothesis (H₀):** Status quo; no difference or effect exists.
**Alternative Hypothesis (H₁):** Researcher's claim; difference or effect exists.

**Steps in Hypothesis Testing:**
1. State H₀ and H₁
2. Choose significance level (α) — typically 0.05 or 5%
3. Select appropriate test
4. Calculate test statistic
5. Find p-value or critical value
6. Make decision: Reject H₀ if p < α

**Type I Error (α):** Rejecting H₀ when it's actually true (False Positive)
**Type II Error (β):** Failing to reject H₀ when it's actually false (False Negative)

---

### 7.2 Parametric Tests

**Assumptions of Parametric Tests:**
- Data is interval or ratio scale
- Normal distribution of data
- Homogeneity of variance

**1. z-test:**
- Used when: n > 30 AND population variance known
- Tests: Population mean against a value; OR two population means
```
z = (X̄ - μ) / (σ/√n)
```

**2. t-test:**
- Used when: n < 30 OR population variance unknown

**Three Types of t-test:**
| Type | Purpose | Formula |
|------|---------|---------|
| **One-sample t-test** | Compare sample mean to known value | t = (X̄ - μ) / (s/√n) |
| **Independent samples t-test** | Compare means of two groups | t = (X̄₁ - X̄₂) / SE_diff |
| **Paired samples t-test** | Compare means of same group at two times | t = d̄ / (s_d/√n) |

**Degrees of freedom:** df = n - 1 (one sample); df = n₁ + n₂ - 2 (two sample)

---

### 7.3 ANOVA (Analysis of Variance)

**Definition:**
ANOVA tests whether there are statistically significant differences among the means of THREE OR MORE groups. It is an extension of the t-test.

**When to use:** Compare means of 3+ independent groups.
Example: Do customer satisfaction scores differ across three store formats (hypermarket, supermarket, convenience store)?

**One-Way ANOVA:**
- One independent variable (factor) with 3+ levels
- H₀: All group means are equal (μ₁ = μ₂ = μ₃)
- H₁: At least one group mean differs

**ANOVA Table:**

| Source | SS | df | MS = SS/df | F |
|--------|----|----|-----------|---|
| Between groups | SSB | k-1 | MSB | MSB/MSW |
| Within groups | SSW | N-k | MSW | |
| Total | SST | N-1 | | |

Where: k = number of groups, N = total sample size

**F-ratio:** F = MSB / MSW
- If F > F_critical (from F-table) → Reject H₀ → Groups differ significantly

**Post-Hoc Tests (When ANOVA is significant):**
ANOVA tells you groups differ but not WHICH pairs. Post-hoc tests identify specific differences:
- **Tukey's HSD:** Most common; controls Type I error
- **Bonferroni:** Conservative
- **Scheffé:** Most conservative; for complex comparisons

**Two-Way ANOVA:**
- Two independent variables; tests main effects AND interaction
- Example: Effect of BOTH price level and packaging type on purchase intention

## 📌 CHAPTER 8: Non-Parametric Tests

### 8.1 What are Non-Parametric Tests?

**Definition:**
Non-Parametric (or distribution-free) tests make NO assumptions about the population distribution. Used when:
- Data is nominal or ordinal
- Sample size is very small
- Parametric assumptions are violated

---

### 8.2 Common Non-Parametric Tests

| Parametric Equivalent | Non-Parametric Alternative | Use Case |
|----------------------|--------------------------|---------|
| One-sample t-test | **Wilcoxon Signed Rank Test** | Median vs hypothesized value |
| Independent t-test | **Mann-Whitney U Test** | Compare two independent groups |
| Paired t-test | **Wilcoxon Signed Rank Test** | Compare matched pairs |
| One-Way ANOVA | **Kruskal-Wallis Test** | Compare 3+ independent groups |
| Two-Way ANOVA | **Friedman Test** | Compare 3+ related groups |
| Pearson Correlation | **Spearman Rank Correlation** | Ordinal data relationship |

**Chi-Square Test (χ²):** Tests association between TWO CATEGORICAL (nominal) variables.
```
χ² = Σ[(Observed - Expected)² / Expected]
df = (r-1)(c-1)    where r=rows, c=columns
```

**Example:** Is there an association between gender and brand preference?
- H₀: No association between gender and brand preference
- If χ² > χ²_critical → Association exists

## 📌 CHAPTER 9: Report Writing

### 9.1 Structure of a Marketing Research Report

**Standard Format:**
```
1. Title Page
2. Executive Summary (1-2 pages — decision-makers read ONLY this)
3. Table of Contents
4. Introduction / Background
5. Problem Definition & Research Objectives
6. Research Methodology
7. Findings & Analysis (with tables, charts)
8. Conclusions & Recommendations
9. Limitations
10. Appendices (questionnaire, data tables, bibliography)
```

**Executive Summary — Most Important:**
- Written LAST but placed FIRST
- Contains problem, methodology, key findings, recommendations
- Should stand alone (busy executives may read nothing else)

### 10.2 Important Multivariate Techniques

**Factor Analysis:**
- Reduces many variables to fewer underlying factors
- Used for: Scale development, market segmentation
- Example: 20 attitude questions → 4 underlying factors (price sensitivity, quality orientation, convenience, status)

**Cluster Analysis:**
- Groups respondents into homogeneous segments based on similarity
- Used for: Market segmentation, customer profiling
- Example: Segment Indian mobile users into 5 clusters by usage behavior

**Conjoint Analysis:**
- Determines how consumers value different attributes of a product
- Used for: Product design, pricing decisions
- Example: How do consumers trade off between phone battery life, camera quality, and price?

**Discriminant Analysis:**
- Classifies respondents into groups based on predictor variables
- Example: Predict whether a customer will churn (yes/no) based on satisfaction, tenure, usage

### 10.3 Case Study — Amul Dairy: Marketing Research for Rural India

**Background:** GCMMF (Amul) wanted to understand dairy consumption patterns in tier-2 and tier-3 towns to plan expansion.

**Research Design:**
- **Problem:** Which dairy products have highest growth potential in smaller cities?
- **Exploratory:** FGDs in 3 cities with housewives → Found demand for value-added products (paneer, ghee, flavored milk) was high but availability poor
- **Descriptive:** Survey of 5,000 households across 50 cities → Measured consumption frequency, willingness to pay, brand preference
- **Findings:** 70% preferred loose paneer (price/freshness), but 40% willing to pay premium for packaged Amul paneer if consistently available

**Action:**
- Amul increased distribution reach in tier-2 cities (partnered with local distributors)
- Launched "Amul Fresh" sub-brand for paneer and dahi at slightly lower price point
- Ran awareness campaign emphasizing consistent quality and safety vs. loose market

**Result:** 35% volume growth in paneer segment in target cities over 2 years.

**Lesson:** Research reveals not just WHAT consumers want but WHERE and HOW to reach them. Distribution insight was as valuable as product insight.
